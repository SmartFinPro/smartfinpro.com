# Division 296 Tax Explained: Impact on Superannuation in 2026

<AnswerBlock
  question="What is Division 296 tax?"
  answer="Division 296 is an additional 15% tax on earnings from superannuation balances exceeding $3 million, effective from 1 July 2025. This means superannuation earnings above the threshold are taxed at 30% total (15% standard + 15% Division 296)."
/>

The introduction of Division 296 tax marks the most significant change to Australia's superannuation system in over a decade. If your super balance exceeds or is approaching $3 million, understanding how this new tax works isn't optional—it's essential for protecting your retirement wealth.

From 1 July 2025, Australians with total superannuation balances (TSB) exceeding $3 million face an additional 15% tax on the proportion of earnings attributable to the excess. While the government estimates this affects only 80,000 individuals currently, rising super balances and static thresholds mean many more Australians will be caught in coming years.

This comprehensive guide breaks down exactly how Division 296 tax works, who it affects, how it's calculated, and proven strategies to minimise your exposure.

## Understanding Division 296 Tax: The Fundamentals

### What Is Division 296 Tax?

Division 296 tax is a levy on superannuation earnings for balances exceeding $3 million. It's calculated on the proportion of earnings that relate to the amount over the threshold.

The tax operates as follows:

- **Standard super earnings tax:** 15% (unchanged)
- **Division 296 additional tax:** 15% on earnings above $3 million
- **Total effective tax rate:** 30% on earnings from the excess balance

Importantly, this is not a 30% tax on your entire super balance—only on the earnings attributable to amounts over $3 million.

### Who Does It Affect?

Division 296 tax affects individuals whose total superannuation balance (TSB) exceeds $3 million at any point during the financial year. Your TSB includes:

- Accumulation account balances
- Account-based pension balances
- Defined benefit interests
- All super accounts across multiple funds
- Both member and employer contributions

The ATO will automatically calculate your TSB using data from all super funds. You cannot avoid the tax by splitting balances across multiple funds.

### When Does It Start?

The Division 296 tax applies from the 2025-26 financial year onwards (starting 1 July 2025). The ATO will issue the first tax assessments for this in late 2026, with payment due by the standard tax deadline.

### Why Was It Introduced?

The Australian Government introduced Division 296 as part of broader budget repair measures and to address concerns about the sustainability of superannuation tax concessions. The stated objectives include:

1. Reducing the cost of super tax concessions to the budget
2. Improving equity in the super system
3. Ensuring super tax concessions are "targeted and sustainable"

Treasury estimates the measure will raise approximately $2.3 billion over four years, affecting less than 0.5% of Australians with super.

## How Division 296 Tax Is Calculated

Understanding the calculation method is critical for tax planning. The formula is more complex than it initially appears.

### The Calculation Formula

The Division 296 tax liability is calculated using this formula:

**Division 296 Tax = (Proportion of earnings × Total earnings) × 15%**

Where:
- **Proportion of earnings** = Excess balance ÷ Total superannuation balance
- **Excess balance** = TSB minus $3 million threshold
- **Total earnings** = Change in TSB + withdrawals - contributions

### Step-by-Step Calculation Example

Let's work through a practical example:

**Scenario:**
- TSB at 30 June 2025: $3.8 million
- TSB at 1 July 2024: $3.5 million
- Contributions during year: $100,000
- Withdrawals during year: $50,000

**Step 1: Calculate total earnings**
Total earnings = ($3,800,000 - $3,500,000) + $50,000 - $100,000 = $250,000

**Step 2: Calculate excess balance**
Excess balance = $3,800,000 - $3,000,000 = $800,000

**Step 3: Calculate proportion**
Proportion = $800,000 ÷ $3,800,000 = 21.05%

**Step 4: Calculate Division 296 tax**
Division 296 tax = $250,000 × 21.05% × 15% = **$7,894**

This is in addition to the standard 15% tax already paid on earnings within the super fund.

### Key Calculation Considerations

**Earnings Include Unrealised Gains**
Unlike standard super taxation, Division 296 tax applies to both realised and unrealised capital gains. If your property or share holdings inside super increase in value (even if not sold), those gains count as "earnings" for Division 296 purposes.

This is controversial because you may face a tax bill without having sold assets to generate cash to pay it.

**Negative Earnings Don't Create Credits**
If your super experiences negative earnings (losses) in a financial year, you cannot carry forward credits or offsets to future years. Each year is assessed independently.

**Multiple Accounts Are Aggregated**
The ATO aggregates all your super interests, including:
- Multiple APRA-regulated funds
- Self-managed super funds (SMSFs)
- Defined benefit schemes
- Foreign super (in some cases)

### TSB Measurement Timing

Your TSB is measured at two points:
1. **30 June** of the relevant financial year
2. **Just before each super event** during the year (contributions, withdrawals, rollovers)

The ATO uses the higher of these measurements to determine if you exceed the $3 million threshold.

## Who Needs to Pay: Detailed Scenarios

### Scenario 1: SMSF with Property Holdings

**Profile:** John, 58, SMSF balance $3.4 million (includes commercial property valued at $2.1 million)

**Situation:**
- Property revalued from $2.0m to $2.2m during FY26 (+$200,000 unrealised gain)
- Share portfolio: +$80,000 realised gains, +$50,000 unrealised gains
- Concessional contributions: $30,000
- Pension withdrawals: $120,000

**Division 296 Impact:**
Total earnings = $200,000 + $80,000 + $50,000 + $30,000 - $120,000 = $240,000

Excess balance = $3,400,000 - $3,000,000 = $400,000
Proportion = 400k ÷ 3,400k = 11.76%

Division 296 tax = $240,000 × 11.76% × 15% = **$4,235**

**Key Issue:** John faces a tax bill despite not selling the property. He may need to withdraw additional funds from super to pay the ATO.

### Scenario 2: Multiple Accumulation Funds

**Profile:** Maria, 52, multiple super accounts totalling $3.1 million

**Accounts:**
- Industry fund A: $1.8 million
- Industry fund B: $900,000
- Old employer fund: $400,000

**Situation:**
Maria doesn't realise Division 296 applies because no single fund exceeds $3 million. However, the ATO aggregates all accounts.

**Division 296 Impact:**
Even though balances are split, the combined TSB triggers Division 296 tax. Maria should consider consolidating funds for better tax management and lower fees.

### Scenario 3: Defined Benefit Schemes

**Profile:** David, 61, public servant with defined benefit pension plus SMSF

**Balances:**
- Defined benefit scheme: $2.3 million (calculated value)
- SMSF: $850,000
- Total TSB: $3.15 million

**Situation:**
Defined benefit interests are valued using the ATO's special formula (16 × annual pension entitlement). This inflates the TSB compared to accumulation balances.

**Division 296 Impact:**
David exceeds the threshold by $150,000. The calculation becomes complex because defined benefit earnings are determined differently than accumulation fund earnings.

### Scenario 4: Just Under the Threshold

**Profile:** Linda, 64, TSB of $2.85 million

**Situation:**
Linda is currently under the threshold but concerned about:
- Market growth pushing her over in future years
- No indexation of the $3 million threshold
- Employer contributions continuing to be made

**Strategy:**
Linda should model scenarios and consider strategies like partial withdrawals or contribution management to stay under the threshold if possible.

## Strategies to Minimise Division 296 Tax

### Strategy 1: Withdraw Excess to Stay Below Threshold

**How It Works:**
If you're just over the $3 million threshold, withdrawing enough to bring your TSB below $3 million eliminates the tax entirely.

**Calculation:**
If your TSB is $3.2 million, withdrawing $200,000+ brings you below the threshold.

**Considerations:**
- Only available if you've met a condition of release (typically age 60+)
- Withdrawn amounts taxed in personal name (but potentially tax-free if over 60)
- Can re-contribute up to caps if beneficial
- Loses the tax-advantaged super environment permanently

**Best For:** Individuals slightly over threshold who don't need all funds in super for estate planning.

### Strategy 2: Contribute to Spouse's Super

**How It Works:**
Shift future contributions to a spouse's super account to slow the growth of your own balance.

**Requirements:**
- Spouse must be under age 75
- Subject to spouse's contribution caps
- Can use contribution splitting for concessional contributions

**Tax Benefits:**
- Keeps family wealth in tax-advantaged super
- Both members benefit from super tax concessions
- May qualify for spouse contribution tax offset (if spouse earns &lt;$40,000)

**Limitations:**
- Doesn't reduce existing balances, only controls future growth
- Takes years to have significant impact
- Both spouses may eventually exceed threshold

**Best For:** Couples where one member is approaching threshold and other has lower balance.

### Strategy 3: Asset Allocation Adjustments

**How It Works:**
Shift to investments that produce lower taxable earnings or switch between super and personal holdings.

**Tactics:**

**Inside Super (for amounts under $3m):**
- Growth assets (shares, property) that benefit from 15% tax on earnings
- Franked dividend strategies (effective tax rate &lt;15%)

**Outside Super (for amounts over $3m):**
- Tax-efficient assets like index ETFs (low turnover)
- Negatively geared property
- Assets with CGT discount (50% in personal name if held >12 months)

**Considerations:**
- CGT implications of moving assets out of super
- Restructuring costs vs Division 296 savings
- Estate planning implications

**Best For:** Sophisticated investors with balances well over $3 million who can afford professional advice.

### Strategy 4: Insurance Premium Deductions

**How It Works:**
Life and TPD insurance held in super reduces your TSB and earnings calculations.

**Mechanics:**
- Premiums paid from super reduce account balance
- Reduces both TSB and calculated earnings
- Death benefit can still go to beneficiaries

**Limitations:**
- Insurance needs must be genuine
- Premiums can be expensive for older members
- May not justify cost purely for Division 296 reduction

**Best For:** Those needing insurance anyway who can structure it tax-efficiently.

### Strategy 5: Pension Phase Strategies

**How It Works:**
Moving to pension phase doesn't avoid Division 296 but allows strategic withdrawals.

**Tactics:**
- Minimum pension withdrawals are mandatory (but count as withdrawals in calculation)
- Can make larger discretionary withdrawals to reduce TSB
- Recontribute within caps if beneficial for tax planning

**Considerations:**
- Pension earnings still count toward Division 296 calculation
- Transfer balance cap (currently $1.9 million) limits tax-free pension amounts
- Excess pension balance remains in accumulation (taxed at 15%)

**Best For:** Members already in pension phase who can manage withdrawal strategies.

### Strategy 6: SMSF-Specific Tactics

**For SMSF Members:**

**Partial Commutation:**
- Wind back pension to reduce balance in tax-free pension phase
- Keeps more in accumulation where you have control

**Asset Segregation:**
- Clearly separate assets supporting pensions vs accumulation
- Ensures correct tax treatment and simplifies Division 296 calculations

**In-Specie Transfers:**
- Transfer assets out of super to personal name (if over 60 and in pension phase)
- Allows tax-free withdrawal of high-growth assets
- Beware CGT on future growth outside super

**Property Management:**
- Consider whether property revaluations trigger Division 296 unnecessarily
- Valuations must still be accurate and defensible to ATO

**Best For:** SMSF members with control over asset structures and timing.

### Strategy 7: Multi-Year Tax Planning

**How It Works:**
Structure contributions, withdrawals, and asset sales across multiple years to minimise Division 296 impact.

**Tactics:**
- Defer concessional contributions to next FY if close to threshold
- Time asset sales to occur in lower-earning years
- Strategic withdrawal timing to manage TSB levels

**Considerations:**
- Requires detailed financial modelling
- Market conditions can disrupt plans
- Contribution caps must still be observed

**Best For:** High-net-worth individuals with complex super arrangements.

## Common Misconceptions About Division 296 Tax

### Myth 1: "It's a Tax on Your Super Balance"

**Reality:** Division 296 is a tax on earnings, not on the balance itself. A $4 million super balance doesn't automatically create a $150,000 tax bill (15% of $1 million excess). The tax only applies to the proportion of earnings related to the excess.

### Myth 2: "I Can Avoid It by Using Multiple Funds"

**Reality:** The ATO aggregates all your super interests. Splitting a $4 million balance into four $1 million accounts doesn't avoid Division 296 tax.

### Myth 3: "The $3 Million Threshold Will Be Indexed"

**Reality:** The legislation does NOT index the $3 million threshold to inflation or average weekly earnings. This is a static threshold, meaning inflation and super growth will push more Australians into the net over time.

By 2050, Treasury modelling suggests 500,000+ Australians could be affected (up from 80,000 today).

### Myth 4: "I Won't Pay Tax on Unrealised Gains"

**Reality:** Division 296 earnings include both realised AND unrealised capital gains. If your SMSF property increases in value by $500,000 without selling, that counts as earnings for Division 296 purposes.

This is one of the most controversial aspects of the tax.

### Myth 5: "Negative Earnings Create Tax Credits"

**Reality:** If your super makes a loss, you don't get credits or refunds for future years. Each financial year is assessed independently. A -$200,000 loss in FY26 doesn't offset $200,000 in gains in FY27.

### Myth 6: "Only Retirees Are Affected"

**Reality:** While most people with $3m+ in super are near or in retirement, younger high-income earners with aggressive super strategies (like property in SMSFs) can also be caught. Age is not a factor in the tax application.

### Myth 7: "I Can Pay the Tax from My Super Fund"

**Partial Reality:** SMSF members can pay the Division 296 tax from their fund, but this requires a formal release authority from the ATO. Members of APRA-regulated funds may have limited options and might need to pay from personal resources.

## Division 296 vs Other Super Taxes

Understanding how Division 296 interacts with other super taxes helps with holistic planning.

### Division 296 vs Transfer Balance Cap (TBC)

| Feature | Division 296 Tax | Transfer Balance Cap |
|---------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Threshold** | $3 million TSB | $1.9 million (2024-25) |
| **What It Limits** | Taxes earnings above threshold | Limits tax-free pension balance |
| **Tax Rate** | 15% on excess earnings | Excess goes to accumulation (15% tax) |
| **Indexed?** | No | Yes (indexed to CPI) |
| **Applies To** | All super interests | Only pension phase balances |

**Key Difference:** TBC limits how much you can hold in tax-free pension phase. Division 296 taxes earnings on total balances over $3 million, regardless of phase.

**Strategic Interplay:** Balances over $1.9 million but under $3 million are in a "sweet spot"—excess sits in accumulation (15% tax) without triggering Division 296 (additional 15% tax).

### Division 296 vs Excess Contributions Tax

| Feature | Division 296 | Excess Contributions |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Trigger** | TSB > $3 million | Exceeding annual caps |
| **Penalty** | 15% on proportion of earnings | Up to 47% on excess amount |
| **Ongoing?** | Annual tax every year | One-time penalty per breach |
| **Can Be Withdrawn?** | No withdrawal option | Can request excess release |

**Planning Note:** Managing contribution timing helps avoid excess contributions tax, but doesn't prevent Division 296 if your TSB exceeds threshold.

### Division 296 vs CGT in Super

| Feature | Inside Super (Standard) | Inside Super (Division 296) | Outside Super (Personal) |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Realised Gains Tax** | 15% (10% if held >12 months) | 30% on excess portion | Up to 47% (23.5% with CGT discount) |
| **Unrealised Gains Tax** | None | 15% on excess portion | None |
| **Estate Tax** | None | Subject to death benefits tax if to non-dependant | None |

**Key Insight:** For balances over $3 million, holding growth assets inside super may be less tax-efficient than holding them personally (where unrealised gains aren't taxed annually).

## Impact on SMSFs vs Retail/Industry Funds

Division 296 tax affects all super structures, but administrative burden and planning flexibility differs.

### SMSF Considerations

**Advantages:**
- Full control over asset allocation and timing
- Can implement sophisticated tax strategies
- Flexibility in valuations (within ATO guidelines)
- Ability to manage contribution and withdrawal timing
- Can pay Division 296 tax from fund (with ATO release authority)

**Disadvantages:**
- Complex calculations required
- Trustees responsible for accurate reporting
- Actuarial certificates may be needed
- Higher risk of ATO audit if aggressive strategies used
- Property revaluations can trigger unexpected tax

**SMSF-Specific Strategies:**
1. **In-specie pension payments** (transfer assets out tax-free if over 60)
2. **Limited recourse borrowing arrangement (LRBA) wind-down** to reduce TSB
3. **Asset segregation** for clearer tax allocation
4. **Careful timing** of property revaluations

### APRA-Regulated Fund Considerations

**Advantages:**
- Fund handles all calculations and reporting
- No trustee liability for errors
- Professional management of compliance
- Easier to understand (less control needed)

**Disadvantages:**
- Limited control over asset allocation
- Can't time contributions/withdrawals as precisely
- May not be able to pay Division 296 from super (need personal funds)
- Less flexibility in strategic planning

**Retail/Industry Fund Strategies:**
1. **Consolidation** of multiple accounts for easier management
2. **Investment option selection** (lower-growth options to reduce earnings)
3. **Contribution management** (pause or reduce contributions)
4. **Withdrawal planning** (strategic lump sums to reduce TSB)

## Tax Return and Payment Process

### How You'll Be Assessed

1. **ATO Calculates Your TSB:** The ATO receives data from all super funds and calculates your total superannuation balance automatically.

2. **Division 296 Assessment Issued:** If you exceed $3 million, the ATO issues a Division 296 tax assessment (separate from your income tax return).

3. **Payment Due:** Tax is due by your standard tax lodgement deadline (31 October or later if using a tax agent).

4. **Payment Options:**
   - Pay from personal funds
   - Request release authority from ATO to pay from super (SMSF or with fund consent)

### Payment from Super Fund

**SMSF Process:**
1. ATO issues Division 296 assessment
2. SMSF trustee requests release authority
3. ATO issues release authority document
4. Trustee pays from SMSF bank account
5. Amount counts as a benefit payment

**APRA Fund Process:**
1. ATO issues Division 296 assessment
2. Member requests fund to pay (fund consent required)
3. Fund may refuse if not in pension phase
4. If refused, member must pay from personal funds

### Record Keeping Requirements

You must maintain:
- Annual TSB statements from all funds
- Contribution records
- Withdrawal records
- Rollover documentation
- Asset valuations (especially for SMSFs)
- Actuarial certificates (if required)

Records must be kept for 5 years after the relevant Division 296 assessment.

## Estate Planning Implications

Division 296 tax has significant estate planning consequences that require proactive management.

### Death Benefits and Division 296

**What Happens When You Die:**
- Division 296 tax is calculated up to date of death
- Tax liability may be paid from estate or super death benefit
- Beneficiaries receive super minus Division 296 liability
- Standard death benefits tax still applies to non-dependants

**Strategic Considerations:**
1. **Binding Death Benefit Nominations:** Ensure clarity on who receives super and who pays Division 296 tax liability
2. **Insurance in Super:** Death benefit proceeds can pay Division 296 liability
3. **Withdrawal Before Death:** If terminal illness, consider strategic withdrawals to minimize Division 296 on death

### Division 296 vs Reversionary Pensions

**Reversionary Pension Impact:**
- If pension reverts to spouse, TSB transfers to spouse's balance
- May push spouse over $3 million threshold
- Division 296 tax assessed on deceased member up to death
- Surviving spouse faces Division 296 on ongoing earnings

**Planning Strategy:** Consider non-reversionary pensions with lump sum death benefit nominations to give beneficiaries more flexibility.

### Testamentary Trust Strategies

Some estate planners recommend:
- Withdrawing excess super above $3m before death (if time permits)
- Paying to testamentary trust to maintain tax effectiveness
- Avoiding Division 296 on death benefits
- Preserving wealth for beneficiaries

**Caution:** Testamentary trusts have their own tax implications and complexity. Professional advice essential.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. Is Division 296 tax indexed to inflation?

No. The $3 million threshold is NOT indexed to inflation, wages, or any other measure. It is a static threshold set in legislation. This means over time, more Australians will be caught by the tax as super balances grow and inflation erodes the real value of $3 million.

### 2. Can I avoid Division 296 by transferring super to my spouse?

Not directly. You cannot simply transfer existing super balances to your spouse. However, you can:
- Use contribution splitting for future concessional contributions
- Make spouse contributions (subject to caps)
- Redirect future employer contributions to spouse's super (requires employer agreement)

These strategies take years to have material impact on high balances.

### 3. What if I have a defined benefit pension?

Defined benefit interests are included in your TSB using the ATO's valuation formula (generally 16 × annual pension entitlement). If your defined benefit value plus other super exceeds $3 million, Division 296 applies.

Earnings from defined benefit interests are calculated differently using the ATO's prescribed method.

### 4. Does Division 296 apply to foreign super?

Generally yes, if the foreign super is considered a "foreign superannuation fund" under Australian tax law and you are an Australian tax resident. The ATO includes foreign super in TSB calculations in many cases.

However, tax treaties and specific fund structures can create exceptions. Professional cross-border tax advice is essential.

### 5. Can I get a refund if my super makes losses?

No. Division 296 is calculated annually on positive earnings. If your super makes a loss in a financial year, you simply have no Division 296 liability for that year. You cannot carry forward loss credits to offset future years' taxes.

### 6. What if I'm just over the threshold temporarily?

Division 296 is assessed annually. If your TSB exceeds $3 million even briefly during the financial year (at 30 June or just before a super event), you are subject to the tax for that year based on your excess at the measurement time.

Strategic withdrawal timing can help if you're borderline.

### 7. How does Division 296 affect SMSF property valuations?

SMSF property must be valued at market value annually (30 June). Unrealised gains from property revaluations count as "earnings" for Division 296 purposes.

This means:
- Property value increases trigger Division 296 tax even without selling
- You may need to fund the tax payment from other assets
- Strategic timing of property sales may help

You cannot manipulate valuations to avoid Division 296—valuations must be defensible to the ATO.

### 8. Can I pay Division 296 tax from my SMSF?

Yes, SMSF members can pay Division 296 tax from their fund, but it requires:
1. ATO to issue a Division 296 assessment
2. Trustee to request a release authority from ATO
3. ATO to issue the release authority
4. Trustee to pay from fund bank account

The amount paid counts as a benefit payment and may have tax implications depending on your age and fund phase.

### 9. What happens if I move overseas?

If you become a non-resident for tax purposes:
- Division 296 generally doesn't apply (as it's an Australian tax)
- However, SMSF residency rules may force wind-up
- Accessing super while non-resident has different tax treatment
- Returning to Australia may trigger retrospective issues

Seek specialist tax advice before relocating.

### 10. How do I know if my TSB exceeds $3 million?

The ATO calculates your TSB using data from:
- All APRA-regulated super funds
- SMSFs (from annual returns)
- Defined benefit schemes
- Government super schemes

You can check your TSB via:
- MyGov account (ATO section)
- Contacting each super fund for balances
- Tax agent with access to ATO systems
- Your annual EOFY super statements

The ATO will notify you if you exceed the threshold.

### 11. Can I have multiple SMSFs to avoid Division 296?

No. The ATO aggregates all super interests regardless of how many funds you have. Having four SMSFs with $750,000 each still creates a $3 million TSB subject to Division 296.

Multiple SMSFs may even increase costs (annual SMSF admin fees, audits, tax returns).

### 12. Will Division 296 be repealed or changed?

As of February 2026, Division 296 is law and operational. While some political parties have indicated they would review or repeal the tax, legislative change would require:
- Government policy decision
- Passage through both houses of Parliament
- Royal assent

Relying on future repeal is risky for tax planning. Plan as if Division 296 is permanent, and adjust if legislation changes.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Division 296 is real and operational:** From 1 July 2025, earnings on super balances over $3 million face an extra 15% tax (30% total).

2. **Unrealised gains are taxed:** Unlike other tax systems, Division 296 taxes unrealised capital gains, creating potential cash flow issues.

3. **The threshold is not indexed:** $3 million today will be worth much less in 20 years. More Australians will be caught over time.

4. **Strategic planning can reduce impact:** Withdrawals, spouse contributions, asset allocation, and timing strategies can all minimise Division 296 liability.

5. **SMSFs offer more flexibility:** Self-managed funds provide greater control for Division 296 planning, but also more complexity and risk.

6. **Professional advice is essential:** The interaction between Division 296, transfer balance cap, contribution caps, and estate planning is complex. DIY strategies risk costly mistakes.

7. **Don't panic if just over threshold:** Small excess balances create relatively modest tax bills. The cost of aggressive avoidance strategies may exceed the tax itself.

8. **Monitor your TSB regularly:** Know where you stand and model scenarios for future years. Proactive planning beats reactive crisis management.

## Next Steps

If your superannuation balance is approaching or exceeds $3 million:

1. **Calculate your current TSB** across all funds and account types
2. **Model your Division 296 liability** for the current and next 3 financial years
3. **Review your contribution strategy** (employer, concessional, non-concessional)
4. **Assess withdrawal options** if you've met a condition of release
5. **Consider spouse super strategies** if applicable
6. **Evaluate asset allocation** between super and personal holdings
7. **Update estate planning** documents and death benefit nominations
8. **Engage qualified professionals** (financial planner, tax agent, estate lawyer)

Division 296 tax is here to stay. Understanding how it works and planning accordingly is now an essential component of retirement planning for high-net-worth Australians.
