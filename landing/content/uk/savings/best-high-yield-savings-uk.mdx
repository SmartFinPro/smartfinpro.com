### Do You Need a Cash ISA?

**You need a Cash ISA if:**
- You're a **higher or additional rate taxpayer** with savings above £500/£0
- You're a **basic rate taxpayer** with savings earning more than £1,000/year in interest (roughly £22,000+ at 4.5%)

## FSCS Protection: What You Need to Know

The Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) protects up to **£85,000 per person, per banking licence** if a bank fails.

### Critical Points:

**Per banking licence, not per account.** If you have a savings account and current account with the same bank, they share the £85,000 limit.

**Joint accounts get £170,000 protection.** Each person's £85,000 applies separately.

**Some banks share licences.** For example:
- Halifax, Lloyds, Bank of Scotland, Scottish Widows (all Lloyds Banking Group)
- NatWest, Royal Bank of Scotland, Ulster Bank (all NatWest Group)
- HSBC, First Direct, M&S Bank (all HSBC Holdings)

**If you have more than £85,000:** Spread your savings across banks with different licences. The Raisin platform makes this easier by offering products from multiple independently-licensed banks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the highest savings interest rate in the UK right now?

The highest headline rate is 7.00% AER from First Direct's regular saver (limited to £300/month, current account required). For unrestricted savings, the best easy-access rate is 4.95% AER (Raisin UK) and the best 1-year fixed rate is 5.20% AER (Gatehouse Bank).

### Is my money safe in a savings account?

Yes, provided the account is FSCS-protected. All accounts in our comparison are protected up to £85,000 per person, per banking licence. This protection applies even if the bank fails.

### Should I choose easy-access or fixed-rate savings?

It depends on when you'll need the money. Easy-access is essential for your emergency fund (3-6 months of expenses). Fixed-rate bonds are better for money you won't need for 1-5 years. The best approach is usually a combination of both.

### Do I need to pay tax on savings interest?

Most people don't, thanks to the Personal Savings Allowance: basic rate taxpayers can earn £1,000/year in interest tax-free; higher rate taxpayers get £500. Interest earned in a Cash ISA is always tax-free. You only pay tax if your total savings interest exceeds your PSA.

### How often should I check my savings rates?

At least every 3-6 months. Banks frequently change rates, especially on easy-access accounts. Set a calendar reminder to compare your current rate against the best available. If your rate has dropped significantly, consider switching.

### Can I open savings accounts with multiple banks?

Yes, and we recommend it. Spreading savings across banks provides better FSCS protection and allows you to access the best rates from different providers. Platforms like Raisin UK make this especially easy.

### What's the difference between AER and gross interest?

AER (Annual Equivalent Rate) shows what you'd earn over a full year with compounding. Gross rate shows the simple interest without compounding. AER is always equal to or slightly higher than the gross rate. Always compare AER for a fair comparison between accounts.

### Are challenger banks safe for savings?

Yes, provided they're FSCS-protected and FCA-authorised. Banks like Gatehouse, Shawbrook, and OakNorth hold full UK banking licences and offer the same £85,000 FSCS protection as high-street banks. In fact, they often offer better rates precisely because they have lower overhead costs.

### How much should I have in savings?

A good starting target is 3-6 months of essential expenses (£8,000-£16,000 for the average UK household). Beyond that, savings goals are personal—house deposit, retirement, education, or general wealth building.

### What happens to my savings if interest rates fall?

Easy-access and notice account rates will likely fall in line with the base rate. Fixed-rate bonds are unaffected during their term—your rate is locked in. This is why we recommend fixing a portion of your savings now while rates are high.
