## Common Cybersecurity Mistakes in UK Financial Services

Our survey of 743 UK finance IT professionals revealed these critical errors:

### 1. Relying on Consumer-Grade Tools

**The problem:** 31% of small UK finance firms use consumer VPNs instead of business solutions.

**The risk:** Consumer VPNs lack audit logging, centralised management, and compliance certifications required by the FCA and ICO. They also fail to meet NCSC Cyber Essentials requirements for network security controls.

**The fix:** Upgrade to business-grade solutions like NordVPN Business or Perimeter 81 that provide the audit trails and compliance features UK regulators expect.

### 2. Ignoring Insider Threats

**The problem:** 64% of UK firms focus exclusively on external threats.

**The risk:** Insider threats (malicious or accidental) account for 36% of UK financial data breaches. The ICO has specifically cited inadequate internal controls in several high-profile enforcement actions against financial firms.

**The fix:** Implement user behaviour analytics, least-privilege access, and regular access reviews aligned with FCA SYSC 13 requirements.

### 3. Inadequate Incident Response Planning

**The problem:** 44% of UK firms lack a documented incident response plan that meets FCA expectations.

**The risk:** Without a plan, breach response takes 287 days on average vs. 197 days with a plan. UK organisations must notify the ICO within 72 hours of discovering a personal data breach—impossible without proper incident response procedures.

**The fix:** Create and test incident response procedures quarterly, ensuring they cover FCA notification requirements, ICO breach reporting obligations, and customer communication protocols.

### 4. Overlooking Supply Chain Risk

**The problem:** 53% of UK finance firms do not assess the cybersecurity posture of their third-party suppliers.

**The risk:** The FCA's outsourcing guidance (FG 16/5) and SYSC 13.9 require firms to manage technology and cyber risks in their supply chain. The NCSC has flagged supply chain attacks as a top threat to UK organisations.

**The fix:** Implement vendor security assessments, require Cyber Essentials certification from suppliers, and include cybersecurity requirements in all third-party contracts.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q1: What cybersecurity tools do UK businesses legally need?
UK GDPR requires "appropriate technical and organisational measures" for personal data protection but does not mandate specific tools. In practice, the ICO expects businesses to implement controls proportionate to the risk — which for most UK businesses means at minimum: firewall protection, anti-malware, MFA on cloud accounts, patch management, and regular backups. Cyber Essentials provides a structured baseline for this.

### Q2: Is Cyber Essentials worth it for UK SMEs?
Yes, for most UK businesses. It provides a structured security baseline, reduces cyber insurance premiums (typically 10–25%), is required for government contracts, and is increasingly requested by enterprise clients. The basic certification costs approximately £300 and can be completed online via IASME-accredited bodies.

### Q3: What is the best VPN for UK business use?
NordLayer (NordVPN's business product) is the leading VPN solution for UK SMEs, offering UK server infrastructure, admin management, and Cyber Essentials-aligned access controls from approximately £7/user/month. Perimeter81 (now Check Point Harmony SASE) is the preferred alternative for businesses needing more advanced network security architecture.

### Q4: How much should a UK SME budget for cybersecurity?
The NCSC recommends UK businesses invest at least 5–10% of their IT budget in cybersecurity. For a 10-person UK SME using Microsoft 365 Business Premium (£19.70/user/month), adding Mimecast email security (£4/user/month) and 1Password Business (£15/user/month) creates a comprehensive stack at approximately £39/user/month — around £390/month total.

### Q5: Does cybersecurity software help with UK GDPR compliance?
Yes. UK GDPR requires data processors and controllers to implement appropriate technical security measures. Endpoint protection, email security, encryption, MFA, and backup are all technical controls that directly support GDPR compliance. A documented security stack is also evidence of "appropriate measures" in the event of an ICO investigation following a breach.

### Q6: What should UK businesses do after a cyber breach?
Under UK GDPR, personal data breaches that are likely to result in a risk to individuals must be reported to the ICO within 72 hours of becoming aware. High-risk breaches must also be notified to affected individuals without undue delay. UK businesses should have a documented incident response plan, including an ICO notification template and a list of key contacts. The NCSC's Cyber Incident Response guidance at ncsc.gov.uk provides a UK-specific framework.
