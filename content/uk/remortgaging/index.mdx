# Complete Remortgaging Guide UK 2026

With over 1.4 million UK fixed-rate mortgages ending in 2026 and the Bank of England base rate at 4.5%, remortgaging has never been more critical for British homeowners. Those who fail to act before their fixed rate expires face automatic reversion to Standard Variable Rates (SVR) of 7.5% to 9.5%, resulting in monthly payment increases of £300 to £800. On a typical £250,000 mortgage, the difference between remortgaging proactively to a competitive rate (4.8%) versus defaulting to SVR (8.9%) amounts to £6,420 annually or £32,100 over a five-year term in unnecessary interest payments. This guide covers everything UK homeowners need to know about remortgaging in 2026, from timing and process to broker selection and cost optimisation.

## Why Remortgaging Matters in 2026

The financial consequences of inaction are severe for homeowners whose fixed-rate deals are ending this year. When your fixed rate expires, your lender automatically moves you onto their Standard Variable Rate, which at the major UK banks currently sits between 7.5% and 9.5%. This is not a modest increase. On a £250,000 mortgage with 20 years remaining, a homeowner who was paying £1,437 per month on an expiring 3.5% fix would see their monthly payment jump to £2,178 on an 8.9% SVR. That is an extra £741 every month disappearing into unnecessary interest, totalling £8,892 per year. By contrast, remortgaging to a new 5-year fix at 4.8% would result in monthly payments of £1,643, saving £6,420 annually compared to SVR.

The remortgaging triggers in 2026 are clear. The most common scenario is a fixed rate ending, which affects 1.4 million households this year alone. Beyond that, homeowners should consider remortgaging if interest rates have fallen 0.5% or more below their current rate, if their property value has risen significantly (improving their LTV ratio and unlocking better rates), if they want to release equity for home improvements or debt consolidation, or if their financial situation has improved through higher income or better credit. Property value increases are particularly powerful because every 5% improvement in LTV typically unlocks rates 0.1% to 0.3% lower. A homeowner who purchased at £300,000 with 90% LTV but whose property is now worth £380,000 would have a current LTV of roughly 71%, potentially saving £1,740 annually through improved rate access.

## When Should You Remortgage?

Timing is everything in remortgaging. The optimal approach is to begin your research six months before your fixed rate ends, obtain Agreement in Principle offers four months before expiry, submit your full application three months out, and complete the switch two weeks before your deal finishes. Your lender is required to notify you between four and six months before your fixed rate ends, and this notification should be your immediate trigger to act. The reason six months is the right starting point is that UK lenders allow you to lock in rates up to six months in advance. If rates rise during this period, you are protected at the lower rate. If rates fall, most lenders will allow you to switch to the lower rate before completion.

For homeowners considering an early exit from their current fix because rates have dropped significantly, the maths must be done carefully. Most UK lenders charge Early Repayment Charges (ERCs) of 1% to 5% of the outstanding balance to exit a fixed deal early, typically structured as 5% in year one declining by 1% each subsequent year. On a £200,000 mortgage two years into a five-year fix, the ERC would be roughly 3% or £6,000. If the new rate saves £150 per month, that is £1,800 per year, meaning break-even takes 40 months. Only remortgage early if you plan to remain in the property for at least three more years after the switch.

<Tip>
**Lock in rates early:** UK lenders allow you to secure a remortgage rate up to 6 months before your current deal ends. If rates rise, you keep the locked rate. If rates fall, most lenders let you switch to the lower rate before completion. There is no downside to starting early.
</Tip>

Rising property values can also unlock significant savings through improved LTV ratios. The rate reductions at each LTV threshold are meaningful:

## How to Remortgage: Step-by-Step

The remortgaging process has ten distinct steps. First, gather your current mortgage details including lender name, outstanding balance, current interest rate, fixed rate end date, and Early Repayment Charge schedule. Check your annual mortgage statement, online portal, or call your lender directly. Second, calculate your equity and LTV by dividing your outstanding mortgage by your current property value and multiplying by 100. A £180,000 mortgage on a £300,000 property gives you a 60% LTV, putting you in the best rate bracket.

Third, check your credit score for free across all three UK bureaus: Experian (free via their app, target 961+ for best rates), Equifax (free via ClearScore, target 531+), and TransUnion (free via Credit Karma UK, target 628+). If your score is below target, dispute any errors, pay down credit card balances below 30% utilisation, ensure you are on the electoral roll, and wait three to six months for improvements before applying. Fourth, determine your affordability. UK lenders stress-test at 6% to 7% interest rates and typically lend 4.5x to 5.5x combined annual income. Monthly debt payments must stay below 40% of gross income.

Fifth, compare rates and product types. In March 2026, two-year fixed rates range from 4.3% to 5.8%, five-year fixes from 4.2% to 5.5%, and trackers sit at roughly base rate plus 1.5% (6.0%). Two-year fixes suit those expecting rate decreases, five-year fixes offer the best balance for most homeowners, and trackers are only appropriate for risk-tolerant borrowers comfortable with payment volatility.

<CollapsibleSection title="Steps 6-10: Application Through Completion" count={5}>

**Step 6 — Choose between DIY or broker.** Going direct to your lender saves broker fees and gives you direct control, but you only access one lender's products. A whole-of-market broker accesses 90+ lenders, provides expert advice, improves approval odds for complex cases, and is typically free (the lender pays commission). Use a broker for first-time remortgages and complex income situations.

**Step 7 — Obtain Agreement in Principle (AIP).** You will need proof of income (3 months' payslips or SA302 for self-employed), bank statements (3-6 months), proof of address, photo ID, and details of existing debts. Soft search AIPs from Habito and Trussle do not affect your credit score. AIPs are valid for 60-90 days.

**Step 8 — Submit full mortgage application.** Provide full income verification (P60, SA302), complete bank statements, and solicitor details. The lender arranges a property valuation (£150-£300). Processing takes 2-6 weeks from application to mortgage offer.

**Step 9 — Legal work (conveyancing).** Even though you are staying in the same property, a solicitor must review the mortgage offer, conduct title searches, arrange redemption of your old mortgage, register the new mortgage with Land Registry, and transfer funds. Costs run £500-£1,200 including searches. Some lenders offer free legal services for straightforward remortgages.

**Step 10 — Complete and switch.** Your solicitor receives funds from the new lender, your old mortgage is redeemed, the new mortgage begins on the agreed date, and you set up a new Direct Debit. Total timeline from start to completion is 8-16 weeks.

</CollapsibleSection>

## Remortgage Costs

Understanding the full cost picture is essential because the lowest interest rate does not always mean the cheapest deal overall. Unavoidable costs include a valuation fee (£150-£500), legal fees (£500-£1,200), Land Registry fee (£40-£100), and potentially a lender arrangement fee (£0-£2,000). Optional costs include broker fees (£0-£1,500, though many charge nothing), Early Repayment Charges if exiting a fix early (1%-5% of balance), and a mortgage exit fee from your old lender (£50-£300). Total upfront costs typically range from £700 to £4,000 depending on your choices.

Many lenders now offer fee-free remortgage deals with zero arrangement fee, free valuation, and free legal work for basic cases. The trade-off is a slightly higher interest rate, typically 0.1% to 0.2% more. On a £250,000 five-year fix, a fee-free deal at 4.8% with no fees costs £102,897 total, while a fee-paying deal at 4.5% with £1,500 in fees costs £100,665. The fee-paying option saves £2,232 over the term. Choose fee-free if you plan to remortgage again within two to three years; choose fee-paying if you are fixing for five or more years and want the lowest overall cost.

<Warning>
**Do not ignore the total cost of borrowing.** A lower interest rate with a £1,995 arrangement fee can actually cost more than a higher rate with no fees. Always compare the "total amount payable" over your fixed term, not just the headline rate. Ask your broker for total cost comparisons across products.
</Warning>

One of the most expensive mistakes homeowners make is choosing the wrong mortgage term. Extending from 15 years to 25 years on a £200,000 mortgage at 4.5% reduces monthly payments by £419 but adds £57,900 in total interest over the life of the loan. Choose the shortest term you can comfortably afford, and overpay when possible. Another common error is accepting a product transfer from your current lender without comparing the market. Lender product transfers are convenient (no valuation, no legal fees, fast process), but rates are often 0.2% to 0.5% higher than the best market deals. On a £250,000 mortgage, that 0.5% difference costs £1,248 per year.

## Remortgaging with Bad Credit

Homeowners with impaired credit can still remortgage, though the available rates and lender options narrow considerably. The credit score bands determine your realistic options: an Experian score of 961 or above gives access to all lenders at rates of 4.2% to 4.8%, scores of 881 to 960 qualify for most high street banks at 4.6% to 5.4%, the 721 to 880 range requires specialist lenders at 5.5% to 7.2%, and scores below 720 are limited to adverse credit specialists charging 6.5% to 9.5% or higher. Factors that count as bad credit include CCJs within three years, defaults on credit accounts, bankruptcy or IVA within six years, and late mortgage payments within the past 12 months.

To improve your chances, start six months before remortgaging by pulling all three credit reports and disputing errors, paying credit card balances below 30% utilisation, ensuring all bills are paid by Direct Debit, registering on the electoral roll, and avoiding any new credit applications. Working with a specialist adverse credit broker is essential in this situation. Once you have remortgaged and made 12 to 24 months of on-time payments, your score improves significantly, allowing you to remortgage again at better rates.

## Should You Fix for 2, 5, or 10 Years?

The choice of fixed-rate term is one of the most consequential decisions in the remortgaging process. Two-year fixes offer the lowest rates (4.3%-4.9%) and flexibility to remortgage sooner if rates fall, but you face remortgage costs every two years (£1,500-£2,500 in fees and admin) and renewed exposure to rate movements. Five-year fixes carry only marginally higher rates (4.5%-5.2%) while providing medium-term certainty, avoiding remortgage costs for half a decade, and simplifying financial planning. Ten-year fixes offer ultimate payment security but at a meaningful premium (5.2%-6.1%) with substantial ERCs and significant opportunity cost if rates decline.

For most homeowners in 2026, a five-year fixed rate offers the best balance. With base rates expected to stabilise between 4% and 5% through 2028, locking in a five-year fix at 4.5% to 5% provides certainty without the rate premium of a ten-year deal. Choose a two-year fix only if you expect rates to fall significantly by 2028 or plan to move within two years. Choose a ten-year fix only if you strongly prioritise absolute payment certainty and are willing to pay a 0.5% to 1% rate premium for that security.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does remortgaging take in the UK?

From initial research to completion, expect 8-16 weeks. The breakdown is roughly: rate research (1-2 weeks), obtaining AIP (1 week), full application and valuation (2-4 weeks), mortgage offer (2-3 weeks), and legal work plus completion (3-4 weeks). Starting 6 months before your fixed rate ends ensures you complete the process without reverting to SVR.

### What happens if my fixed rate ends and I have not remortgaged?

You automatically revert to your lender's Standard Variable Rate, typically 7.5% to 9.5%. This can increase monthly payments by £300-£800 on a typical mortgage. You are not locked into SVR and can remortgage at any time with no early exit penalties, but you will pay the higher rate until your new mortgage completes (6-12 weeks).

### Should I use a mortgage broker or go direct?

A whole-of-market broker is the right choice for most remortgages. Brokers access 90+ lenders versus one if you go direct, are free for most borrowers, and significantly improve approval odds for complex cases. Only go direct if you have excellent credit, simple income, and your current lender offers a competitive product transfer within 0.2% of the best market rates.

### Can I remortgage with bad credit?

Yes, but expect higher rates and limited lender choice. Specialist adverse credit lenders charge 5.5% to 9.5% compared to 4.5% for excellent credit. Improve your score for 6-12 months before applying if possible, and always work with a specialist broker who understands adverse credit underwriting.

### How much equity do I need to remortgage?

A minimum of 5-10% equity (90-95% LTV) is required, but better rates need 20%+ equity. The key LTV thresholds are: 90% to 85% (saves ~0.4%), 85% to 80% (saves ~0.3%), 80% to 75% (saves ~0.2%), and 75% to 60% (saves ~0.2%). If you are close to a threshold, consider overpaying your mortgage before remortgaging to unlock a better rate bracket.

### What fees are involved in remortgaging?

Typical costs are: arrangement fee (£0-£2,000, often added to mortgage), valuation (£150-£500), legal fees (£500-£1,200), and old lender exit fee (£50-£300). Total upfront costs range from £700 to £4,000. Many lenders offer fee-free remortgages with slightly higher rates. Compare total cost over your fixed term to determine which approach is cheaper.

### Can I remortgage to release equity?

Yes. Lenders allow you to borrow up to 80-90% of your current property value. Calculate available equity as: (current value x 80%) minus current mortgage balance. For example, a £350,000 home with a £200,000 mortgage provides £80,000 of available equity at 80% LTV. Ensure affordability after the increased borrowing.

### Can I remortgage if I am self-employed?

UK lenders typically require 2-3 years of accounts or SA302 tax calculations. Some specialist lenders accept 1 year of accounts for established professionals. Lenders average your income over 2-3 years, so inconsistent earnings reduce borrowing capacity. Use a broker experienced in self-employed mortgages.
