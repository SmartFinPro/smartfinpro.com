## What Actually Happens When Your Fixed Rate Ends?

Your mortgage doesn't end when your fixed-rate period expires - only the fixed interest rate ends. Here's what happens automatically:

### The Automatic Switch to SVR

**Day 1 after your fixed rate expires:**
- Your mortgage continues as normal
- You automatically move onto your lender's Standard Variable Rate (SVR)
- Your monthly payment increases (sometimes dramatically)
- You can leave at any time with no early repayment charges

**There is no action required** - this happens automatically. However, doing nothing is the most expensive option.

### Understanding the SVR (Standard Variable Rate)

The SVR is your lender's default interest rate for customers not on a fixed deal. Key characteristics:

**It's variable:** Your lender can increase or decrease it at any time, for any reason. They're not obliged to follow Bank of England base rate changes.

**It's expensive:** SVRs average 8.00% in February 2026, compared to:
- 2-year fixed rates: 4.65-5.25%
- 5-year fixed rates: 4.35-4.95%
- 10-year fixed rates: 4.50-5.10%

**It has no exit fees:** You can remortgage away from the SVR at any time without paying early repayment charges.

**It varies by lender:** Some lenders have SVRs as low as 7.50%, others as high as 8.50%.

## Your 5 Options When Your Fixed Rate Ends

### Option 1: Remortgage to a New Lender (Most Popular)

**What it means:** Apply for a mortgage with a completely different lender, paying off your existing mortgage with the new one.

**The process:**
1. Compare rates across all lenders (use a broker for whole-of-market access)
2. Apply for a new mortgage
3. New lender conducts valuation and affordability checks
4. Solicitor handles legal transfer
5. New lender pays off old mortgage
6. You start making payments to new lender

**Timeline:** 4-8 weeks from application to completion

**Costs:**
- Arrangement fee: £0-£2,000 (often added to mortgage)
- Valuation: Usually free for remortgages
- Legal fees: Usually free (included by lender)
- Broker fee: £0 with free brokers like Habito

**Who it's best for:**
- Anyone who can find a significantly better rate elsewhere (usually 0.3%+ better than your current lender's offers)
- People who want to release equity
- Those unhappy with their current lender's service
- Anyone wanting to switch mortgage type or features

**Pros:**
✓ Access to the whole market (90+ lenders)
✓ Usually find the best available rates
✓ Can release equity if needed
✓ Can switch product types
✓ Get incentives (cashback, free legals, free valuation)
✓ Escape poor lender service

**Cons:**
✗ Takes longer (4-8 weeks vs instant for product transfer)
✗ Full affordability checks required
✗ Need to provide full documentation again
✗ Might not be approved if circumstances have worsened

**Real example:**

Sarah's 5-year fixed rate at 2.89% ended in January 2026. Her lender's SVR is 8.15%, but their product transfer rates were 5.75% for a 2-year fix.

Using a broker, she found:
- Santander 2-year fix: 4.65% (£999 fee)
- HSBC 5-year fix: 4.40% (£999 fee)
- Nationwide 5-year fix: 4.50% (no fee)

She chose HSBC's 5-year fix. On her £220,000 mortgage:
- Lender's product transfer at 5.75%: £1,363/month
- New HSBC deal at 4.40%: £1,221/month
- **Monthly saving: £142**
- **5-year saving: £8,520 (minus £999 fee = £7,521 net saving)**

### Option 2: Product Transfer with Current Lender (Easiest)

**What it means:** Stay with your existing lender but switch to one of their new fixed-rate products.

**The process:**
1. Contact your lender (usually 3-6 months before deal ends)
2. Review their available products
3. Choose a new fixed rate
4. Sign new mortgage offer
5. New rate starts on agreed date

**Timeline:** Can complete in days or weeks

**Costs:**
- Arrangement fee: £0-£999 (often lower than remortgage fees)
- No valuation required
- No legal fees
- No broker needed

**Who it's best for:**
- People who value simplicity and speed
- Those with changed circumstances (might not pass affordability checks elsewhere)
- When your lender's rates are competitive with the market
- If you're remortgaging very close to deadline

**Pros:**
✓ Extremely simple and fast
✓ Usually no affordability checks (if income similar)
✓ No valuation required
✓ No legal work
✓ Guaranteed acceptance (assuming you've made payments)
✓ Can do it right up to the day your deal ends

**Cons:**
✗ Limited to one lender's products
✗ Often not the best rate available
✗ Can't release equity
✗ Miss out on incentives from other lenders
✗ Stuck with poor service if lender is difficult

**Real example:**

Michael's 2-year fix at 4.79% ended in February 2026. His lender (Halifax) offered him:
- 2-year fix: 5.29% (£999 fee)
- 5-year fix: 4.99% (£999 fee)

He was tempted by the simplicity but decided to check the market using a broker. Best whole-of-market rates were:
- 2-year fix: 4.65% (£999 fee)
- 5-year fix: 4.35% (£999 fee)

The 5-year market rate was 0.64% better than Halifax's offer. On his £185,000 mortgage:
- Halifax product transfer: £1,080/month
- Best market rate: £1,008/month
- **Monthly saving by remortgaging: £72**
- **5-year saving: £4,320**

He chose to remortgage rather than product transfer, saving over £4,000.

**Key lesson:** Always compare your lender's product transfer rates against the whole market, even if you end up choosing the product transfer for convenience.

### Option 3: Overpay Your Mortgage (Advanced Strategy)

**What it means:** Make extra payments to reduce your mortgage balance faster, then remortgage with a lower balance (and lower LTV).

**The strategy:**
1. When your fixed rate ends, temporarily stay on SVR
2. Overpay as much as possible (there's no overpayment limit on SVR)
3. After 3-6 months, remortgage with lower balance
4. Potentially qualify for better LTV tier

**Timeline:** Stay on SVR for 3-6 months while overpaying

**Who it's best for:**
- People who are close to an LTV threshold (e.g. 81% LTV, close to 80%)
- Those with significant spare income or a lump sum
- Homeowners who can afford the higher SVR payments temporarily
- People with very large mortgages where LTV tiers matter enormously

**The maths:**

LTV tiers and typical rates (February 2026):
- 95% LTV: 5.75%
- 90% LTV: 5.25%
- 85% LTV: 5.00%
- 80% LTV: 4.75%
- 75% LTV: 4.50%
- 60% LTV: 4.35%

**Example scenario:**

Property value: £300,000
Current mortgage: £243,000 (81% LTV)
Current rate ending: 3.50%

**Option A: Remortgage immediately at 81% LTV**
- Rate available: 4.95% (85% LTV tier)
- Monthly payment: £1,396
- Total interest over 5 years: £78,800

**Option B: Overpay for 6 months, then remortgage**
- Stay on 8% SVR: £1,919/month
- Overpay £1,000/month extra (total £2,919/month)
- After 6 months: Balance reduced to £237,000 (79% LTV)
- Remortgage at 4.75% (80% LTV tier)
- New monthly payment: £1,357
- Total interest over 5.5 years: £76,400

**Outcome:** Option B costs £2,400 less in total interest, despite paying the high SVR for 6 months.

**Pros:**
✓ Reduce mortgage balance quickly
✓ Potentially access better LTV tier
✓ No overpayment limits on SVR
✓ Good use of spare cash earning low interest elsewhere

**Cons:**
✗ Requires significant spare income
✗ Pay high SVR rate in the meantime
✗ Only worthwhile if close to LTV threshold
✗ Risk of house prices falling (increasing LTV)
✗ Complicated to calculate optimal timing

**Warning:** This strategy only works if you're very close to an LTV threshold and have significant spare cash. For most people, remortgaging immediately is better.

### Option 4: Port Your Mortgage (For Movers)

**What it means:** Take your existing mortgage with you when you move house, avoiding early repayment charges.

**The process:**
1. Find new property
2. Apply to port your mortgage
3. Lender values new property
4. If borrowing more, apply for additional borrowing
5. Transfer mortgage to new property

**Timeline:** Same as house moving process (8-12 weeks typical)

**Who it's best for:**
- People planning to move house soon (within 6-12 months)
- Those with excellent mortgage rates they want to keep
- Anyone who would face high early repayment charges

**Pros:**
✓ Avoid early repayment charges
✓ Keep good rate if moving during fixed period
✓ Lender usually approves (already know you)

**Cons:**
✗ Only works if moving house
✗ May not be the best rate available for new property
✗ Lender must approve new property
✗ If borrowing more, additional borrowing at current rates (usually worse)
✗ Not all mortgages are portable

**Real example:**

David's 5-year fix at 2.59% ends in June 2026, but he's selling and buying a new house in March 2026. His ERC is 2% (£4,200 on his £210,000 mortgage).

His options:
1. Pay the £4,200 ERC and get a new mortgage
2. Port his 2.59% rate to the new property

He chose to port. His new property costs £320,000, requiring a £260,000 mortgage. He ported his existing £210,000 at 2.59% and borrowed an additional £50,000 at the current rate (4.75%).

Blended rate: 3.05% overall, much better than a new mortgage at 4.65%.

**Important:** Check whether your mortgage is portable (mortgage offer document) and whether porting makes sense compared to getting a new mortgage.

### Option 5: Stay on SVR (Rarely Recommended)

**What it means:** Do nothing. Accept the SVR and higher payments.

**Who it's best for:**
- People planning to sell very soon (within 3-6 months)
- Those who can't pass affordability checks for a new mortgage
- Homeowners who prioritise ultimate flexibility (no fixed period, can overpay or leave any time)
- People in temporary financial difficulty who want to reduce payments later

**Pros:**
✓ No effort required
✓ No arrangement fees
✓ Ultimate flexibility (overpay unlimited, exit any time)
✓ No commitment period

**Cons:**
✗ Most expensive option
✗ Rate can increase at any time
✗ No payment certainty
✗ Waste thousands in unnecessary interest

**When it actually makes sense:**

**Scenario 1: Selling imminently**

If you're selling in 3-6 months, the cost of arranging a new mortgage (fees, time, effort) may exceed the SVR overpayment for such a short period.

**Calculation:**
- SVR overpayment: £350/month
- Selling in 4 months: Total cost = £1,400
- New mortgage arrangement fee: £999
- Legal fees: £500 (if not free)
- **Staying on SVR cheaper in this case**

**Scenario 2: Affordability challenges**

If your income has dropped significantly or you have new debts, you might not pass affordability checks for a new mortgage. Product transfer might not be available if income changed drastically.

In this case, staying on SVR might be your only option until circumstances improve.

**Scenario 3: Expecting a windfall**

If you're expecting a large sum (inheritance, bonus, house sale) in 3-6 months that will allow you to pay off the mortgage entirely, staying on SVR temporarily makes sense.

**For 95% of people, staying on SVR is the wrong choice.** If none of the above scenarios apply to you, you should remortgage.

## When to Start the Process: Critical Timelines

### The 6-Month Rule

**Best practice:** Start researching 6 months before your fixed rate ends.

**Why 6 months?**
- You can reserve rates up to 6 months in advance
- Gives time to improve credit score if needed
- Can compare market thoroughly
- Ensures you don't fall onto SVR

### Month-by-Month Action Plan

**6 Months Before:**
- Check your mortgage end date (on annual statement)
- Review your credit report (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion)
- Estimate your property value
- Calculate your LTV
- Start monitoring rates

**5 Months Before:**
- Contact a whole-of-market broker
- Get initial recommendations
- Compare against your lender's product transfer rates
- Decide whether to remortgage or product transfer

**4 Months Before:**
- Improve credit score (pay down cards, register to vote, dispute errors)
- Gather documents (payslips, bank statements, ID)
- Get mortgage illustration showing exact costs

**3 Months Before:**
- Submit full application
- Lender arranges valuation
- Chase broker/lender for updates

**2 Months Before:**
- Receive mortgage offer
- Legal work begins
- Review and sign documents
- Set completion date

**1 Month Before:**
- Final legal checks
- Funds transferred
- Old mortgage redeemed
- New mortgage starts

**On End Date:**
- Your new deal begins seamlessly
- No gap, no SVR period
- Direct debit to new lender

### What If You've Left It Late?

**2-4 weeks before end date:**
- You can still remortgage but it will be tight
- Consider product transfer as faster backup
- Use a broker who offers fast-track service
- Be prepared to fall onto SVR for 1-2 months if completion delays

**Already on SVR:**
- Don't panic - you can leave any time
- Start process immediately
- No rush fees or penalties
- You'll just pay the high SVR rate until your new deal completes

**Every month on SVR costs you £300-500 in unnecessary interest, so act quickly.**

### Step 2: Compare Total Cost, Not Just Rate

**The rate is important, but total cost matters more.**

**Example:** Which is cheaper?

**Deal A:**
- Rate: 4.40%
- Fee: £1,999
- Monthly payment (£200k, 25 years): £1,104
- Total 5-year cost: (£1,104 × 60) + £1,999 = **£68,239**

**Deal B:**
- Rate: 4.55%
- Fee: £0
- Monthly payment: £1,122
- Total 5-year cost: (£1,122 × 60) + £0 = **£67,320**

**Deal B is cheaper despite higher rate** because it has no fee.

**Formula for comparison:**
```
(Monthly payment × Months of fixed term) + All fees = Total cost
```

Choose the deal with the lowest total cost unless you highly value the flexibility of a shorter fix.

### Step 3: Consider Total Cost of Mortgage, Not Just Monthly Payment

A lower monthly payment isn't always better if it costs more overall.

**Example:** Same mortgage, different terms

**Option A: 25-year term**
- Rate: 4.50%
- Monthly payment: £1,112
- Total interest paid: £133,600

**Option B: 30-year term**
- Rate: 4.50%
- Monthly payment: £1,013 (£99 less per month)
- Total interest paid: £164,680

**Option B costs £31,080 more** in interest, despite lower monthly payments.

Only extend your term if you genuinely can't afford the higher payments. If you can afford the 25-year payments, don't extend to 30 years just to reduce monthly costs.

### Step 4: Don't Ignore Overpayment Flexibility

**Standard overpayment allowance:** 10% of outstanding balance per year with no fees.

**Why it matters:** If you get a bonus, inheritance, or pay rise, you can reduce your mortgage faster without penalties.

**Check:**
- What's the overpayment allowance? (0%, 10%, 20%, unlimited)
- Are there payment holiday options?
- Can you underpay in difficult times?

**Some lenders (e.g., Nationwide, First Direct) allow unlimited overpayments** on certain products. If you plan to overpay, these are worth considering even at slightly higher rates.

## Common Mistakes When Your Fixed Rate Ends

### Mistake 1: Doing Nothing

**The error:** Assuming your lender will automatically move you to a good rate or contact you proactively.

**The reality:** Lenders send one letter 3-4 months before your deal ends. They're happy for you to stay on the expensive SVR. It's your responsibility to act.

**The cost:** £3,000-£5,000 per year in unnecessary interest.

**The fix:** Set a calendar reminder 6 months before your deal ends. Start the process early.

### Mistake 2: Only Checking Your Current Lender

**The error:** Accepting your lender's product transfer offer without comparing the wider market.

**The reality:** Lenders often charge existing customers 0.3-0.5% more than they charge new customers for identical products. They rely on your laziness.

**The cost:** £50-£150 per month (£600-£1,800 per year).

**The fix:** Always get a whole-of-market broker to compare rates, even if you end up doing a product transfer for convenience.

### Mistake 3: Choosing Rate Over Total Cost

**The error:** Choosing the lowest interest rate without considering fees and term.

**The reality:** A 4.30% deal with a £2,000 fee might cost more than a 4.50% deal with £0 fee.

**The cost:** £500-£2,000 over the fixed period.

**The fix:** Calculate total cost = (monthly payment × months) + all fees. Choose the lowest total cost.

### Mistake 4: Not Checking Your Credit Report First

**The error:** Applying for mortgages without knowing what's on your credit report.

**The reality:** Errors, outdated information, or unknown issues on your report can cause rejections or worse rates. Each rejection harms your score further.

**The cost:** 0.5-1% higher rate or outright rejection.

**The fix:** Check all three credit agencies (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion) 3 months before applying. Dispute any errors immediately.

### Mistake 5: Leaving It Too Late

**The error:** Starting the process 2-4 weeks before your deal ends.

**The reality:** Remortgaging takes 4-8 weeks. If you start late, you'll fall onto SVR for 1-3 months while the new mortgage completes.

**The cost:** £300-£500 per month for every month on SVR.

**The fix:** Start 6 months before. If you've left it late, use a fast-track broker and consider product transfer as backup.

## Special Situations

### Your Income Has Dropped

**Challenge:** You might not pass affordability checks for a new mortgage or even a product transfer.

**Solutions:**
1. **Extend your mortgage term** to reduce monthly payments (e.g., 20 years to 25 years)
2. **Add income** - add a partner or family member to the mortgage
3. **Product transfer** - some lenders don't re-check affordability for existing customers
4. **Specialist broker** - they know which lenders are flexible on income changes
5. **Temporarily stay on SVR** - buy time until income recovers

**Important:** Don't hide income changes. Lenders verify income and can withdraw offers if you're dishonest.

### You're Self-Employed

**Challenge:** Self-employed income assessment is stricter than employed.

**Solutions:**
1. **Use a specialist broker** who knows self-employed-friendly lenders
2. **Apply after a strong financial year** if possible
3. **Ensure all accounts/tax returns are filed and up to date**
4. **Provide 2-3 years' accounts** (some lenders accept 1 year if strong)
5. **Consider lenders who assess differently** (e.g., some use latest year only, not average)

**Best lenders for self-employed:** Atom Bank, Halifax, Santander, Skipton Building Society.

### You Have Bad Credit

**Challenge:** Missed payments, defaults, or CCJs since taking out your mortgage make remortgaging difficult.

**Solutions:**
1. **Use a bad credit specialist broker** (not a generalist)
2. **Wait if possible** (credit issues have less impact over time)
3. **Explain the circumstances** (one-off issues viewed better than ongoing problems)
4. **Accept higher rates temporarily** (2-5 years of good payment history allows you to remortgage to better rates later)
5. **Specialist lenders:** Pepper Money, Bluestone, Vida Homeloans, Aldermore

**Timeline for credit issues:**
- Missed payments: Less impact after 3 years, gone from report after 6 years
- Defaults: Significant for 3 years, gone after 6 years
- CCJs: Major impact for 4 years, gone after 6 years

### Your Property Value Has Fallen

**Challenge:** If house prices have dropped, your LTV has increased, limiting your options.

**Solutions:**
1. **Get an accurate valuation** (not just online estimates)
2. **Improve property before valuation** (repairs, decoration, kerb appeal)
3. **Provide evidence of comparable sales** at higher prices
4. **Overpay to reduce LTV** if you have cash available
5. **Product transfer if in negative equity** (remortgaging very difficult with negative equity)

**If in negative equity:** Very few options. Usually limited to product transfer with current lender. Consider waiting for house prices to recover if possible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How early can I start the remortgage process?

You can start 6 months before your current deal ends. Most lenders allow you to reserve rates up to 6 months in advance. This is the optimal time to begin - early enough to compare thoroughly but not so early that market conditions change significantly.

### What if rates drop after I've applied?

Most lenders allow you to switch to a lower rate if they reduce rates before completion, as long as you're still within your product availability period. Ask your broker or lender about their rate reduction policy when applying.

### Can I remortgage if I'm in negative equity?

Remortgaging in negative equity is extremely difficult. Very few lenders offer negative equity mortgages, and those that do charge very high rates. Your best options are: 1) Product transfer with your current lender, 2) Wait for house prices to recover, or 3) Pay down your mortgage to get out of negative equity.

### Will remortgaging affect my credit score?

Initially yes - each mortgage application creates a hard search on your credit report, which temporarily reduces your score by 5-10 points. However, this impact is minimal and disappears after 3-6 months. Multiple applications in a short period are treated as one search if clearly mortgage shopping.

### Do I need a solicitor?

Yes, all remortgages require legal work to transfer the mortgage from your old lender to your new one. However, most remortgage deals include free legal work, so you won't pay solicitor fees. If free legal work isn't included, expect to pay £300-£800.

### Can I release equity when remortgaging?

Yes, this is one of the main reasons people remortgage. You can typically borrow up to 85-90% of your property's value, though the best rates are available up to 75-80% LTV. The lender will assess affordability based on the higher borrowing amount.

### What documents do I need?

You'll need: ID (passport/driving licence), address proof (utility bill), income proof (3 months' payslips + P60 for employed; 2-3 years' accounts for self-employed), 3-6 months' bank statements, current mortgage statement, and property insurance details.

### Should I fix for 2, 5, or 10 years?

It depends on your circumstances:
- **2 years:** Best rates, but you'll need to remortgage again soon. Choose if you expect rates to fall or circumstances to change.
- **5 years:** Good balance - decent rates and medium-term certainty. Best for most people.
- **10 years:** Maximum certainty but slightly higher rates and large ERCs if you leave early. Choose if you want long-term stability and plan to stay in the property.

### What if I want to move house during my fixed term?

Check if your mortgage is portable (stated in your mortgage offer). If it is, you can take your mortgage with you to a new property. If it isn't portable, you'll pay early repayment charges (typically 1-5% of outstanding balance) when you move.
