## What Happens When Your Fixed Rate Ends

Over 1.2 million UK fixed-rate mortgages are ending in 2026. If yours is among them, you are likely facing a significant payment increase unless you take action before the deadline. The difference between doing nothing and actively managing your remortgage could save you thousands of pounds per year in unnecessary interest payments. This guide explains exactly what happens when your fixed rate expires and walks you through all five options available to UK homeowners.

<ExecutiveSummary title="Key Findings">

- **SVR rates average 8.00% in 2026 — up to 4% above current fixed deals**
- **Staying on SVR costs the typical homeowner an extra £3,000 to £5,000 per year**
- **You can reserve a new rate up to 6 months before your deal ends**
- **Remortgaging via a whole-of-market broker gives access to 90+ lenders and the best available rates**

**Bottom line:** For 95% of homeowners, remortgaging to a new deal or completing a product transfer is significantly cheaper than remaining on the SVR. Start the process at least 6 months before your fixed rate expires.

</ExecutiveSummary>

Your mortgage does not end when your fixed-rate period expires — only the promotional interest rate ends. On the first day after expiry, you automatically move onto your lender's Standard Variable Rate. There is no paperwork, no notification beyond a single letter sent 3 to 4 months beforehand, and no action required on your part for the switch to happen. However, doing nothing is the most expensive choice you can make. The SVR is a default rate your lender can adjust at any time, for any reason, without following Bank of England base rate changes. In February 2026, SVRs range from 7.50% to 8.50% depending on the lender, compared to 2-year fixed rates at 4.65% to 5.25% and 5-year fixed rates at 4.35% to 4.95%.

<Warning>
**SVR rate alert:** The average SVR in 2026 is 8.00%, which is roughly double the best available fixed rates. On a typical £200,000 mortgage with 25 years remaining, this translates to an extra £428 per month or £5,136 per year in unnecessary interest. Your lender is not obliged to contact you proactively — the responsibility to act is entirely yours.
</Warning>

## The Real Cost of Staying on SVR

The payment difference between a competitive fixed rate and the average SVR is substantial across all mortgage sizes. On a £150,000 mortgage with 20 years remaining, moving from a 4.50% fixed rate to 8.00% SVR increases your monthly payment by approximately £307, costing an extra £3,684 per year. For a £200,000 mortgage over 25 years, the monthly increase rises to roughly £428, or £5,136 annually. At £300,000 over 22 years the gap widens further: an additional £641 per month and £7,692 per year. These are not theoretical numbers — they represent real money leaving your account every month that you remain on the SVR without switching to a new deal.

## Your 5 Options Explained

### Option 1: Remortgage to a New Lender (Most Popular)

Remortgaging means applying for a mortgage with a completely different lender, which pays off your existing loan and starts a new agreement. This is the most popular choice because it gives you access to the entire market — more than 90 lenders — rather than being limited to your current provider's products. The process typically takes 4 to 8 weeks from application to completion. Costs usually include an arrangement fee of £0 to £2,000 (which can be added to the mortgage), while valuations and legal fees are almost always free for remortgages. Using a free whole-of-market broker means you pay nothing for advice and gain access to exclusive products not available when applying direct.

This option works best for anyone who can find a rate at least 0.3% better than their current lender's product transfer offer, anyone who wants to release equity, and anyone looking to switch mortgage type or features. The main trade-off is that full affordability checks are required, you need to provide complete documentation again, and there is no guarantee of approval if your financial circumstances have changed since your original mortgage.

### Option 2: Product Transfer with Current Lender (Easiest)

A product transfer means staying with your existing lender but switching to one of their newer fixed-rate products. This is by far the simplest route — you can often complete it in days rather than weeks. There is no valuation required, no legal work, and typically no fresh affordability assessment if your income has remained broadly similar. You can arrange a product transfer right up to the day your deal ends, making it an excellent backup option if you have left things late.

The downside is that you are limited to a single lender's product range, which may not be the most competitive. Lenders often charge existing customers 0.3% to 0.5% more than they charge new customers for identical products. Always compare your lender's product transfer rates against the whole market before accepting, even if you ultimately choose the product transfer for its convenience.

### Option 3: Overpay Your Mortgage (Advanced Strategy)

If you are very close to a lower loan-to-value threshold — for example, sitting at 81% LTV when the next pricing tier kicks in at 80% — temporarily staying on the SVR while making aggressive overpayments can be worthwhile. Because the SVR carries no early repayment charges, you can overpay without limit. After 3 to 6 months of overpaying, you remortgage at a lower balance and potentially qualify for a better LTV bracket with significantly improved rates. The maths only work if you are within a few percentage points of the threshold and have substantial spare cash available. For most homeowners, remortgaging immediately remains the better option.

### Option 4: Port Your Mortgage (For Movers)

Porting means taking your existing mortgage with you when you move house, preserving your current rate and avoiding early repayment charges. This is relevant if you are planning to move within the next 6 to 12 months and have a competitive rate you want to keep. If you need to borrow more for a higher-value property, the additional amount will be at current market rates, creating a blended rate across the two portions. Not all mortgages are portable — check your mortgage offer document to confirm eligibility before relying on this option.

### Option 5: Stay on SVR (Rarely Recommended)

Staying on the SVR requires no effort and carries no fees, but it is the most expensive option by a wide margin. The only situations where remaining on SVR genuinely makes sense are when you plan to sell within 3 to 6 months (where the cost of a new arrangement may exceed the SVR overpayment for such a short period), when your income has dropped so far that you cannot pass affordability checks elsewhere, or when you are expecting a large windfall that will allow you to pay off the mortgage entirely in the near future.

<Warning>
**Early repayment charges:** If you are still within your fixed-rate period and considering leaving early, check your ERC schedule carefully. ERCs typically range from 1% to 5% of the outstanding balance and reduce year by year. In some cases, paying the ERC and remortgaging to a significantly lower rate can still save you money overall — ask a broker to run the numbers.
</Warning>

## When to Start the Process

The optimal time to begin is 6 months before your fixed rate ends. At that point, most lenders allow you to reserve a rate, locking in current pricing while giving yourself ample time to compare deals and complete the application process. This timeline also provides a buffer for improving your credit score if needed, gathering documents, and handling any delays in valuation or legal work without falling onto the SVR.

<CollapsibleSection title="Month-by-Month Action Plan" count={7}>

- **6 months before:** Check your mortgage end date on your annual statement, review your credit report across all three agencies (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion), estimate your property value, and calculate your LTV
- **5 months before:** Contact a whole-of-market broker, get initial recommendations, and compare against your lender's product transfer rates
- **4 months before:** Work on improving your credit score (pay down cards, register to vote, dispute errors), gather documents (payslips, bank statements, ID), and obtain a mortgage illustration
- **3 months before:** Submit your full application and allow the lender to arrange a valuation
- **2 months before:** Receive your mortgage offer, begin legal work, review and sign documents, and set a completion date
- **1 month before:** Final legal checks are completed, funds are transferred, and your old mortgage is redeemed
- **On end date:** Your new deal begins seamlessly with no gap and no SVR period

</CollapsibleSection>

If you have already fallen onto the SVR, do not panic. You can leave at any time with no exit fees or penalties. Start the process immediately — every month on SVR costs you £300 to £500 in unnecessary interest on a typical mortgage.

## How to Find the Best Rates

A whole-of-market broker is the single most effective tool for securing the best remortgage deal. Unlike going direct to a lender (which limits you to one product range) or using a comparison website (which typically covers 20 to 30 lenders without advice), a broker has access to more than 90 lenders including exclusive products not available to the public. Brokers handle all paperwork, chase lenders on your behalf, and provide FCA-regulated advice tailored to your specific situation. Most earn their commission from the lender, meaning the service is free to you.

When comparing deals, focus on total cost rather than headline rate alone. A mortgage at 4.40% with a £1,999 fee can cost more over five years than a deal at 4.55% with zero fees. The formula is straightforward: multiply your monthly payment by the number of months in the fixed term, then add all upfront fees. Choose whichever deal produces the lowest total figure. Also consider overpayment flexibility — standard allowances are 10% per year without penalty, but some lenders such as Nationwide and First Direct permit unlimited overpayments on certain products, which is valuable if you expect bonuses or windfalls.

<Tip>
**Rate reservation tip:** Most lenders let you lock in a rate up to 6 months before completion. If rates fall between reservation and completion, many will let you switch to the lower rate at no cost. Ask your broker about this rate reduction policy before committing — it gives you downside protection while securing today's pricing.
</Tip>

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

The most damaging mistake is simply doing nothing. Lenders send one letter before your deal ends and are perfectly happy for you to drift onto their expensive SVR. Set a calendar reminder 6 months before expiry and treat the remortgage process with the same urgency you would give to any other decision worth thousands of pounds per year.

The second most common error is only checking your current lender's rates. Product transfer offers are convenient but frequently 0.3% to 0.5% worse than the best deals on the open market. Always get a whole-of-market comparison, even if you ultimately choose your existing lender for simplicity. Third, many homeowners chase the lowest interest rate without calculating total cost including fees, and end up paying more overall. Fourth, failing to check your credit report before applying leads to preventable rejections and worse rates. Review all three agencies at least 3 months before you plan to apply and dispute any errors immediately.

Finally, leaving the process until the last 2 to 4 weeks before your deal ends creates unnecessary risk. Remortgaging typically takes 4 to 8 weeks, so starting late almost guarantees a period on SVR while the new mortgage completes. If you find yourself in this position, arrange a product transfer as a fast backup while pursuing a full remortgage in parallel.

## Special Situations

### Your Income Has Dropped

If your earnings have decreased since you took out your mortgage, passing affordability checks for a new deal becomes harder. Consider extending your mortgage term to reduce monthly payments, exploring a product transfer (some lenders skip affordability checks for existing customers), or working with a specialist broker who knows which lenders are flexible on income changes. Adding a partner to the mortgage can also help, but never misrepresent your income — lenders verify everything and can withdraw offers if they find discrepancies.

### Self-Employed Borrowers

Self-employed income assessment is stricter than for employed applicants. Use a specialist broker who understands which lenders accept one year of accounts versus requiring two or three years. Apply after a strong financial year if timing allows, ensure all HMRC returns are filed and up to date, and consider lenders such as Atom Bank, Halifax, Santander, or Skipton Building Society, which are known for pragmatic self-employment underwriting.

### Bad Credit

Missed payments, defaults, or CCJs since your original mortgage make remortgaging more difficult but not impossible. A bad credit specialist broker (not a generalist) can identify lenders like Pepper Money, Bluestone, Vida Homeloans, or Aldermore that specialise in adverse credit. The impact of credit issues diminishes over time — missed payments have less effect after 3 years and disappear from your report after 6. Accepting a slightly higher rate now and building a clean payment history for 2 to 5 years allows you to remortgage to mainstream rates later.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<details>
<summary>How early can I start the remortgage process?</summary>

You can start up to 6 months before your current deal ends. Most lenders allow you to reserve rates that far in advance, giving you time to compare deals thoroughly and avoid falling onto the SVR. Starting early also provides a buffer for improving your credit score or gathering documentation if needed.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What happens if I do nothing when my fixed rate ends?</summary>

You automatically move onto your lender's Standard Variable Rate (SVR), which averages 8.00% in 2026 — roughly double the best available fixed rates. On a typical £200,000 mortgage, this translates to approximately £428 extra per month or £5,136 per year in unnecessary interest. Your lender is not obliged to proactively warn you about this cost.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I remortgage if I am self-employed?</summary>

Yes. Self-employed borrowers should use a specialist whole-of-market broker who knows which lenders accept one or two years of accounts. You will need SA302 tax calculations, business bank statements, and proof of ongoing contracts if you are a contractor. Lenders such as Atom Bank and Halifax are known for pragmatic self-employment underwriting.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Should I fix for 2 years or 5 years in 2026?</summary>

A 5-year fix offers the best balance between competitive rates and medium-term certainty for most borrowers. With 5-year rates currently at 4.35-4.95% vs 2-year rates at 4.65-5.25%, the 5-year deal is cheaper and provides longer protection against future rate rises. Choose 2 years only if you expect your circumstances to change significantly, such as moving house or a major income change.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Will remortgaging affect my credit score?</summary>

Each mortgage application creates a hard search, which may temporarily reduce your score by around 5-10 points. This impact typically disappears within 3-6 months. Multiple applications within a short window are usually treated as a single search by credit reference agencies, so shopping around does not multiply the impact.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Do I need a solicitor to remortgage?</summary>

Yes, all remortgages require legal work to transfer the charge between lenders. However, most remortgage deals from mainstream lenders include free legal work as part of the package, so you typically will not pay solicitor fees out of pocket. Your broker will confirm whether the deal includes a free legal service before you apply.

</details>
