## Regulatory & Market Context

### Who Regulates Business Banking in Canada?

Canadian business banking operates under a robust federal framework. The **Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI)** supervises all federally chartered banks and sets capital adequacy requirements under the *Bank Act*. The **Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC)** enforces consumer protection obligations. **FINTRAC** administers Anti-Money Laundering and Know Your Customer requirements across all institutions. For deposits, the **Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation (CDIC)** insures eligible business deposits up to **CAD 100,000 per depositor, per deposit category** at member institutions.

Key 2026 developments: Open banking Phase 1 (read-access via accredited third parties) rolls out this year under the Consumer-Driven Banking Act, enabling accounting software and fintech apps to connect directly to business bank accounts with customer consent. OSFI's fintech entry regime pilot (June 2026) will allow non-bank fintechs to hold limited deposit-taking licences, potentially expanding the competitive landscape further.

### The HSBC Canada Exit

In March 2024, RBC completed its CAD 13.5 billion acquisition of HSBC Canada. All former HSBC Canada business clients were migrated to RBC accounts. Businesses previously banking with HSBC Canada are now RBC clients with access to RBC's full business product suite.

## Security & Deposit Protection

For businesses holding significant operating reserves, the distinction between CDIC-insured and non-CDIC institutions is material. All Big Six banks (RBC, TD, Scotiabank, BMO, CIBC, National Bank), EQ Bank/Equitable Bank, and Tangerine are CDIC members. Deposits in eligible categories are insured up to CAD 100,000 per depositor, per category.

Wise Business and Airwallex are **not** CDIC members. They hold FINTRAC registration as Money Services Businesses, which provides AML/KYC compliance but not deposit insurance. Canadian businesses should not hold more than minimal operating balances at non-CDIC institutions.

## Canadian Business Banking Regulatory Context

### CDIC Protection and Regulatory Framework

Canadian business accounts benefit from Canada Deposit Insurance Corporation (CDIC) protection on eligible deposits up to $100,000 per depositor per category. This protection applies to the Big Six banks and most Schedule I and II banks — credit unions have separate provincial deposit insurance.

The Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI) regulates federally chartered banks, ensuring capital adequacy, liquidity standards, and consumer protection. For business accounts, this regulatory oversight provides confidence in the stability of the institutions holding business funds.

Open Banking developments in Canada are reshaping the business banking landscape. Following the federal government's 2023 Open Banking commitment, businesses can increasingly expect standardised API access to bank data, enabling better accounting software integration and financial management tools. This positions Canadian business banking to catch up with the UK and EU open banking ecosystems in the coming years.
