Choosing between Wealthsimple and Questrade is one of the most common decisions facing Canadian investors. Both are leading platforms, but they serve fundamentally different investor types and strategies.

Wealthsimple excels as a robo-advisor with automated, professionally managed portfolios perfect for hands-off investors. Questrade shines as a self-directed platform offering granular control, advanced tools, and rock-bottom fees for active traders.

This comprehensive comparison breaks down fees, features, account types, investment options, and user experience to help you choose the platform that best fits your investing style and financial goals.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<details>
<summary>Which is cheaper: Wealthsimple or Questrade?</summary>

Questrade is cheaper for most investors. DIY investors pay $0 management fee at Questrade versus 0.40%–0.50% at Wealthsimple Invest. Questrade's robo-advisor (Portfolio IQ) charges 0.20%–0.25% versus Wealthsimple's 0.40%–0.50%. The only exception is active stock trading, where Wealthsimple Trade's $0 commissions beat Questrade's $4.95–$9.95 per trade. On a $100,000 portfolio, Questrade saves approximately $250–$450 per year.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Is Wealthsimple good for beginners?</summary>

Yes, Wealthsimple is designed specifically for beginners. It features a 5-minute onboarding process, fully automated portfolio management, and an intuitive interface that requires no prior investment knowledge. Questrade's self-directed platform requires you to research, select, and manage your own investments — better suited to investors who already understand asset allocation and ETF selection.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Which platform supports TFSA and RRSP accounts?</summary>

Both Wealthsimple and Questrade support TFSA, RRSP, FHSA, and non-registered accounts. For TFSAs, Questrade has the edge: $0 management fee means all tax-free growth stays in your account rather than going to fees. For RRSPs, Questrade allows you to hold US-listed ETFs (VTI, VXUS) to avoid the 15% foreign withholding tax — a significant advantage that Wealthsimple does not offer in its robo-advisor product.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Does Questrade have a robo-advisor like Wealthsimple?</summary>

Yes. Questrade Portfolio IQ is a robo-advisor that charges 0.20%–0.25% annually, roughly half the 0.40%–0.50% that Wealthsimple Invest charges. Both offer automated portfolio management, ETF-based diversification, and automatic rebalancing. Questrade Portfolio IQ is cheaper but less polished — Wealthsimple's interface and user experience remain superior, especially for first-time investors.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I use both Wealthsimple and Questrade at the same time?</summary>

Yes, and many Canadian investors do. A common approach is to use Wealthsimple Invest for a hands-off RRSP, Questrade self-directed for a TFSA with full control and lower fees, and Wealthsimple Trade for commission-free Canadian stock trading. There are no restrictions on holding accounts at multiple CIRO-registered platforms simultaneously.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How do I switch from Wealthsimple to Questrade (or vice versa)?</summary>

Both platforms support account transfers. You initiate the transfer at the receiving platform (not the one you are leaving), and the process typically takes 5–10 business days for registered accounts (TFSA, RRSP). Questrade covers transfer fees up to $150 per account. Note that in-kind transfers preserve your existing holdings, while cash transfers liquidate them first — for registered accounts, an in-kind transfer is usually preferred to avoid triggering ACB calculations.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Which platform is safer for my money?</summary>

Both platforms are equally safe. Wealthsimple and Questrade are both CIRO-registered (Canada's national self-regulatory organization) and members of the Canadian Investor Protection Fund (CIPF), which provides up to $1 million in coverage if a member firm becomes insolvent. Your assets are held in segregated accounts, meaning they are legally separate from the company's own assets and protected even if the platform fails.

</details>
