## Is It Safe to Use AI for Financial Planning in Canada?

Before diving into specific tools, it is worth addressing the most common concern among Canadian users. AI finance tools operating in Canada must comply with the *Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act* (PIPEDA), and in Quebec, the stricter *Law 25* applies, with fines up to CAD 25 million for violations.

Most reputable platforms use 256-bit encryption, two-factor authentication, and do not use your personal data to train their AI models. However, Canadian users should be aware of three key risks: first, many US-built tools store data on American servers, which may not satisfy Law 25's data residency requirements; second, AI-generated financial advice can contain errors, particularly around Canadian tax rules (TFSA contribution limits, GST/HST filing, CRA requirements); and third, OSFI Guideline E-23, finalised in September 2025, will require all federally regulated financial institutions to validate and govern AI models by May 2027 — signalling that bank-embedded AI features face stricter oversight.

**Bottom line:** Use AI tools for efficiency and insight, but verify material financial decisions with a qualified Canadian CPA or licensed advisor.

## How Canadian Regulations Affect AI Finance Tools

Canada's regulatory framework for AI in finance is evolving rapidly. Three developments in 2025–2026 are reshaping the landscape.

**OSFI Guideline E-23** (finalised September 2025, effective May 2027) requires all federally regulated financial institutions — banks, insurance companies, trust companies — to validate, monitor, and govern AI models throughout their lifecycle under EDGE principles (Explainability, Data management, Governance, Ethics). For consumers, this means bank-embedded AI features will face stricter oversight than standalone fintech apps like Monarch Money or Bloom.

**The Consumer-Driven Banking Act** passed in 2024 and Phase 1 (read-only data sharing) launches in early 2026 under Bank of Canada supervision. This open banking framework will eventually allow AI tools to access your banking data via secure API, eliminating the unreliable screen-scraping that currently causes CIBC and BMO connectivity failures. Expect a significant improvement in third-party app reliability by 2027.

**AIDA (Bill C-27)** — Canada's proposed AI-specific legislation — died when the federal election was called in April 2025. A voluntary Code of Conduct for responsible AI development remains in effect, and binding legislation is expected to be reintroduced in the new parliament.

## Canadian Regulatory Context for AI in Finance

### OSFI and FINTRAC Compliance Requirements

Canadian financial AI tools must operate within a specific regulatory environment. The Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI) has issued guidance on the use of technology and AI in federally regulated financial institutions, emphasising model risk management and explainability.

FINTRAC (Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada) reporting obligations apply when AI tools process transactions that may trigger AML/ATF reporting requirements. AI-assisted compliance tools must be configured to generate compliant FINTRAC reports — a capability not all international tools support natively.

Provincial securities regulators (OSC, AMF, BCSC) have issued guidance on the use of AI in investment advice, aligning broadly with international standards on explainability and audit trails. AI tools used by registered advisors must support documentation requirements that satisfy provincial regulatory obligations.
