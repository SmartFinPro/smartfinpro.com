## Common Mistakes and How Wealthsimple Tax Catches Them

Canadian tax returns are full of easily missed deductions and commonly made errors. Here is how Wealthsimple Tax's optimization engine addresses the most frequent mistakes.

**1. Forgetting to claim unused RRSP contribution room:** Many Canadians have accumulated unused RRSP room from previous years and do not realize they can carry it forward indefinitely. Wealthsimple Tax pulls your current RRSP deduction limit from your Notice of Assessment via CRA Auto-Fill and prominently displays how much you can still contribute before the March deadline to claim it on your current-year return.

**2. Missing the medical expense threshold:** The medical expense tax credit only applies to amounts exceeding 3% of your net income (or C$2,635 in 2025, whichever is less). Many Canadians discard their medical receipts thinking the amount is too small, not realizing that expenses paid by either spouse across a 12-month period ending in the tax year can be combined to clear the threshold. Wealthsimple Tax's optimization engine automatically tests whether including your spouse's medical expenses on one return increases your combined credit.

**3. Not claiming home office expenses:** Self-employed Canadians and employees who worked from home in 2025 (with employer certification) can claim home office expenses. The flat-rate method (C$2/day) requires minimal documentation, while the detailed method captures actual utility, internet, and rent proportions. Wealthsimple Tax offers both methods and shows which generates a larger deduction for your specific situation.

**4. Overlooking charitable donation bundling:** The federal donation tax credit is 15% on the first C$200 donated and 29% (or higher in some provinces) on amounts above C$200. If you donated C$150 each year for two years, claiming C$300 in one year gets you the higher rate on C$100 of donations versus claiming C$150 each year at the lower rate. Wealthsimple Tax flags this opportunity automatically.

**5. Missing student loan interest deductions:** Interest paid on federal and provincial student loans (but not private bank loans) is eligible for a federal non-refundable tax credit. If your income was too low to benefit in a previous year, you can carry the deduction forward for up to five years. Wealthsimple Tax tracks this automatically and applies it in the most tax-efficient year.

## Tips for Getting the Maximum Refund

Every percentage point of additional refund represents real money. These evidence-based strategies consistently improve Canadian tax outcomes when executed before the filing deadline.

**1. Make your RRSP contribution before March 2, 2026.** The RRSP contribution deadline for the 2025 tax year is 60 days after December 31, 2025, which falls on March 1, 2026 (or March 2 in a non-leap year). Contributions made by this date are deductible on your 2025 return. At a 33% marginal rate, a C$10,000 RRSP contribution generates a C$3,300 refund directly. Use Wealthsimple Tax's RRSP calculator to find your exact break-even and optimal contribution amount.

**2. Maximize your FHSA deduction if you are a first-time buyer.** The First Home Savings Account allows contributions of C$8,000 per year (C$16,000 in year one with carry-forward) that are fully deductible from income — exactly like an RRSP. Unlike an RRSP, there is no repayment required when you withdraw to buy your first home. If you contributed to an FHSA in 2025, Wealthsimple Tax automatically applies the deduction. If you have not yet opened an FHSA and qualify as a first-time buyer, opening one and contributing before December 31 each year is one of the most valuable tax moves available to Canadians under 71 who have not owned a home in the previous four years.

**3. Claim every eligible tuition credit and carry-forward unused amounts.** Post-secondary students accumulate tuition credits via T2202 slips. If your income was too low to use the credit in the year it was earned, you can carry it forward indefinitely or transfer up to C$5,000 to a parent or grandparent. Wealthsimple Tax automatically carries forward unused tuition and recommends the optimal transfer strategy.

**4. Track all medical expenses for both spouses across a 12-month window.** The CRA allows you to claim medical expenses for any 12-month period ending in the tax year (not just the calendar year), and you can claim expenses for your spouse and dependants. Combining both spouses' expenses on the higher-income earner's return often maximizes the credit because the 3% income threshold is lower relative to the total medical expenses claimed. Wealthsimple Tax tests this automatically.

**5. Claim your home office if you are fully remote.** Employees who worked from home for more than 50% of their time for at least four consecutive weeks and have a signed T2200 from their employer can deduct home office expenses. The detailed method typically generates a larger deduction than the flat rate for anyone paying rent in a major Canadian city. Collect your monthly rent, internet, and utility bills throughout the year so you have documentation at filing time.
