## How Canadian Mortgages Work

A Canadian mortgage has two distinct time horizons that are often confused by first-time buyers. The **amortisation period** is the total length of the loan, typically 25 years for insured mortgages and up to 30 years for uninsured mortgages. The **term** is the period during which your agreed rate and conditions apply, typically ranging from 1 to 5 years, after which you must renew. The majority of Canadian mortgages have 5-year fixed terms, though 3-year fixed and variable-rate products have gained significant popularity as rates declined through 2024 and 2025. Understanding the distinction between amortisation and term is critical because when your term expires, you renegotiate your rate at current market conditions, meaning a 25-year mortgage is actually a series of shorter-term rate agreements.

Payment frequency options affect the total interest paid over the life of the mortgage more than most borrowers realise. You can choose from monthly, semi-monthly, bi-weekly, weekly, and accelerated bi-weekly or weekly payment schedules. Accelerated bi-weekly payments are particularly powerful: by making the equivalent of 26 half-payments per year (instead of 24 under a standard bi-weekly schedule), you effectively make one extra full monthly payment annually. On a CAD $500,000 mortgage at 4.5% over 25 years, switching to accelerated bi-weekly payments can save tens of thousands of dollars in interest and reduce the amortisation period by several years.

### Fixed vs. Variable Rate

A **fixed-rate mortgage** locks your interest rate and payment for the full term. In 2026's environment of stable-to-declining rates, fixed rates provide certainty but forgo the potential benefit if rates decline further. A **variable-rate mortgage (VRM)** or **adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM)** moves with the Bank of Canada's prime rate. In a VRM, your payment stays constant but the proportion going to interest versus principal shifts with each rate change. In an ARM, your actual payment amount changes with each Bank of Canada rate decision. With the BoC at 2.25% and limited further cut expectations, variable rates are competitive against 5-year fixed rates for the first time since 2022, making this a genuinely difficult decision for borrowers rather than the straightforward choice it was in 2023.

### Open vs. Closed Mortgages

A **closed mortgage** has prepayment penalty provisions if you break it before the term ends. Most Canadian mortgages are closed, and the penalty structure matters enormously. Fixed-rate closed mortgages at the major banks typically use the Interest Rate Differential (IRD) method to calculate penalties, which can result in charges of tens of thousands of dollars if you break mid-term when rates have dropped. A **three months' interest** penalty, which is standard for variable-rate closed mortgages and some monoline fixed-rate products, is far less punitive. An **open mortgage** allows full repayment at any time without penalty, which is useful for buyers expecting to sell within the term. However, open mortgages carry interest rates typically 1-2% above equivalent closed rates, making them significantly more expensive for the certainty they provide.

<Tip>
**Payment strategy:** Choose accelerated bi-weekly payments from the start. The savings compound significantly over the amortisation period, and most borrowers never notice the difference in their cash flow. Combined with using your annual prepayment privilege (typically 10-20% of the original principal), you can cut years off your mortgage and save substantial interest.
</Tip>

## OSFI Stress Test and CMHC Insurance Rules

The Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI) and the Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) together define the regulatory framework that every Canadian mortgage borrower must navigate. Understanding these rules is not optional: they determine how much you can borrow, what insurance you need, and what rates you qualify for, regardless of your actual income or savings.

### The OSFI B-20 Mortgage Stress Test

OSFI's Guideline B-20 requires all federally regulated lenders to qualify mortgage applicants at the **greater of** the contract (offered) rate **plus 2%**, or **5.25%**. With insured 5-year fixed rates around 4.5-5.0% in early 2026, the effective stress test rate is 6.5-7.0% for most borrowers. This means your mortgage qualifying income must be sufficient to carry payments at that higher rate, even though you will actually pay the much lower contract rate. The stress test applies to all new mortgages, renewals with a new lender, and refinances. It does not apply to renewals with your existing lender, though some lenders voluntarily apply it.

The practical impact of the stress test is substantial. A household earning CAD $100,000 annually with no other debts might qualify for a mortgage of roughly CAD $420,000-$450,000 under the stress test, whereas without the stress test the same household could theoretically borrow CAD $520,000-$560,000 based on the actual contract rate. The stress test therefore reduces purchasing power by approximately 15-20%, which is by design. OSFI implemented B-20 to prevent borrowers from overextending in a low-rate environment and then facing payment shock when rates rise at renewal.

### CMHC Mortgage Insurance

CMHC provides mortgage default insurance for Canadian buyers with less than 20% down payment. This is not optional: if your down payment is below 20%, your lender is required by law to insure the mortgage. The key rules as of 2026 reflect significant changes made in late 2024:

| Rule | Current (2026) | Previous |
|------|---------------|----------|
| **Maximum insured purchase price** | CAD $1.5 million | CAD $1 million (pre-Dec 15, 2024) |
| **Minimum down payment** | 5% on first $500K; 10% on $500K-$1.5M | 5% on first $500K; 10% on $500K-$1M |
| **Maximum amortisation (insured)** | 30 years (first-time buyers & new builds) | 25 years (pre-Aug 1, 2024) |
| **CMHC premium range** | 0.60%-4.00% of mortgage amount | Same |

The December 2024 increase in the insured purchase limit from CAD $1 million to CAD $1.5 million was a landmark change for Canadian housing. It significantly expanded CMHC access in high-priced markets like Vancouver and Toronto, where average home prices routinely exceed $1 million. Buyers in those cities can now access insured mortgage rates with less than 20% down on homes up to $1.5 million, which was previously impossible. The CMHC premium itself ranges from 0.60% to 4.00% of the insured mortgage amount and is added to the mortgage balance rather than paid upfront.

<Warning>
**Stress test impact on purchasing power:** With 5-year fixed insured rates around 4.5-5.0%, the effective stress test rate of 6.5-7.0% significantly reduces how much you can borrow. Calculate your qualifying amount at the stress test rate, not the advertised rate, before house hunting. Many buyers are surprised to find they qualify for 15-20% less than they expected.
</Warning>

## First-Time Homebuyer Programs

Canada offers several programs specifically designed to help first-time buyers enter the housing market. The most powerful of these is the First Home Savings Account, but the RRSP Home Buyers' Plan and various provincial programs also provide meaningful support. Understanding how to combine these programs is the key to maximising your financial advantage as a first-time buyer in 2026.

### FHSA (First Home Savings Account)

The **First Home Savings Account** was introduced in 2023 and represents the single most tax-efficient strategy for Canadian first-time buyers saving a down payment. The FHSA allows contributions of up to CAD $8,000 per year with a lifetime maximum of CAD $40,000. Contributions are tax-deductible, meaning they reduce your taxable income in the year of contribution, just like RRSP contributions. Withdrawals for a qualifying first home purchase are completely tax-free, similar to a TFSA. This combination of tax-deductible contributions and tax-free withdrawals is unique in Canadian tax law and effectively gives first-time buyers a double tax benefit that no other registered account provides.

For a first-time buyer in a 30% marginal tax bracket contributing the maximum CAD $8,000 per year, the FHSA generates an immediate annual tax refund of CAD $2,400. Over five years of maximum contributions, that is CAD $12,000 in tax savings on top of the CAD $40,000 in principal saved, plus any investment growth inside the account (also tax-free on withdrawal). For Canadian first-time buyers who have not yet opened an FHSA, doing so immediately and maximising contributions is the highest-priority financial action available.

### HBP (Home Buyers' Plan)

The **Home Buyers' Plan** allows first-time buyers to withdraw up to CAD $60,000 from their RRSP tax-free for a home purchase. This limit was increased from CAD $35,000 effective April 2024, nearly doubling the accessible amount. The withdrawal must be repaid to the RRSP over 15 years, with repayments beginning the second year after the withdrawal. If you miss a scheduled repayment, the amount is added to your taxable income for that year. Combined with the FHSA, a first-time buyer can potentially access CAD $100,000 in tax-sheltered savings for a down payment (CAD $40,000 FHSA plus CAD $60,000 HBP), making the combination a powerful tool for entering expensive housing markets.

### First-Time Home Buyer Incentive (Ended)

The federal **First-Time Home Buyer Incentive (FTHBI)**, which was a shared equity program, ended for new applications in early 2024. First-time buyers should now focus exclusively on the FHSA and HBP combination for down payment savings. The FTHBI's closure simplifies the landscape: the two remaining federal programs are straightforward and do not require giving up equity in your home.

### Provincial Programs

Several provinces offer additional first-time buyer assistance that stacks on top of the federal FHSA and HBP programs:

- **Ontario:** Land Transfer Tax rebate up to CAD $4,000 for first-time buyers
- **British Columbia:** Property Transfer Tax exemption for homes under CAD $835,000 for first-time buyers
- **Alberta:** No provincial land transfer tax at all, which is a significant cost saving compared to Ontario and British Columbia regardless of buyer status

## Mortgage Rate Environment: Early 2026

The Bank of Canada's policy rate of 2.25% in early 2026 has set the prime rate at approximately 4.45% (prime equals the BoC rate plus approximately 2.2%). This represents a dramatic shift from the 5.0% peak reached in mid-2023, and the cumulative rate cuts have fundamentally changed the mortgage rate landscape. Both fixed and variable rates have come down substantially, but the relationship between them has shifted in ways that affect borrower strategy.

The current rate ranges across major mortgage products paint a clear picture of where the market stands:

| Product | Rate Range (Early 2026) | Notes |
|---------|------------------------|-------|
| **5-year fixed insured** | 4.25%-4.75% | Best broker rates; bank posted rates higher |
| **5-year fixed uninsured** | 4.50%-5.00% | For purchases over $1.5M or renewals |
| **Variable rate insured** | 3.70%-3.95% | Prime minus 0.50-0.75% |
| **3-year fixed** | 4.10%-4.60% | Gaining popularity as a middle ground |
| **Prime rate** | ~4.45% | BoC 2.25% + ~2.2% |

Variable rates have returned to competitive territory relative to fixed rates for the first time since 2022. Insured variable rates in the 3.70-3.95% range are meaningfully below the 5-year fixed insured range of 4.25-4.75%, creating a genuine rate advantage of 0.30-1.05% for variable-rate borrowers. However, this advantage comes with the risk that the Bank of Canada could reverse course if inflation resurges, though current economic projections suggest the BoC is more likely to hold steady or cut modestly rather than raise rates in 2026.

The practical difference between these rates is significant on a typical Canadian mortgage. On a CAD $500,000 mortgage amortised over 25 years, the difference between a 4.50% fixed rate and a 3.80% variable rate amounts to approximately CAD $200 per month or CAD $2,400 per year. Over a 5-year term, that is a potential saving of CAD $12,000, though the variable-rate borrower accepts the risk that rates could move against them during the term.

<Tip>
**Rate negotiation:** Never accept the first rate your bank offers. Big bank posted rates are typically 0.25-0.50% above the best available rates. Mention competing offers from monoline lenders or rate comparison platforms, and ask explicitly for a rate match or discount. Existing bank customers with mortgages, investments, or significant deposits have additional negotiating leverage.
</Tip>

## How to Find the Best Mortgage Rate in Canada

The Canadian mortgage market has three distinct channels for obtaining a mortgage, each with different rate structures, service models, and trade-offs. Understanding which channel to use, and when to use multiple channels simultaneously, can save you thousands of dollars over the life of your mortgage.

### Big Banks vs. Monoline Lenders vs. Mortgage Brokers

**Big Banks (RBC, TD, BMO, Scotiabank, CIBC, National Bank)** offer posted rates that are generally higher than the best market rates, but with the convenience of your existing banking relationship, integrated mortgage and banking products, and branch access for in-person service. Negotiating below the posted rate is standard practice at every major bank. If your banker does not offer a discount below the posted rate on first ask, you should treat that as the starting point of a negotiation rather than a final answer.

**Monoline lenders (MCAP, First National, RMG, Merix Financial, Lendwise)** focus exclusively on mortgages and do not offer chequing accounts, savings products, or credit cards. Because they have lower overhead and compete purely on mortgage pricing, they often offer rates meaningfully below big bank posted rates. Monoline lenders work primarily through mortgage brokers and generally require a broker introduction. The trade-off is that you will not have a branch to visit, and your mortgage servicing experience will be entirely digital or phone-based.

**Mortgage brokers**, licensed under provincial mortgage broker legislation, have access to rates from multiple lenders including monolines, credit unions, and banks. A qualified mortgage broker can typically find 0.25-0.50% lower rates than a bank's front-book rate. On a CAD $500,000 mortgage, that difference saves CAD $1,250-$2,500 per year in interest. Brokers are paid by the lender (not by you) on most standard mortgage transactions, so there is generally no direct cost to the borrower for using a broker.

**Rate comparison platforms (Ratehub, Nesto, Mortgage Sandbox)** aggregate publicly available rates from multiple lenders and allow direct mortgage origination at competitive rates. Both [Ratehub](/ca/housing/ratehub-review) and Nesto operate as licensed mortgage brokers with access to the full lender spectrum. These platforms are particularly useful for benchmarking: even if you ultimately get your mortgage through your bank, knowing the best available rate from a comparison platform gives you powerful leverage in negotiations.

### Key Comparison Factors Beyond the Rate

The advertised interest rate is only one component of the total mortgage cost, and borrowers who focus exclusively on rate often miss more expensive features buried in the contract terms.

| Factor | Why It Matters | What to Look For |
|--------|---------------|------------------|
| **APR** | Includes fees that can make a low-rate mortgage more expensive | Compare APR across lenders, not just the interest rate |
| **Prepayment privileges** | Ability to pay 10-20% of original mortgage annually without penalty | Higher privilege percentage = faster paydown option |
| **Portability** | Take your mortgage to a new property if you move within the term | Avoids expensive penalties if you sell and buy |
| **Penalty calculation** | IRD vs. 3 months' interest dramatically affects break costs | Big banks typically use IRD, which is significantly more expensive |
| **Renewal flexibility** | Ability to switch lenders at renewal without penalty | Some lenders make switching difficult with collateral charges |

The penalty calculation method deserves special attention. If you break a fixed-rate mortgage at a big bank that uses the IRD method, the penalty can be CAD $10,000-$20,000 or more on a CAD $500,000 mortgage, depending on how much rates have dropped since you signed. The same mortgage at a monoline lender using the 3 months' interest method might carry a penalty of only CAD $3,000-$4,000. This difference alone can outweigh a slightly lower rate at the bank.

## Fixed vs. Variable: The 2026 Decision Framework

The fixed-versus-variable decision in 2026 is genuinely difficult because both options are competitive. In 2023, when the BoC rate was 5.0% and fixed rates were in the 5-6% range, the decision was largely academic. In 2026, with variable rates at 3.70-3.95% and 5-year fixed rates at 4.25-4.75%, the choice involves real trade-offs that depend on your personal financial situation and risk tolerance.

Borrowers with tight budgets, fixed incomes, or low tolerance for payment uncertainty should favour fixed rates. The certainty of knowing your exact payment amount for the full 5-year term has genuine value, particularly for first-time buyers who are already stretching to enter the market. The cost of that certainty in 2026 is the 0.30-1.05% rate premium over variable rates, which on a CAD $500,000 mortgage translates to roughly CAD $100-$300 per month.

Borrowers who can absorb potential payment increases and who believe the Bank of Canada may cut further could benefit substantially from variable rates. If the BoC holds at 2.25% or cuts to 2.00% over the next two years, a variable-rate borrower will have saved meaningfully compared to their fixed-rate counterpart. However, if inflation resurges and the BoC raises rates, the variable-rate borrower could end up paying more than the fixed-rate alternative. The 3-year fixed term has emerged as a popular middle ground, offering a shorter rate commitment at rates slightly below the 5-year fixed while still providing certainty during the most uncertain period of the rate cycle.

Alberta buyers benefit from the absence of provincial land transfer tax, which saves this family thousands of dollars compared to an equivalent purchase in Ontario or British Columbia. The uninsured rate of 4.65% is slightly higher than insured rates, which is the standard premium for conventional mortgages in Canada.

### Scenario 3: Vancouver Investor Renewing (CAD $1.2M Property)

A property investor in Vancouver renewing a CAD $900,000 mortgage on a CAD $1.2 million investment property. They are renewing with a new lender to access better rates.

| Item | Amount |

## Key Glossary for Canadian Mortgage Borrowers

Understanding the terminology is essential for navigating conversations with brokers, lenders, and lawyers during the mortgage process. These are the terms every Canadian buyer and homeowner should know.

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Amortisation** | Total repayment period — up to 25 years insured (30 years for first-time buyers/new builds), up to 30 years uninsured |
| **Term** | Rate-lock period, typically 1-5 years; not the same as amortisation |
| **Stress test** | OSFI B-20 qualification rate — contract rate plus 2%, minimum 5.25% |
| **CMHC insurance** | Mandatory default insurance for less than 20% down payment, up to $1.5M purchase price |
| **Prime rate** | Bank's base lending rate, approximately BoC rate plus 2.2%; currently ~4.45% |
| **IRD penalty** | Interest Rate Differential penalty for breaking a fixed mortgage early; can be very expensive at major banks |
| **Open mortgage** | Repayable anytime without penalty; higher interest rate (typically 1-2% above closed) |
| **Closed mortgage** | Penalty applies to early repayment; lower rate; most Canadian mortgages are closed |
| **Monoline lender** | Mortgage-only lender (no banking products); typically offers lower rates via brokers |
| **FHSA** | First Home Savings Account — CAD $8,000/year, $40,000 lifetime, tax-deductible in, tax-free out |
| **HBP** | Home Buyers' Plan — withdraw up to CAD $60,000 from RRSP tax-free for first home; repay over 15 years |

<Warning>
**IRD penalty caution:** Before signing any fixed-rate mortgage, ask your lender to explain exactly how they calculate the IRD penalty and request a sample calculation. The difference between a 3 months' interest penalty (common at monoline lenders) and a full IRD penalty (common at big banks) can be tens of thousands of dollars on a CAD $500,000+ mortgage. This single contract term can be more important than the rate itself.
</Warning>
