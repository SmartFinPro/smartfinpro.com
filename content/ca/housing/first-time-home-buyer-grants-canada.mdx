## Maximizing Government Support for Your First Home Purchase

Buying your first home in Canada has never been more expensive—but government programs have also never been more generous. Between federal and provincial incentives, first-time buyers can access over $100,000 in tax-advantaged savings, grants, rebates, and shared equity support.

The challenge is navigating the complex landscape of overlapping programs, each with different eligibility requirements, contribution limits, and repayment rules. This comprehensive guide breaks down every federal and provincial program available in 2026, helping you maximize support for your first home purchase.

For a household buying a $600,000 home in Toronto, strategically combining federal programs (FHSA + Home Buyers' Plan + First-Time Home Buyer Incentive + Land Transfer Tax Rebate) can save $35,000-50,000 in upfront costs and taxes. That's the difference between scrambling for 5% down ($30,000) and comfortably putting down 10% ($60,000) with emergency savings intact.

## Federal First-Time Home Buyer Programs

### 1. First Home Savings Account (FHSA) — The Most Powerful Tool

The FHSA, introduced in 2023, is Canada's newest and most generous registered account for first-time buyers. It combines the best features of TFSAs (tax-free growth) and RRSPs (tax-deductible contributions).

**Contribution Limits (2026):**
- Annual limit: $8,000
- Lifetime limit: $40,000
- Unused annual room does NOT carry forward (use it or lose it)

**Tax Benefits:**
- **Contributions are tax-deductible** (like RRSP)—immediate tax refund based on marginal rate
- **Growth is tax-free** (like TFSA)—no tax on dividends, interest, or capital gains
- **Withdrawals are tax-free** when used for qualifying first home purchase

**Example Tax Savings:**

Income: $80,000 (38% marginal rate in Ontario)
- Contribute $8,000 → Tax refund: $3,040
- Repeat for 5 years = $40,000 contributed, $15,200 in refunds
- Investment grows to $48,000 (7% annual return)
- Withdraw $48,000 tax-free for down payment
- **Total benefit: $15,200 (refunds) + $8,000 (tax-free growth) = $23,200**

**Eligibility:**
- Age 18+ and Canadian resident
- First-time home buyer (no home ownership in year of opening account or previous 4 calendar years)
- No income requirements

**How It Works:**
1. Open FHSA at bank, credit union, or robo-advisor (Wealthsimple, Questrade, etc.)
2. Contribute up to $8,000/year (can defer deduction to higher-income year)
3. Invest contributions (growth portfolios for 5+ year timeline, conservative for 1-2 years)
4. Withdraw funds tax-free when purchasing qualifying home
5. Must make qualifying withdrawal within 15 years of opening or by age 71

**Qualifying Home Purchase:**
- Property located in Canada
- You intend to occupy as principal residence within one year
- Written purchase agreement signed

**What Happens If You Don't Buy?**
- Transfer entire balance to RRSP or RRIF tax-free (no RRSP room needed)
- No penalty for not buying—your savings aren't lost
- Must close account by end of year you turn 71 or 15th anniversary, whichever is earlier

**FHSA Strategy:**
1. **Open immediately** even if buying in 5+ years (you lose $8,000 annual room each year you wait)
2. **Maximize contributions** every year to reach $40,000 in 5 years
3. **Invest aggressively** if buying in 5+ years (80-90% equity ETFs)
4. **Invest conservatively** if buying in 1-2 years (GICs, high-interest savings, bond ETFs)
5. **Defer deduction** if you expect significantly higher income next year

**Where to Open FHSA:**
- **Wealthsimple Invest:** Automated portfolios, $0 minimum, 0.50% fee
- **Questrade:** Self-directed or robo (Portfolio IQ), $1,000 minimum
- **TD, RBC, BMO, Scotiabank:** Traditional banks, GIC and mutual fund options
- **EQ Bank:** High-interest savings FHSA (4.00% in 2026)—best for 1-2 year timeline

**Common Mistakes:**
- Not opening FHSA early (losing annual contribution room permanently)
- Over-contributing (1% monthly penalty on excess)
- Withdrawing for non-qualifying purchase (becomes taxable income)
- Letting account expire at 15 years without transferring to RRSP

### 2. Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) — Borrow from Your RRSP

The HBP allows first-time buyers to withdraw up to $60,000 from RRSPs tax-free to purchase a home. Unlike FHSA, the HBP is a loan from yourself that must be repaid.

**2026 Limit:** $60,000 per person ($120,000 for couples)

**Key Features:**
- Withdraw up to $60,000 from RRSP without paying tax
- Must repay over 15 years (starting second year after withdrawal)
- Minimum annual repayment: 1/15 of amount withdrawn
- Missed repayments are added to taxable income
- Can combine with FHSA ($40,000 + $60,000 = $100,000 per person)

**Eligibility:**
- First-time home buyer (no home ownership in past 4 years)
- Must have written purchase agreement or building contract
- Must intend to occupy as principal residence within one year
- RRSP funds must be on deposit for at least 90 days before withdrawal

**Example:**

**Couple buying $700,000 home:**
- Each withdraws $60,000 from RRSP via HBP = $120,000
- Each maxed FHSA = $80,000
- Total tax-advantaged down payment: $200,000 (28.5%)
- Remaining $500,000 financed with mortgage (avoid CMHC insurance at 20%+)

**Repayment Rules:**
- First repayment due in second year after withdrawal
- Annual minimum: Total withdrawn ÷ 15
- Example: Withdrew $60,000 → Repay $4,000/year minimum
- Contribute repayment to RRSP and designate as HBP repayment on tax return
- Missed repayment is added to income and taxed

**Strategic Considerations:**

**Pros:**
- Access large sum (up to $60,000) for down payment
- No immediate tax on withdrawal
- Can combine with FHSA for $100,000 total

**Cons:**
- Must repay over 15 years (reduces future RRSP room)
- Lose tax-deferred growth on withdrawn funds
- Missed repayments trigger income tax
- Reduces retirement savings if not repaid diligently

**HBP vs FHSA:**

| Feature | FHSA | Home Buyers' Plan (HBP) |
|---------|------|----------------------|
| Contribution limit | $40,000 lifetime | $60,000 withdrawal |
| Tax on contribution | Deductible (refund) | Deductible when contributed to RRSP |
| Tax on withdrawal | None | None (but must repay) |
| Repayment required | No | Yes (15 years) |
| Best for | First-time buyers 3-5+ years out | Immediate buyers with existing RRSP savings |

**Optimal Strategy:**
- Use FHSA as primary tool (no repayment required)
- Use HBP as supplementary if you need larger down payment
- Ensure you can afford HBP repayment ($4,000/year on $60,000 withdrawal)

**Special Case: Repeat Use of HBP**

You can use HBP again if:
- You've repaid the entire previous HBP balance
- You haven't owned a home in the 4 years before new withdrawal
- Example: Used HBP in 2010, repaid by 2025, separated and now buying new home in 2026 (eligible)

### 3. First-Time Home Buyer Incentive (Shared Equity Program)

The federal government's shared equity program provides 5-10% of home price as interest-free loan (no ongoing payments), repaid when you sell or after 25 years.

**2026 Limits:**
- 5% for resale homes
- 10% for new builds
- Maximum home price: $722,000 (4× qualifying income in high-cost markets: Toronto, Vancouver, Victoria)

**How It Works:**
1. Government provides 5-10% of purchase price as second mortgage
2. No monthly payments required
3. No interest charged
4. Repaid when you sell, refinance, or after 25 years
5. Repayment amount based on property value at time of repayment

**Example:**

Purchase price: $500,000 (new build)
- Down payment (5%): $25,000
- First-Time Home Buyer Incentive (10%): $50,000
- Mortgage: $425,000 (instead of $475,000)

**Monthly savings:**
- Original mortgage payment (5.5%, 25yr): $2,915
- With incentive: $2,609
- **Savings: $306/month**

**Repayment after 10 years:**
- Home value: $700,000
- Incentive repayment: 10% × $700,000 = $70,000 (you pay market appreciation)

**Eligibility:**
- Total household income ≤ $120,000 ($150,000 in Toronto, Vancouver, Victoria)
- Minimum 5% down payment from own funds (cannot use incentive as down payment)
- First-time buyer OR haven't owned home in past 4 years
- Mortgage + incentive ≤ 4× household income (4.5× in high-cost markets)

**Pros:**
- Reduces monthly mortgage payments (smaller principal)
- No ongoing interest or payments
- Helps buyers qualify for mortgage (lower debt servicing ratio)

**Cons:**
- Must repay based on market value (not original amount)—lose upside if home appreciates
- Income limit ($120,000-150,000) excludes higher earners
- Only available through approved lenders
- Claws back appreciation (if home gains 40%, you repay 40% more)

**Is It Worth It?**

**If home appreciates modestly (3%/year):**
- Borrowed: $50,000
- Repay after 10 years: $67,195
- Effective interest rate: ~2.9%/year (reasonable)

**If home appreciates significantly (6%/year):**
- Borrowed: $50,000
- Repay after 10 years: $89,542
- Effective interest rate: ~6.0%/year (expensive)

**Verdict:** Best for buyers who need help qualifying but expect modest appreciation. Avoid if you're in hot markets with high appreciation potential (you'll pay significant premiums on repayment).

### 4. Home Buyers' Tax Credit (HBTC)

Non-refundable federal tax credit worth $1,500 (15% × $10,000) for first-time buyers.

**Amount:** $10,000 tax credit = $1,500 federal tax reduction

**Eligibility:**
- You or your spouse bought a qualifying home
- Neither owned a home in year of purchase or previous 4 years
- Home located in Canada
- You intend to occupy as principal residence within one year

**How to Claim:**
- Line 31270 on federal tax return
- Provide purchase agreement details
- Credit can be split with spouse/common-law partner

**Value Analysis:**
$1,500 is modest but requires zero effort beyond checking a box on your tax return. Always claim it.

### 5. GST/HST New Housing Rebate

Buyers of new or substantially renovated homes can claim rebate on GST/HST paid.

**Federal GST Rebate (5%):**
- Maximum rebate: $6,300 (for homes under $350,000)
- Partial rebate: $350,000-$450,000 (phased out)
- No rebate: Homes over $450,000

**Provincial HST Rebate (varies by province):**

| Province | HST Rate | Maximum Rebate |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| Ontario | 13% | $24,000 |
| New Brunswick | 15% | $18,000 |
| Nova Scotia | 15% | $18,750 |
| Newfoundland | 15% | $16,080 |
| PEI | 15% | $18,000 |

**Example (Ontario):**

New home price: $600,000
- GST (5%): $30,000
- Provincial HST (8%): $48,000
- Federal GST rebate: $0 (over $450,000 limit)
- Ontario rebate: $24,000
- **Net HST savings: $24,000**

**Eligibility:**
- New or substantially renovated home
- Purchased for use as primary residence
- Fair market value under rebate thresholds

**Assignment Sales:**
If you buy a pre-construction condo and close before it's built, you're eligible for the rebate. Builder typically applies it to reduce purchase price.

## Provincial and Territorial Programs

### Ontario

**Land Transfer Tax Refund**
- **Maximum refund:** $4,000
- **Eligibility:** First-time buyers purchasing home for primary residence
- **How to claim:** Apply when paying land transfer tax at closing (lawyer handles)

**Toronto-specific:** Additional municipal land transfer tax with separate $4,475 rebate for first-time buyers.

**Combined Toronto savings:** $4,000 (provincial) + $4,475 (municipal) = **$8,475**

**Example:**

Purchase price: $750,000 (Toronto)
- Provincial LTT: $11,475
- Municipal LTT: $11,475
- Total before rebate: $22,950
- Less rebates: -$8,475
- **Net LTT: $14,475** (instead of $22,950)

### British Columbia

**First-Time Home Buyers' Program (Property Transfer Tax Exemption)**
- **Full exemption:** Homes up to $835,000
- **Partial exemption:** $835,000-$860,000
- **Savings:** Up to $8,000

**Example:**

Purchase price: $800,000
- Normal property transfer tax: $16,000
- First-time buyer exemption: -$16,000
- **Net tax: $0**

**BC Home Owner Mortgage and Equity Partnership (BC HOME Partnership)**
- Government loan of up to 5% of purchase price (max $40,000)
- Interest-free, payment-free for first 5 years
- Equal monthly payments years 6-25

**Eligibility:**
- BC resident for 12+ months
- First-time buyer OR haven't owned in 2+ years
- Purchase price ≤ $835,000
- Household income ≤ $200,000

## CMHC Mortgage Loan Insurance: When Is It Required?

If your down payment is less than 20%, you must purchase CMHC (or Sagen, Canada Guaranty) mortgage default insurance.

**Premium Rates (2026):**

| Down Payment | Premium Rate | Premium on $500K |
|--------------|-------------|-----------------|
| 5-9.99% | 4.00% | $20,000 |
| 10-14.99% | 3.10% | $15,500 |
| 15-19.99% | 2.80% | $14,000 |
| 20%+ | 0% | $0 |

**Example:**

$600,000 home, 10% down ($60,000)
- Mortgage: $540,000
- CMHC premium (3.10%): $16,740
- Total mortgage with insurance: $556,740

**Avoiding CMHC:**
If you can reach 20% down ($120,000), you save $16,740. This is why combining FHSA + HBP to maximize down payment is so valuable.

**CMHC Premium Financing:**
Premium is typically added to mortgage, increasing your monthly payment. On $16,740 at 5.5% over 25 years, you pay an extra $103/month.

## Additional Strategies and Tips

### 1. Maximize FHSA Before RRSP for Home Buying

**FHSA advantages over RRSP:**
- No repayment required (HBP requires 15-year repayment)
- Tax-free withdrawal (HBP requires eventual repayment or taxes)
- If you don't buy, transfer to RRSP tax-free (no opportunity cost)

**Strategy:** Maximize FHSA first ($8,000/year for 5 years = $40,000), then contribute to RRSP if you need additional HBP funds.

---

### 2. Use Spousal FHSA for Income Splitting

If one partner earns significantly more, the higher earner can contribute to their own FHSA and get a larger tax refund (based on higher marginal rate).

**Example:**
- Partner A: $120,000 income (43% marginal rate)
- Partner B: $50,000 income (29% marginal rate)

Partner A contributes $8,000 → Refund: $3,440
Partner B contributes $8,000 → Refund: $2,320

**Difference:** $1,120 more in refunds by prioritizing higher earner's FHSA.

---

### 3. Invest FHSA Aggressively If Buying in 5+ Years

FHSA is tax-sheltered—use it to hold high-growth investments:
- **5+ years:** 80-90% equity ETFs (VFV, VCN, VEQT)
- **3-4 years:** 60% equity, 40% bonds (VBAL)
- **1-2 years:** GICs or high-interest savings (EQ Bank 4.00%)

**Example:**

$40,000 FHSA over 5 years:
- Conservative (3% return): $42,000
- Balanced (6% return): $46,000
- Growth (8% return): $48,500

The $6,500 difference buys furniture or covers legal fees.

---

### 4. Consider Assignment Sale for Pre-Construction Condos

**Assignment sale:** You buy pre-construction, then sell the contract before closing (often to avoid closing costs or capitalize on appreciation).

**First-time buyer implications:**
- You remain a first-time buyer if you've never closed on a property
- Can use FHSA, HBP, land transfer tax rebates on next purchase
- Profit from assignment is typically capital gain (50% taxable)

**Risk:** If you can't find assignee, you must close and lose first-time buyer status.

---

### 5. Delay Home Purchase to Maximize FHSA

If you're close to buying but haven't maxed FHSA, consider delaying 1-2 years:

**Scenario:**
- Have $30,000 saved, ready to buy $500,000 condo
- FHSA balance: $16,000 (2 years of contributions)

**Option A: Buy now**
- Down payment: $46,000 (9.2%)
- CMHC insurance: $18,200
- Mortgage: $472,200

**Option B: Delay 2 years, max FHSA**
- Contribute $16,000 more to FHSA = $32,000 total + growth = $35,000
- Save additional $20,000 in non-registered
- Down payment: $71,000 (14.2%)
- CMHC insurance: $13,300
- Mortgage: $442,300
- **Savings: $4,900 in CMHC premium + $6,400 tax refunds = $11,300**

If housing market appreciation is modest (2-3%), delaying to max FHSA can save more than rushing in.

## Common Mistakes First-Time Buyers Make

### 1. Not Opening FHSA Early Enough
Annual contribution room doesn't carry forward. If you wait 3 years to open FHSA, you lose $24,000 in contribution room permanently.

**Fix:** Open FHSA immediately when you start thinking about homeownership, even if buying in 5+ years.

---

### 2. Over-Borrowing from RRSP via HBP
Withdrawing $60,000 sounds great until you realize you must repay $4,000/year for 15 years. If you can't afford repayment, missed amounts become taxable income.

**Fix:** Only use HBP if you can comfortably repay $4,000/year (or proportional amount). Budget before withdrawing.

---

### 3. Using First-Time Home Buyer Incentive in Hot Markets
If you take a 10% shared equity loan on a $600,000 Vancouver condo ($60,000 loan) and it appreciates to $900,000 in 10 years, you repay $90,000—a $30,000 premium.

**Fix:** Avoid shared equity programs in high-appreciation markets. Use FHSA + HBP instead.

---

### 4. Forgetting to Claim Land Transfer Tax Rebate
Lawyers sometimes forget to apply first-time buyer rebates. Verify it's included in your statement of adjustments.

**Fix:** Ask your lawyer explicitly: "Did you apply the first-time home buyer land transfer tax rebate?"

---

### 5. Not Verifying First-Time Buyer Status
CRA defines "first-time buyer" as not owning a home in the past 4 years. If you owned a rental property 3 years ago, you're ineligible.

**Fix:** Review CRA definition carefully. If unsure, consult a tax professional before assuming eligibility.

All programs discussed are governed by:

Eligibility criteria and limits are subject to change. Verify current program details at canada.ca/housing before proceeding.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I use FHSA and Home Buyers' Plan together?**

A: Yes! You can withdraw up to $40,000 from FHSA (no repayment) and $60,000 from RRSP via HBP (must repay over 15 years) for a total of $100,000 in tax-advantaged down payment funds. For couples, this doubles to $200,000.

**Q: What happens to my FHSA if I don't buy a home?**

A: You can transfer the entire FHSA balance to your RRSP or RRIF tax-free without using RRSP contribution room. You do not lose your savings. You must close the FHSA by the earlier of: (a) 15 years after opening, or (b) the year you turn 71.

**Q: Am I a first-time buyer if I owned a property 5 years ago?**

A: Yes. CRA defines first-time buyer as someone who hasn't owned a home in the year you open the account (FHSA) or make the withdrawal (HBP) and the 4 preceding calendar years. If you owned a home 5+ years ago, you're eligible.

**Q: Can I use the First-Time Home Buyer Incentive if my household income is $125,000?**

A: No. The income limit is $120,000 for most of Canada, or $150,000 in Toronto, Vancouver, and Victoria Census Metropolitan Areas. If your household income exceeds the limit, you're ineligible.

**Q: Do I have to repay the First-Time Home Buyer Incentive if I sell my home?**

A: Yes. When you sell, refinance more than 20% equity, or after 25 years, you must repay the incentive based on current market value. If the government provided 5% ($25,000) and your home is now worth $600,000, you repay 5% of $600,000 = $30,000.

**Q: Can I claim the Home Buyers' Tax Credit if my spouse isn't a first-time buyer?**

A: Yes, as long as you personally qualify as a first-time buyer. The credit can be claimed by you even if your spouse previously owned a home (as long as you didn't).

**Q: What's the best province for first-time buyer programs?**

A: British Columbia offers the most generous provincial support: full property transfer tax exemption (up to $8,000 savings on homes under $835,000) plus the BC HOME Partnership loan program. Ontario (especially Toronto) is strong for land transfer tax rebates ($8,475 combined provincial + municipal).

**Q: Should I use my TFSA or open an FHSA for my down payment?**

A: Open FHSA. Unlike TFSA, FHSA contributions are tax-deductible (immediate refund) and withdrawals are tax-free for home purchase. TFSA withdrawals are also tax-free but contributions aren't deductible. FHSA gives you both benefits. Use TFSA only after maxing FHSA.

**Q: Can I contribute to FHSA and RRSP in the same year?**

A: Yes. They have separate contribution limits. You can contribute $8,000 to FHSA and up to 18% of previous year's income (max $32,490 in 2026) to RRSP in the same year, and deduct both on your tax return.

**Q: How do I repay the Home Buyers' Plan?**

A: Starting in the second year after withdrawal, contribute the annual minimum (1/15 of total withdrawn) to your RRSP and designate it as an HBP repayment on your tax return (Schedule 7). If you contributed $10,000 to RRSP but only need to repay $4,000 for HBP, you can designate $4,000 as HBP repayment and deduct the remaining $6,000 as a regular RRSP contribution.
