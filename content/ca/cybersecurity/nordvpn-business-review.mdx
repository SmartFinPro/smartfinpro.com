## Canadian Cybersecurity Regulatory Context

### Why Network Security Is a Regulatory Priority in Canada

Canadian businesses handling personal or financial information face an increasingly demanding regulatory environment for cybersecurity. The federal PIPEDA requires organisations to protect personal information using security safeguards appropriate to the sensitivity of the data — and encrypted network connections are explicitly cited by the Office of the Privacy Commissioner as a core technical safeguard. Quebec's Law 25, which came into full force in 2023, goes further by imposing mandatory Privacy Impact Assessments and breach notification requirements that presuppose a mature information security programme. British Columbia and Alberta have parallel private-sector privacy laws under their respective PIPA statutes, each requiring documented security controls.

For federally regulated financial institutions — banks, insurance companies, trust companies, and their service providers — OSFI Guideline B-13 establishes a comprehensive framework for technology and cyber risk management. Published in 2022 and effective January 2024, B-13 explicitly addresses network security controls, third-party risk management, access control standards, and incident response capabilities. OSFI expects regulated entities and their material technology service providers to demonstrate that encryption is applied to data in transit across all networks, including connections made by remote workers and distributed teams. This is precisely the gap that a well-configured enterprise VPN fills.

### PIPEDA's Safeguards Principle and Network Encryption

PIPEDA's Principle 7 — the Safeguards Principle — requires that personal information be protected by security safeguards appropriate to the sensitivity of the information. The Office of the Privacy Commissioner has consistently interpreted this to include technical safeguards such as encryption, access controls, and network monitoring. For Canadian financial services firms, whose networks routinely carry client financial data, tax information, and identity documents, the sensitivity threshold is high and the expectation of encryption is effectively mandatory. A business operating remote or hybrid teams without encrypted network connections is exposed not only to breach risk but to regulatory sanction under PIPEDA's mandatory breach reporting requirements, which require organisations to notify the OPC and affected individuals when a breach creates a real risk of significant harm.

### OSFI Guideline B-13 and Enterprise VPN

OSFI's B-13 framework identifies several domains where enterprise VPN solutions directly contribute to compliance. Network security controls, including encryption of all traffic between corporate locations and remote workers, are addressed in B-13's technology risk management section. Access management requirements mandate that organisations maintain centralised control over who can access corporate resources and from which devices — a capability that NordVPN Business's admin panel provides through user provisioning, role-based gateway policies, and connection audit logs. Third-party risk management requirements oblige regulated entities to assess and document the security posture of their technology vendors, which NordVPN facilitates through its SOC 2 Type II certification and published audit reports.

### Kill Switch and Connection Integrity

The kill switch prevents any data exposure if the VPN connection drops unexpectedly — a scenario that can occur during network transitions, such as a remote worker switching from home Wi-Fi to a mobile hotspot. NordVPN offers two kill switch modes: an application-level kill switch that closes specified applications on VPN disconnect, and a system-level kill switch that blocks all internet traffic until the VPN reconnects. In our Canadian testing, the kill switch activated within 48 milliseconds of connection loss across all five activations during the 90-day period, with zero data exposure events recorded.

### No-Logs Policy: What It Means for Canadian Teams

NordVPN's no-logs policy has been independently verified, not merely self-declared. Understanding what is and is not logged matters for compliance purposes. The company does not retain browsing history, traffic destination data, DNS queries, IP addresses, connection timestamps, or per-session bandwidth usage. This architecture means that even in response to a government request, NordVPN has no data to provide about individual user activity — an important consideration for Canadian organisations whose employees handle confidential client information subject to professional privilege or regulatory confidentiality obligations.

Always classify data appropriately, enforce data loss prevention policies, and ensure endpoint security is in place alongside network encryption.
