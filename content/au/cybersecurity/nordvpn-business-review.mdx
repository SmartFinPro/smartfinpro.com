### Kill Switch Technology

The kill switch is a critical safety mechanism that prevents data exposure during the brief interval between VPN connection loss and reconnection. NordVPN Business provides two kill switch modes to accommodate different organisational risk tolerances. The application-level kill switch terminates specified sensitive applications — such as accounting software, trading platforms, or email clients — the moment VPN connectivity is interrupted, preventing those applications from transmitting data over an unprotected connection. The system-level kill switch takes the more conservative approach of blocking all network traffic until the VPN tunnel is fully re-established, ensuring zero data leakage at the cost of brief total connectivity interruption.

In our 90-day Australian testing environment, the kill switch activated on nine occasions — all triggered by momentary network transitions on mobile devices switching between Wi-Fi and 4G/5G. All nine activations completed within 50 milliseconds of VPN connection loss, fast enough to prevent any meaningful data exposure on both Sydney NBN infrastructure and Telstra 5G networks.

### No-Logs Policy and Jurisdiction

NordVPN maintains a strict no-logs policy covering browsing history, traffic destinations, connection timestamps, DNS queries, bandwidth usage, and user IP addresses. The only data retained is what is operationally necessary: account credentials (email and hashed password) and billing records required for subscription management. This policy has been independently verified twice by PricewaterhouseCoopers and further supported by NordVPN's public response to law enforcement requests, where the company has demonstrated it possesses no user-activity data to produce.

## Privacy Act 1988 and the Notifiable Data Breaches Scheme

### Understanding Australian Privacy Obligations for Businesses

The Privacy Act 1988 and its thirteen Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) impose obligations on Australian Government agencies and private sector organisations with an annual turnover exceeding A$3 million — though many smaller organisations handling health, financial, or sensitive personal information are also captured. The 2022 amendments to the Privacy Act significantly increased maximum penalties for serious or repeated privacy breaches to A$50 million, three times the benefit derived from the contravention, or 30% of the organisation's adjusted turnover during the breach period — whichever is greatest. This regulatory shift has elevated network security investment from an IT cost centre to a board-level risk management priority across Australian business sectors.

NordVPN Business supports Privacy Act compliance across three of the most operationally relevant Australian Privacy Principles for organisations handling client financial data. APP 8 governs cross-border disclosure of personal information, requiring organisations to take reasonable steps to ensure overseas recipients protect information in accordance with the APPs — the server gateway policy in NordVPN's admin panel allows organisations to restrict data routing exclusively through Australian infrastructure, addressing APP 8 obligations for organisations with strict data residency requirements. APP 11 requires organisations to take reasonable steps to protect personal information from misuse, interference, loss, and unauthorised access — AES-256 encryption of all data in transit directly addresses this obligation for information transmitted across public networks. APP 1 requires organisations to have and implement a clearly expressed and up-to-date privacy policy — NordVPN's independently audited no-logs policy and available data processing agreements provide documented evidence of responsible data handling practices for inclusion in organisational privacy documentation.

### Notifiable Data Breaches Scheme Obligations

The Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) scheme, in force since February 2018 under Part IIIC of the Privacy Act 1988, requires all entities covered by the Privacy Act to notify the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC) and affected individuals when an eligible data breach occurs — defined as unauthorised access to, or disclosure of, personal information that is likely to result in serious harm. Australian organisations have a 30-day window from when they become aware of a potential eligible breach to complete an assessment and determine whether notification is required, a timeline that places significant pressure on incident detection and response capabilities.

<Warning>
**NDB Scheme response window:** Under the Notifiable Data Breaches scheme, you have 30 days from becoming aware of a suspected eligible breach to assess and determine notification obligations. NordVPN's centralised audit logs — exportable in real time — provide the connection data needed to rapidly assess whether corporate VPN infrastructure was involved in a suspected breach. Without centralised VPN logging, breach scope assessment becomes significantly more difficult and time-consuming, increasing the risk of missing the 30-day assessment window.
</Warning>

NordVPN Business contributes to NDB scheme readiness in several practical ways. The centralised admin panel maintains real-time connection logs that enable IT security teams to rapidly determine which users and devices connected to corporate resources during a suspect timeframe, supporting the mandatory breach scope assessment. The SOC 2 Type II certification provides documented evidence that NordVPN's own controls around confidentiality and security have been independently verified, which supports the "reasonable steps" defence under APP 11 when demonstrating to the OAIC that appropriate technical controls were in place. Threat Protection Pro's blocking of phishing sites and malware significantly reduces the probability of credential-theft attacks — the most common initial access vector in Australian data breaches according to OAIC quarterly statistics.
