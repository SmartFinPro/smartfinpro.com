Wise's pricing transparency is genuinely best-in-class — the fee is displayed in full before you confirm any transfer, and the mid-market rate has zero spread. However, there are secondary costs that can affect your total cost of ownership. The biggest variable is the currency corridor: while AUD→USD costs around 0.55%, less common corridors (AUD→THB, AUD→IDR) can cost 1.0-1.5%. The Wise Interest management fee of 0.72% also materially reduces your effective yield from the headline 2.98% to approximately 2.26% net — still competitive, but worth understanding before committing large balances.

Wise Business provides what is effectively a global bank account in a single dashboard. After the A$65 activation, you receive local account details in **23+ currencies** — meaning your clients and customers in those countries can pay you as if you had a local bank account. For Australian businesses invoicing US clients, the USD account details are particularly valuable. Your American clients pay via standard domestic ACH transfer — they see a regular US bank account (via Evolve Bank & Trust) and pay zero international wire fees. Funds arrive in hours, not the 2-5 business days typical of SWIFT transfers through Big 4 banks. If you are also managing [forex positions](/au/forex/) alongside your business payments, keeping your FX separate on Wise gives you cleaner cost tracking.

### 2. Mid-Market FX Rate — Zero Markup

The single most important feature of Wise Business for Australian users is its exchange rate model. Wise uses the **mid-market exchange rate** — the same rate you see on Google, Reuters, or XE — with absolutely zero spread or markup. The only cost is the transparent percentage fee displayed before you confirm. This is fundamentally different from every bank and most fintech competitors, which embed a margin of 0.5-3.0% in their exchange rate.

To understand the real impact, we tracked our effective exchange rate on 380+ transactions over six months and compared it to the Reuters mid-market rate at the time of each conversion. On every single transaction, the Wise rate matched the mid-market benchmark within 0.01% — confirming that the "mid-market rate" claim is genuine, not marketing language.

### 3. Wise Interest — Earn on Business Balances

Launched in Australia in April 2025, [Wise Interest](https://wise.com/au/interest/) allows business accounts to earn a variable return on idle AUD balances. The headline rate is approximately **2.98% per annum**, with an annual management fee of 0.72% — yielding a net effective return of approximately 2.26%. Funds are invested in qualifying money market instruments, and the rate fluctuates with market conditions. There are no lock-in periods, no minimum balances, and you can access your funds at any time.

This feature makes Wise competitive with high-interest business savings accounts like Zeller (up to 4% p.a.) and [Airwallex Yield](https://www.airwallex.com/au/yield), though the 0.72% management fee means the net yield is lower than some competitors. For businesses that maintain A$50,000+ in idle AUD balances, the effective annual earning is approximately A$1,130 — meaningful, but not the primary reason to choose Wise.

## Wise Business Features Deep-Dive

### Batch Payments for Australian Businesses

Wise Business supports **batch payment uploads** of up to 1,000 international transfers via a single CSV or XLSX file. This is particularly valuable for Australian businesses that pay international suppliers on regular cycles — for example, a digital agency paying 30 contractors across the US, UK, and Philippines each month, or an importer settling invoices with 20 Chinese suppliers simultaneously. Each payment in the batch is processed individually at the mid-market rate at the time of execution, and you receive a detailed breakdown of fees before confirming the entire batch.

Batch payment features are free on Wise — there is no additional charge beyond the standard per-transfer fee. Combined with the free API access, this makes Wise a powerful payment infrastructure layer for businesses that need to automate high-volume international payouts.

### Debit Cards & Team Expense Management

Every Wise Business account includes a free virtual Mastercard debit card. Physical cards can be ordered for A$6 each, and team members can be issued individual cards with customisable spending limits. All card transactions use the mid-market rate for any foreign currency spending, making Wise cards among the cheapest options for international business travel or online subscriptions paid in foreign currencies.

However, Wise's expense management features are basic compared to platforms like Airwallex. There are no receipt capture workflows, no approval chains for card spending, and no multi-level expense controls. If your business has more than 10 team members with cards and needs structured expense approval processes, [Airwallex](https://www.airwallex.com/au/) or a dedicated expense management tool like [SAP Concur](https://www.concur.com.au/) will serve you better. For protecting your business from [cybersecurity threats](/au/cybersecurity/) while managing multiple team cards and API keys, Wise's 2FA and device-based security provide a solid foundation.

### Fund Safeguarding

Wise is **not an Authorised Deposit-taking Institution (ADI)**, which means customer funds are not protected by the [Australian Government's Financial Claims Scheme](https://www.apra.gov.au/financial-claims-scheme-for-adis) (which guarantees up to A$250,000 per depositor at ADIs like CBA, Westpac, ANZ, and NAB). - Customer funds are held in **segregated accounts at approved Australian financial institutions** (ADIs)
- Funds **cannot be used for Wise's operational purposes** or mixed with corporate capital
- In the event of Wise's insolvency, customer funds are **legally separate** and would be returned to customers ahead of other creditors
- The PPF licence requires regular reporting to APRA on fund positions and compliance

For businesses holding large AUD balances (over A$100,000), we recommend keeping only working capital in Wise and maintaining primary reserves at an ADI where the [Government Guarantee](https://www.apra.gov.au/financial-claims-scheme-for-adis) applies.

## How Wise Makes Money

Understanding Wise's revenue model helps you anticipate where costs may increase and where the platform's incentives align — or diverge — with yours as a customer.

Wise (Wise plc) generates revenue through **five primary channels** in Australia:

1. **Per-transaction FX fees** — The transparent percentage fee (from 0.63%) on every currency conversion is Wise's core revenue source. Because there is no monthly subscription, Wise earns more when you transact more — aligning its incentives with helping you make payments efficiently
2. **Wise Interest management fee** — The 0.72% annual fee on invested balances through [Wise Interest](https://wise.com/au/interest/) generates margin on idle funds. As business balances grow, this becomes an increasingly material revenue stream
3. **Card interchange fees** — Wise earns a percentage (typically 0.3-0.5%) from the Mastercard network every time you or your team use a Wise debit card for purchases
4. **SWIFT receiving fees** — The ~USD 6.11 fixed fee charged when USD arrives via SWIFT (rather than domestic ACH) represents margin on inbound international wires
5. **Physical card fees** — A$6 per additional physical card, generating modest revenue from larger teams

This model means Wise is incentivised to handle as many transactions as possible while keeping fees competitive enough to attract volume. Unlike subscription-based competitors, Wise does not profit from months where you do not transact — which is why the per-transaction fee is slightly higher than Revolut's in-allowance rate for high-volume users.
