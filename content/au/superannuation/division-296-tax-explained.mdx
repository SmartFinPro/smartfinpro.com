## Understanding Division 296 Tax

The introduction of Division 296 represents the most consequential change to Australia's superannuation system since the transfer balance cap was introduced. From 1 July 2025, Australians with a total superannuation balance (TSB) exceeding $3 million face an additional 15% tax on the proportion of earnings attributable to the excess above that threshold. While the government estimates this currently affects only around 80,000 individuals, the lack of indexation means many more Australians will be captured in the decades ahead as super balances grow and inflation erodes the real value of $3 million. Treasury estimates the measure will raise approximately $2.3 billion over its first four years.

Your TSB includes all superannuation interests aggregated by the ATO: accumulation account balances, account-based pension balances, defined benefit interests valued using the ATO's formula (generally 16 times annual pension entitlement), and all accounts across multiple funds. You cannot avoid the threshold by splitting your balance across several super funds because the ATO automatically aggregates every interest. The tax applies from the 2025-26 financial year onwards, with the first assessments expected in late 2026 and payment due by the standard tax lodgement deadline.

## How Division 296 Tax Is Calculated

Understanding the calculation formula is critical for planning. The Division 296 tax liability follows a proportional approach rather than taxing only the excess balance directly.

**Division 296 Tax = (Proportion of earnings x Total earnings) x 15%**

Where the proportion of earnings equals the excess balance divided by the total superannuation balance, the excess balance equals your TSB minus $3 million, and total earnings equals the change in your TSB plus withdrawals minus contributions during the financial year.

### Step-by-Step Calculation Example

Consider this scenario: your TSB at 30 June 2025 is $3.8 million, your TSB at 1 July 2024 was $3.5 million, you made $100,000 in contributions and $50,000 in withdrawals during the year. Total earnings would be ($3,800,000 minus $3,500,000) plus $50,000 minus $100,000, equalling $250,000. The excess balance is $800,000 ($3.8 million minus $3 million). The proportion is $800,000 divided by $3,800,000, which equals 21.05%. The Division 296 tax is therefore $250,000 multiplied by 21.05% multiplied by 15%, equalling approximately $7,894. This amount is payable in addition to the standard 15% tax already levied on earnings within the super fund.

<Warning>
**Unrealised gains create real tax bills:** Unlike standard super taxation, Division 296 counts both realised and unrealised capital gains as "earnings." If your SMSF property increases in value by $500,000 without being sold, that increase is included in your Division 296 calculation. You may face a tax liability without having generated any cash to pay it, potentially forcing you to sell assets or withdraw additional funds from super.
</Warning>

### Key Calculation Rules

Several aspects of the calculation are important to understand for proper tax planning. Negative earnings in a financial year do not create credits or offsets that can be carried forward to future years, meaning each year is assessed independently. The ATO measures your TSB at 30 June and just before each super event during the year (contributions, withdrawals, rollovers), using the higher measurement to determine whether you exceed the threshold. Multiple accounts across APRA-regulated funds, SMSFs, defined benefit schemes, and in some cases foreign super are all aggregated into a single TSB figure.

## Practical Scenarios

### SMSF with Property Holdings

John, aged 58, holds an SMSF balance of $3.4 million including commercial property valued at $2.1 million. During FY26, the property is revalued from $2.0 million to $2.2 million (creating a $200,000 unrealised gain), his share portfolio generates $80,000 in realised gains and $50,000 in unrealised gains, he makes $30,000 in concessional contributions, and he withdraws $120,000 in pension payments. His total earnings are $240,000, the excess balance is $400,000, and the proportion is 11.76%. His Division 296 tax liability comes to approximately $4,235 despite not having sold the property. He may need to withdraw additional funds or sell other assets to pay the ATO.

### Defined Benefit Plus SMSF

David, aged 61, is a public servant with a defined benefit scheme valued at $2.3 million (using the ATO's 16 times annual pension formula) plus an SMSF holding $850,000. His combined TSB of $3.15 million exceeds the threshold by $150,000. The calculation becomes complex because defined benefit earnings are determined using the ATO's prescribed method rather than the standard approach used for accumulation balances. Professional advice is essential for members in this situation.

<Tip>
**For members approaching the threshold:** If your TSB is between $2.7 million and $3 million, model your projected balance growth over the next three to five years. The static threshold means even modest returns could push you over. Consider adjusting your contribution strategy, making strategic withdrawals, or reallocating growth assets outside super before you cross the $3 million line.
</Tip>

## Strategies to Minimise Division 296 Tax

### Withdraw Excess to Stay Below Threshold

If your TSB slightly exceeds $3 million, withdrawing enough to bring your balance below the threshold eliminates the tax entirely. This strategy is only available if you have met a condition of release (typically age 60 or older), and withdrawn amounts may be tax-free for members over 60. The trade-off is permanently losing the tax-advantaged super environment for those funds.

### Contribute to Spouse's Super

Redirecting future contributions to a spouse's account slows the growth of your own balance while keeping family wealth in the concessional super environment. You can use contribution splitting for concessional contributions and make spouse contributions subject to caps. This approach takes years to have material impact on large balances but is effective for couples where one partner is approaching the threshold while the other has a significantly lower balance.

### Asset Allocation Adjustments

For amounts above $3 million, holding growth assets outside super may be more tax-efficient than keeping them within the fund. Inside super, growth assets benefit from 15% tax on earnings for the portion under $3 million, but the excess portion faces a 30% effective rate including unrealised gains. Outside super, assets held personally benefit from no tax on unrealised gains and a 50% CGT discount if held for more than 12 months, with the maximum marginal rate on discounted gains being 23.5%. The cost of restructuring (including CGT on assets transferred out of super) must be weighed against the ongoing Division 296 savings.

<CollapsibleSection title="Additional Minimisation Strategies" count={4}>

- **Insurance premium deductions:** Life and TPD insurance held in super reduces both your TSB and calculated earnings, though insurance must serve a genuine need and premiums can be expensive for older members
- **Pension phase management:** Moving to pension phase does not avoid Division 296, but strategic pension withdrawals reduce your TSB and count as withdrawals in the earnings calculation, potentially lowering the proportional tax
- **SMSF-specific tactics:** In-specie transfers of assets out of super (tax-free if over 60 and in pension phase), asset segregation for clearer tax allocation, and careful timing of property revaluations
- **Multi-year tax planning:** Structure contributions, withdrawals, and asset sales across multiple financial years to minimise Division 296 impact, including deferring concessional contributions and timing asset sales for lower-earning years

</CollapsibleSection>

## Common Misconceptions

Many Australians misunderstand how Division 296 operates. It is not a tax on your super balance itself but rather on the earnings attributable to the amount exceeding $3 million. A $4 million super balance does not create a $150,000 tax bill. The tax only applies to the proportion of earnings related to the excess, which in practice produces much smaller liabilities than people expect.

The threshold is not indexed. Unlike the transfer balance cap, which is indexed to CPI, the $3 million Division 296 threshold is static and set in legislation. This is one of the most significant long-term consequences of the measure, as inflation and compounding super returns will progressively capture more Australians over time. Splitting your balance across multiple funds does not help because the ATO aggregates all superannuation interests regardless of how many accounts you hold. Having four accounts of $750,000 each still creates a $3 million TSB.

Negative earnings do not create credits. If your super experiences losses in one financial year, you simply have no Division 296 liability for that year. You cannot carry forward losses to offset future gains. Additionally, Division 296 affects all age groups with balances above the threshold, not only retirees. Younger high-income earners with aggressive super strategies, particularly those holding property in SMSFs, can also be captured.

<Tip>
**Do not rely on future repeal:** While some political parties have indicated they would review Division 296, legislative change would require passage through both houses of Parliament and Royal Assent. Plan as if the tax is permanent and adjust if legislation changes rather than gambling on repeal.
</Tip>

## SMSF vs APRA Fund Considerations

Division 296 affects all super structures, but the administrative burden and planning flexibility differ significantly between SMSFs and APRA-regulated funds.

SMSF members benefit from full control over asset allocation and timing, the ability to implement sophisticated tax strategies such as in-specie transfers and asset segregation, and the option to pay Division 296 tax directly from the fund via an ATO release authority. However, SMSFs also carry greater complexity. Trustees are responsible for accurate reporting and may require actuarial certificates, property revaluations can trigger unexpected tax liabilities, and the ATO scrutinises aggressive strategies closely.

Members of APRA-regulated industry and retail funds benefit from professional management of all calculations and compliance, with no trustee liability for errors. The trade-off is limited control over contribution and withdrawal timing, less flexibility in strategic planning, and potentially needing to pay Division 296 from personal funds if the fund does not consent to a release from the super account.

## Tax Return and Payment Process

The ATO calculates your TSB automatically using data from all super funds and issues a separate Division 296 assessment if you exceed the $3 million threshold. Payment is due by your standard tax lodgement deadline (31 October, or later if you use a tax agent). SMSF members can request a release authority from the ATO to pay the tax from their fund, with the amount counting as a benefit payment. APRA fund members must request their fund's consent for a release, which the fund may refuse if the member is not in pension phase.

You must maintain records of annual TSB statements from all funds, contribution and withdrawal documentation, rollover records, asset valuations (especially for SMSFs), and any actuarial certificates for at least five years after the relevant Division 296 assessment.

## Estate Planning Implications

Division 296 introduces important estate planning considerations. When a member dies, Division 296 is calculated up to the date of death and the tax liability may be paid from the estate or from the super death benefit, reducing the amount received by beneficiaries. If a pension is reversionary and transfers to a surviving spouse, it may push the spouse's own TSB above $3 million, creating an ongoing Division 296 liability for the survivor.

Members approaching the threshold should review their binding death benefit nominations to clarify who receives super and who bears Division 296 liability, consider non-reversionary pensions with lump sum death benefit nominations for greater beneficiary flexibility, and evaluate strategic withdrawals of amounts above $3 million into personal ownership or testamentary trust structures where appropriate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the $3 million threshold indexed to inflation?

No. The threshold is static and not indexed to inflation, wages, or any other measure. Over time, more Australians will be affected as super balances grow and inflation reduces the real value of $3 million. Treasury modelling projects that over 500,000 individuals could be subject to Division 296 by 2050.

### Can I avoid Division 296 by transferring super to my spouse?

You cannot directly transfer existing super balances to a spouse. However, you can use contribution splitting for future concessional contributions, make spouse contributions subject to caps, and redirect future employer contributions to a spouse's account with employer agreement. These strategies take years to materially reduce high balances.

### Does Division 296 apply to defined benefit pensions?

Yes. Defined benefit interests are included in your TSB using the ATO's valuation formula (generally 16 times annual pension entitlement). If the combined value of your defined benefit plus other super exceeds $3 million, Division 296 applies. Earnings from defined benefit interests are calculated using the ATO's prescribed method.

### How does Division 296 affect SMSF property valuations?

SMSF property must be valued at market value annually as at 30 June. Unrealised gains from revaluations count as earnings for Division 296. You cannot manipulate valuations to avoid the tax since all valuations must be accurate and defensible to the ATO. This means property value increases trigger Division 296 even without a sale.

### What if my super makes a loss in one year?

You have no Division 296 liability for any financial year where your super earnings are negative. However, losses cannot be carried forward as credits to offset positive earnings in future years. Each year is assessed independently.

### Can I have multiple SMSFs to avoid Division 296?

No. The ATO aggregates all superannuation interests regardless of how many funds you hold. Multiple SMSFs also increase your annual costs through duplicate administration fees, audit fees, and tax return preparation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<details>
<summary>What is Division 296 tax?</summary>

Division 296 is an additional 15% tax on earnings from superannuation balances exceeding $3 million, effective from 1 July 2025. Total tax on earnings above the threshold becomes 30% (15% standard + 15% Division 296). The ATO assesses it annually and notably includes both realised gains and unrealised increases in asset values.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Who is affected by Division 296 tax?</summary>

Approximately 80,000 Australians with total superannuation balances exceeding $3 million are affected. This includes high-balance SMSF members, executives with large defined benefit entitlements, and members who have accumulated significant balances through decades of contributions and compounding returns. The $3 million threshold is not indexed to inflation under the current legislation.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How does Division 296 tax treat unrealised gains?</summary>

This is the most controversial aspect of Division 296. Unlike ordinary income tax, Division 296 applies to unrealised gains — increases in asset value even when you haven't sold the asset. This particularly affects SMSF members holding illiquid assets such as direct property or private equity, who may face tax bills without available cash to pay them.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I pay my Division 296 tax personally rather than from my super?</summary>

Yes. The ATO allows Division 296 tax to be paid personally from outside superannuation, which preserves your super balance. This election must be made before the due date. Paying personally can be advantageous if you want to maximise the tax-free compound growth remaining in your fund during the accumulation phase.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Does Division 296 apply to defined benefit funds?</summary>

Yes, but the calculation methodology differs for defined benefit members. The ATO uses a notional earnings calculation based on the increase in the present value of your defined benefit interest. Many public sector and corporate defined benefit members with large accrued entitlements will be caught, even without a conventional account balance exceeding $3 million.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Should I withdraw super to get below the $3 million threshold?</summary>

This is a highly individual decision requiring advice from a qualified SMSF adviser or tax professional. Key factors include your age, preservation age access rules, marginal tax rate on withdrawals, alternative investment options outside super, and estate planning goals. For most members, the 30% rate on earnings above $3 million is still lower than the top marginal rate on personal income.

</details>
