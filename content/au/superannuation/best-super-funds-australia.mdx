## Our Ranking Methodology

We assessed every major super fund across five weighted criteria designed to reflect what matters most to Australian members building long-term retirement wealth. Investment performance carries the heaviest weighting because returns compound over decades and represent the primary driver of your final retirement balance. Fees receive the second-highest weighting because even small percentage differences erode significant capital over a 30-year career. Investment options, member services, and insurance round out the assessment with lower but still meaningful weightings that reflect their impact on member experience and flexibility.

### Investment Performance (40% Weighting)

The performance assessment examines 10-year returns for the balanced option, consistency across different market cycles, performance relative to peer funds and benchmarks, and risk-adjusted returns. We focus on the 10-year horizon because shorter periods can be distorted by a single year of unusually strong or weak markets. Consistency matters because a fund that delivers steady 8% returns is generally preferable to one that swings between 15% and 1% even if the arithmetic average is similar.

### Fees (30% Weighting)

Fee analysis covers total fees as a percentage of balance, administration fees, investment fees, and any hidden costs or performance fees. We calculate total costs on a $100,000 balance in the balanced option to provide an apples-to-apples comparison. Retail fund averages are included as a benchmark to illustrate how much members can save by choosing a competitive fund.

### Investment Options, Services & Insurance (30% Combined)

The remaining 30% of our weighting is split across investment options (15%), member services (10%), and insurance (5%). Investment options are scored on range of choices, ethical and ESG availability, direct investment access, and lifecycle options. Member services cover digital platform quality, financial advice availability, customer support, and educational resources. Insurance scoring examines default cover value, premium competitiveness, and customisation flexibility.

## Self-Managed Super Funds (SMSFs) vs Industry Funds

Self-managed super funds give members complete control over their investment strategy, including the ability to hold direct property, but they come with significant costs and compliance obligations that make them unsuitable for most Australians. The critical question is whether your balance is large enough to justify the fixed annual costs of running an SMSF compared to the percentage-based fees of a well-run industry fund.

An SMSF is a good fit if your super balance exceeds $500,000 and ideally sits above $1 million, you have genuine financial literacy and interest in active management, you want to own direct property within your super, you have complex estate planning needs, and you have the time and willingness to manage ongoing compliance. An SMSF is a poor fit if your balance is below $200,000 where fees become prohibitively high as a percentage, you prefer hands-off management, you are uncomfortable with trustee responsibilities, or you have limited investment knowledge.

### SMSF Cost Comparison

Typical SMSF annual costs include setup fees of $1,000-$3,000, administration of $1,500-$3,000, audit of $500-$1,500, and accounting and tax preparation of $1,000-$2,500, for a total of approximately $3,000-$9,000 per annum. The break-even analysis against industry funds shows that at a $200,000 balance the SMSF costs 2.50% versus 0.75% for an industry fund. At $500,000 the SMSF costs 1.00% versus 0.70%. The SMSF only becomes marginally cheaper at $1,000,000 where it costs 0.50% versus 0.68%, and it becomes clearly advantageous at $2,000,000 where the SMSF costs just 0.25%.

The verdict is clear: SMSFs make financial sense for balances above $1 million combined with genuine active management interest. Otherwise, top industry funds offer better value with none of the compliance burden.

## Switching Super Funds

Switching to a better-performing, lower-fee fund is one of the simplest ways to improve your retirement outcome, but there are important considerations to address before you make the move. The process itself is straightforward: choose your new fund and open an account, request a rollover via the new fund which handles the transfer, check your insurance arrangements before closing the old fund, and update your employer with your new fund details. The transfer typically takes 7-30 days to complete.

### Before You Switch: Critical Checks

Before initiating any transfer, verify five key areas to avoid unintended consequences. First, check for exit fees, as some older funds charge them even though they have been banned for new accounts. Second, review your insurance cover carefully because switching may result in loss of existing cover, higher premiums if you are older or have health issues, or waiting periods on new policies. Third, confirm that your new fund offers investment options matching your risk profile and preferences. Fourth, if you have a defined benefit fund, do not switch without professional advice because defined benefits are extremely valuable and irreplaceable. Fifth, check whether your current fund has grandfathered low-fee options that are no longer available to new members.

<Warning>
**Defined benefit warning:** If you hold a defined benefit super fund, consult a licensed financial adviser before making any changes. Defined benefit entitlements are calculated based on your salary and years of service, not investment returns, and once forfeited they cannot be recovered. The guaranteed nature of these benefits makes them exceptionally valuable.
</Warning>

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best super fund in Australia?

AustralianSuper ranks number one overall for 2026, combining 8.7% p.a. 10-year returns with 0.68% fees and comprehensive features. However, the best fund depends on your age, priorities, and needs. Young investors may prefer Hostplus for its highest growth returns at 10.2% p.a., pre-retirees benefit from Australian Retirement Trust's lifecycle options, and ESG-focused members should consider AustralianSuper's Sustainable Balanced option or HESTA.

### How do I choose a super fund?

Prioritise low total fees under 0.80%, strong 10-year performance in your chosen investment option, investment options that match your risk profile and values, insurance cover that suits your family situation, and quality digital tools and financial advice access. Do not choose a fund based on short-term performance alone because one good year can mask a decade of mediocrity.

### Should I consolidate multiple super funds?

Yes, in most cases. Multiple funds mean paying duplicate administration fees of $100-$200 per annum each, multiple insurance premiums, and the hassle of tracking performance across several accounts. Consolidate via MyGov or your new fund's online tool. The exception is if you hold a defined benefit scheme, a grandfathered low-fee option, or have specific insurance coverage that would be difficult to replicate.

### Are industry funds better than retail funds?

On average, yes. Industry funds are not-for-profit, meaning all profits are returned to members rather than paid to shareholders. Historically, industry funds have delivered 0.5-1% p.a. higher returns and charge significantly lower fees at around 0.70% versus 1.20% for the retail fund average. However, the best retail funds can compete with mid-tier industry funds, so focus on individual fund performance rather than fund type alone.

### How much do super fund fees matter?

Enormously. A 0.62% fee difference on a $100,000 balance over 30 years with $10,000 annual contributions costs approximately $110,000 in lost retirement wealth. Even a 0.10% difference compounds into tens of thousands over a career. Minimise fees wherever possible because unlike returns, which are uncertain, fees are a guaranteed drag every single year.

### What investment option should I choose based on my age?

The general rule is that younger members with longer horizons can tolerate more risk. In your 20s-30s, consider high-growth options with 80% or more in shares. In your 40s-50s, a balanced or growth option with 60-70% shares is appropriate. From 60 onwards, balanced or conservative options with 40-60% shares help protect your accumulated wealth. However, risk tolerance also matters. If market volatility causes you to panic-sell during downturns, choose a more conservative option regardless of your age.

### When does an SMSF make financial sense?

An SMSF becomes cost-competitive with industry funds at balances above $1 million, where the fixed annual costs of $3,000-$9,000 represent a lower percentage than industry fund fees. Below $500,000, SMSF costs are prohibitively high relative to the balance. You also need financial literacy, active management interest, and willingness to handle compliance obligations.

### Can I switch super funds without losing my insurance?

Switching funds can affect your insurance in several ways. You may lose existing cover entirely, face higher premiums due to age or health conditions, or encounter waiting periods on new policies. The safest approach is to apply for cover with your new fund first, wait until it is confirmed, and only then cancel your old fund. Never close an old super account without first securing alternative insurance if you need it.

<details>
<summary>How are Australian super funds regulated?</summary>

All industry and retail super funds are prudentially regulated by APRA under the Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act. APRA conducts annual performance tests and publishes heatmap data on fund performance and fees. ASIC regulates disclosure obligations. The ATO administers the super system, including employer contribution compliance and SMSF registrations.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is the superannuation guarantee rate in 2026?</summary>

The Superannuation Guarantee (SG) rate is 11.5% of ordinary time earnings for 2025–26. This increases to 12% from 1 July 2026 under legislated schedules. All Australian employers must contribute at least the SG rate to an APRA-regulated complying super fund (or SMSF) for eligible employees. Failure to pay on time incurs the Superannuation Guarantee Charge.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I change my super fund?</summary>

Yes. You can roll over your superannuation from any existing fund to another APRA-regulated fund at any time. There are no government fees for rolling over, though some funds charge exit fees — check your fund's PDS. Since the Stapling rules (2021), if you change employers and don't nominate a fund, your employer uses your existing stapled fund. Consolidating multiple accounts reduces duplicate fees.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What fees should I look for in a super fund?</summary>

Key fees to compare are investment (percentage-based, typically 0.10–0.50% p.a.), administration (flat dollar amount of $50–$200/year), performance fees (some funds charge these on outperformance), and exit fees (largely eliminated but check older funds). For a $50,000 balance, even a 0.50% fee difference adds up to $25,000 over a 30-year career after compound effects.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is the APRA annual performance test for super funds?</summary>

APRA's annual performance test assesses MySuper (default) products against a benchmark portfolio over 8 years. Funds that underperform the benchmark by more than 0.50% per year are required to notify their members in writing. Persistent underperformers face regulatory consequences. This test was introduced in 2021 to drive consolidation and eliminate underperforming products from the market.

</details>
