## ING Savings Maximiser — Best Overall Conditional Rate

ING's Savings Maximiser has been the benchmark conditional savings account in Australia for several years, and in 2026 it continues to lead the market with a conditional rate of approximately 5.50% p.a. — among the highest offered by any [APRA](https://www.apra.gov.au/)-regulated authorised deposit-taking institution for a fully liquid account with no fixed term. The base rate without meeting conditions sits at approximately 0.55% p.a., which underscores the importance of reliably meeting the three monthly requirements every calendar month.

The three-part monthly conditions are manageable for most working Australians: deposit at least A$1,000 from any source into the linked [ING Orange Everyday](https://www.ing.com.au/everyday-banking/orange-everyday.html) transaction account, make five settled card purchases using the Orange Everyday debit card, and ensure the Savings Maximiser balance grows during the calendar month (even by A$1 counts). The Orange Everyday account itself charges no monthly fee, offers unlimited ATM fee rebates at any ATM in Australia, and charges no foreign transaction fees on overseas purchases. For salary earners who make regular card payments for groceries, fuel, and daily expenses, the five-purchase condition is met without extra effort.

The primary limitation is the A$100,000 bonus rate cap. The 5.50% conditional rate applies only to the first A$100,000 in the savings account — any balance above that threshold earns only the base rate of 0.55%. For Australians with savings exceeding A$100,000, ING should be paired with a second savings account such as [UBank USave](/au/savings/ubank-review) to maximise returns across the full balance. On a A$100,000 balance, the Savings Maximiser earns approximately A$5,500 per year before tax, compared to just A$500 at a standard big four rate of 0.50% — a difference of A$5,000 annually for the same level of government-guaranteed deposit risk.

## UBank USave — Best for Large Balances and Simpler Conditions

UBank is a digital bank wholly owned by NAB (National Australia Bank) and offers one of the simplest conditional high-interest account structures in the Australian market. The USave account pays approximately 5.00% p.a. conditional with a single, straightforward condition: deposit at least A$200 per month into either the USave account itself or a linked [UBank USpend](https://www.ubank.com.au/) transaction account. There is no card purchase requirement, no balance growth rule, and no restriction on withdrawals — making it the easiest conditional bonus rate to maintain month after month.

The 5.00% conditional rate represents a modest step down from ING's 5.50%, but the simplicity and the A$250,000 bonus rate cap more than compensate for many savers. The A$250,000 cap is the highest on this list and aligns exactly with the [Financial Claims Scheme](https://www.apra.gov.au/financial-claims-scheme-for-adis) protection limit, meaning the full bonus rate applies up to the government guarantee ceiling. For Australians with savings balances between A$100,000 and A$250,000, UBank is the most cost-effective single-account option because ING's bonus rate cap cuts off at A$100,000. On a A$200,000 balance, UBank earns approximately A$10,000 per year before tax — compared to ING, which would earn A$5,500 on the first A$100,000 at 5.50% plus only A$550 on the remaining A$100,000 at the 0.55% base rate.

As an NAB subsidiary, UBank is an APRA-regulated ADI with full FCS protection, and deposits benefit from the same institutional stability as NAB itself. The digital-only model means no branches — all account management is via the UBank app and phone support. The base rate without meeting the A$200 monthly deposit condition drops to approximately 0.10% p.a., making the deposit habit essential.

In July 2025, Macquarie removed all conditions and fees from its Savings Account, delivering an ongoing unconditional rate of approximately 4.15% p.a. on any balance with no monthly requirements whatsoever.

New customers benefit from a four-month introductory rate of approximately 4.75% p.a. before reverting to the 4.15% ongoing rate. There is no balance cap on the bonus or ongoing rate — whether you deposit A$5,000 or A$500,000, the same rate applies. This makes Macquarie particularly attractive for large-balance savers who would exceed ING's A$100,000 cap and want to avoid managing multiple conditional accounts. For savers who value certainty and simplicity over rate maximisation, that gap may be a reasonable cost. Macquarie Bank is an APRA-authorised ADI with FCS protection, and the mobile app provides real-time balance tracking and instant transfers. For those with larger balances where no single conditional account covers the full amount, Macquarie's no-cap, no-conditions structure provides genuine value.

## How Savings Account Interest Works in Australia

Understanding how Australian savings accounts calculate and pay interest is essential for making informed comparisons. The details — daily calculation, monthly crediting, compounding effects, and tax treatment — determine whether you earn the headline rate or something meaningfully less.

Most Australian savings accounts calculate interest daily on the end-of-day account balance and credit that accrued interest monthly, typically on the last business day of the month. This daily calculation means every dollar earns interest from the day it is deposited until the day it is withdrawn, making it advantageous to transfer surplus cash into your savings account as early as possible each month rather than waiting until month-end. Monthly crediting creates a compounding effect: the interest credited in January earns its own interest in February and beyond. On a A$50,000 balance at 5.50% p.a. with monthly compounding, you earn approximately A$2,824 over twelve months — slightly more than the simple calculation of 5.50% multiplied by A$50,000 (A$2,750), due to the compounding benefit.

Interest earned on Australian savings accounts is assessable income, taxable at your marginal tax rate under [ATO guidelines](https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/investments-and-assets/interest). For a taxpayer on the 32.5% marginal rate, a gross interest rate of 5.50% delivers approximately 3.71% after tax. For a taxpayer on the 37% rate, the after-tax return falls to approximately 3.47%. Interest is reported by your bank to the Australian Taxation Office via your Tax File Number (TFN), and if you do not provide your TFN to the bank, they are required to withhold tax at the top marginal rate of 45% plus the Medicare levy. Always ensure your TFN is lodged with every financial institution where you hold a deposit account to avoid unnecessary withholding.

## APRA Protection — What the Financial Claims Scheme Covers

The safety of Australian savings accounts is underpinned by [APRA](https://www.apra.gov.au/) (the Australian Prudential Regulation Authority) supervision and the Australian Government [Financial Claims Scheme](https://www.apra.gov.au/financial-claims-scheme-for-adis) (FCS). Understanding FCS scope is essential for savers with balances approaching or exceeding A$250,000.

All banks, building societies, and credit unions that are APRA-authorised ADIs (Authorised Deposit-taking Institutions) are covered by the FCS. Under the scheme, up to A$250,000 per account holder per ADI is protected if an ADI fails. This protection is per institution, not per account — meaning if you hold three accounts at the same bank totalling A$400,000, only A$250,000 is protected. Joint accounts are treated separately, with each account holder entitled to their proportional share of the A$250,000 guarantee. The scheme is designed to pay out within seven business days of an institution's failure, providing rapid access to protected funds. [ASIC's MoneySmart](https://moneysmart.gov.au/banking/financial-claims-scheme) provides a detailed consumer guide to FCS eligibility.

Every institution recommended in this guide — ING, UBank, Macquarie, MOVE Bank, BOQ, ANZ, CommBank, and Rabobank — is an APRA-authorised ADI with full FCS coverage. There is no safety distinction between depositing money with a digital bank like UBank, a smaller institution like MOVE Bank, or a big four bank like ANZ. The government guarantee applies equally regardless of institutional size. For Australians with total savings exceeding A$250,000, the prudent strategy is to spread funds across multiple ADIs so that the full balance falls within the FCS guarantee at each institution. You can verify any institution's ADI status on the [APRA register of authorised ADIs](https://www.apra.gov.au/register-of-authorised-deposit-taking-institutions).

## Real-World Scenarios — Which Account Suits You

Choosing the right savings account depends on your balance size, income regularity, spending habits, and tolerance for conditions. The following three scenarios illustrate how different Australian savers should approach the decision based on 2026 rates. All figures use pre-tax interest and assume conditions are met every month.

### Scenario 1: Young Professional Building an Emergency Fund (A$15,000)

A 28-year-old Sydney renter earning A$75,000 per year with A$15,000 in savings for emergencies. Regular salary income and daily card spending make ING conditions easy to meet. At ING Savings Maximiser's 5.50% conditional rate, A$15,000 earns approximately A$825 per year before tax. At a standard big four savings rate of 0.50%, the same balance earns just A$75 per year — a difference of A$750 for the effort of meeting three simple monthly conditions. Alternatively, if eligible by age, BOQ Fast Track Starter at 5.00% on balances up to A$50,000 offers a competitive rate with simpler conditions for younger savers under 35.

### Scenario 2: Couple Saving for a House Deposit (A$120,000)

A dual-income couple in Melbourne with A$120,000 saved toward a property deposit. The ING Savings Maximiser bonus rate cap of A$100,000 means only the first A$100,000 earns 5.50%, while the remaining A$20,000 earns the base 0.55%. A smarter structure: hold A$100,000 in ING at 5.50% (earning A$5,500) and A$20,000 in UBank USave at 5.00% (earning A$1,000) for a combined annual return of A$6,500 before tax. A single Macquarie account at 4.15% unconditional would earn A$4,980 on A$120,000 — a difference of A$1,520 per year that justifies the effort of managing two conditional accounts. For couples considering the [First Home Super Saver Scheme](https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/super-for-individuals/withdraw-super/withdraw-super-to-buy-your-first-home), compare the after-tax return of saving inside super (taxed at 15%) against a high-interest savings account taxed at your marginal rate.

### Scenario 3: Retiree with Irregular Income (A$200,000)

A retired Australian in Brisbane with A$200,000 in savings, drawing irregular amounts for living expenses. Conditional accounts carry genuine risk because missed conditions in any month drop the rate to the base (often below 1%). Macquarie Savings Account at 4.15% unconditional earns approximately A$8,300 per year with zero conditions and no withdrawal restrictions. For a retiree, this certainty is worth the theoretical A$2,700 per year difference compared to ING's 5.50% rate — especially when any missed month at ING's 0.55% base rate would cost approximately A$206 in lost interest for that single month. Spreading across Macquarie (A$200,000) plus a small conditional account for discretionary savings provides a solid conservative structure for retirees who want both simplicity and some rate optimisation.

<Tip>
**House deposit savers:** If you are saving for a property purchase and considering the [First Home Super Saver Scheme](https://www.ato.gov.au/individuals-and-families/super-for-individuals/withdraw-super/withdraw-super-to-buy-your-first-home), compare the after-tax return of saving inside super (taxed at 15%) against a high-interest savings account (taxed at your marginal rate). For those on the 32.5% marginal rate, super may deliver higher after-tax returns — but the trade-off is reduced liquidity and a mandatory 12-month purchase window after withdrawal.
</Tip>
