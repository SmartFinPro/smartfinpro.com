## Betterment Features Deep-Dive

### Tax-Loss Harvesting: How Much It Actually Saves

Tax-loss harvesting is the primary reason most investors choose Betterment over simpler alternatives like target-date funds or a three-fund portfolio at Vanguard. The mechanism works by continuously scanning your taxable portfolio for individual ETF positions that have declined below their purchase price. When a loss is identified, Betterment sells the position and immediately purchases a similar — but not substantially identical — ETF to maintain your target asset allocation. The realized loss becomes a tax deduction: it offsets capital gains dollar-for-dollar and can deduct up to $3,000 per year against ordinary income, with unlimited carryforward of excess losses to future tax years.

According to Betterment's published data and our own testing observations, the average customer captures approximately $2,800 in annual tax savings through this feature. On a $100,000 portfolio, that $2,800 represents a 2.8% tax benefit — which means the tax-loss harvesting alone pays for Betterment's 0.25% management fee more than eleven times over. Even on smaller portfolios, the math is compelling: a $25,000 account paying $62.50/year in fees could still capture hundreds of dollars in tax-loss harvesting value during volatile market periods. During our 90-day testing period from November 2025 through January 2026, we observed 47 individual tax-loss harvesting events on our test portfolio, with the algorithm consistently identifying opportunities during daily market fluctuations.

The critical limitation to understand is that tax-loss harvesting only benefits taxable brokerage accounts. If your entire portfolio sits in Traditional IRAs, Roth IRAs, or 401(k) plans, you receive zero harvesting benefit because these accounts are already tax-advantaged. In that scenario, the 0.25% fee has no offsetting tax benefit, and a zero-fee alternative like [SoFi Invest](https://www.sofi.com/invest/) or Schwab Intelligent Portfolios becomes more competitive purely on cost.

### Direct Indexing at $100,000+

For accounts exceeding $100,000, Betterment offers direct indexing through its partnership with BlackRock. Instead of holding a single S&P 500 ETF, direct indexing purchases the individual stocks that make up the index, enabling tax-loss harvesting at the individual security level rather than the fund level. This dramatically increases the number of harvesting opportunities — if Apple declines while the overall S&P 500 rises, direct indexing can harvest the Apple loss while an ETF-based approach cannot. Betterment estimates direct indexing can generate an additional 1-2% in annual after-tax returns compared to ETF-based harvesting alone.

The $100,000 minimum puts direct indexing out of reach for many investors. Wealthfront offers the same feature at the same threshold, while some newer platforms have lowered the barrier. For investors between $50,000 and $100,000 who prioritize maximum tax efficiency, Wealthfront's daily harvesting frequency may capture more value from the ETF-level approach than Betterment's quarterly scanning does. If you are building a diversified portfolio that includes [trading platforms](/trading/) for individual stock positions alongside your robo-advisor, the direct indexing question becomes less relevant since you can manually harvest losses on your self-directed holdings.

<Warning>
**Wash Sale Rule:** If you hold the same securities in another brokerage account that Betterment is harvesting losses on, you risk triggering IRS wash sale violations that nullify the tax benefit. Betterment cannot see your external accounts. Either consolidate at Betterment or carefully coordinate holdings across brokers to avoid purchasing substantially identical securities within 30 days of a harvest event.
</Warning>

### Betterment Cash Reserve vs. High-Yield Savings Alternatives

Betterment Cash Reserve at 4.75% APY competes directly with standalone high-yield savings accounts from Marcus (Goldman Sachs), Ally Bank, and others. The key differentiator is FDIC coverage: Betterment spreads deposits across multiple FDIC-member partner banks, extending coverage up to $2 million per depositor — eight times the standard $250,000 single-bank limit. For investors holding large cash reserves for emergency funds, home down payments, or business reserves, this expanded FDIC coverage is a meaningful safety advantage.

However, Betterment Cash Reserve trails Wealthfront's cash account at 5.00% APY. On a $50,000 cash balance, that 0.25% difference equals $125 per year — modest but not negligible if you maintain large cash positions. The integrated nature of Betterment's ecosystem (cash reserve, checking, and investment accounts under one dashboard) provides convenience that standalone savings accounts cannot match, but pure yield-seekers will find slightly better rates elsewhere.

## How Betterment Makes Money

Understanding Betterment's revenue model helps you anticipate where costs may increase and where the platform's incentives align — or conflict — with yours as a customer. Transparency about revenue sources is an important trust signal, particularly for a fiduciary that is legally required to prioritize your interests.

Betterment generates revenue through **five primary channels**:

1. **Asset-based management fees** — The 0.25% Digital and 0.40% Premium annual fees on invested assets are Betterment's primary revenue source. A customer with $100,000 invested generates $250-400/year for Betterment. This model aligns incentives — Betterment earns more when your portfolio grows, creating a financial interest in your investment success.

2. **Cash Reserve net interest margin** — Betterment pays 4.75% APY on Cash Reserve deposits but earns a higher rate from the partner banks holding those funds. The spread between what Betterment earns and what it pays you generates significant revenue as cash balances have grown across the customer base.

3. **Checking account interchange** — Every time you use your Betterment Visa debit card, Betterment earns interchange revenue (typically 0.5-1.0% of the transaction) from the Visa network. This revenue subsidizes the fee-free checking account and ATM reimbursement program.

4. **401(k) plan administration** — Betterment at Work provides employer-sponsored retirement plan administration, charging plan-level fees to businesses that offer Betterment as their 401(k) provider. This B2B revenue stream diversifies beyond individual consumer accounts.

5. **Securities lending** — Betterment may lend securities held in customer accounts to institutional borrowers (such as hedge funds executing short sales) and earns a portion of the lending fee. This is standard practice among custodians and does not affect your portfolio holdings or returns.

This revenue model means Betterment is incentivized to grow your portfolio (more AUM = more fees) and to keep you on the platform long-term. Unlike commission-based advisors who earn from trading activity, Betterment has no incentive to churn your portfolio. The fiduciary duty reinforces this alignment.
