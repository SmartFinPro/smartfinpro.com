### How Credit Scores Work

Your credit score is a three-digit number (300-850) that lenders use to decide:
- Whether to approve your application
- What interest rate to offer
- How much credit to extend

### The FICO Score Components

| Factor | Weight | Impact |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Payment history | 35% | Biggest factor—on-time matters most |
| Credit utilization | 30% | How much of available credit you're using |
| Length of credit history | 15% | Age of oldest account and average age |
| Credit mix | 10% | Variety (credit cards, loans, mortgage) |
| New credit inquiries | 10% | Recent applications for new credit |

**Key insight:** Payment history (35%) + utilization (30%) = 65% of your score. Fix these two first.

### Credit Score Ranges & What They Mean

| Score | Rating | Meaning |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 750-850 | Excellent | Best rates on all products |
| 700-749 | Good | Approval likely, good rates |
| 650-699 | Fair | Approval possible, higher rates |
| 600-649 | Poor | Limited approval, expensive rates |
| 300-599 | Very Poor | Rejection likely, special programs needed |

**Goal:** Most people should target 700+, which qualifies for standard lending rates.

## 30-Day Quick Win Plan: +30-50 Points

The first 30 days are about LOW-HANGING FRUIT that require no application process.

### Day 1: Get Your Baseline

**Actions:**
1. Pull all three credit reports: annualcreditreport.com
2. Check your current FICO score: credit.com or creditkarma.com
3. Note today's date and score (your baseline)

**Time:** 20 minutes

**Expected impact:** 0 points (just measurement)

### Days 2-5: Credit Utilization Adjustment

This is the fastest score boost available.

**Actions:**
1. List all credit cards with balances
2. Calculate your utilization: (Total balance) ÷ (Total limit) × 100
3. If above 30%, make payments to bring cards to 10-30% range

**Example:**
- Card 1: $3,000 balance, $5,000 limit = 60% utilization (TOO HIGH)
- Card 2: $800 balance, $2,000 limit = 40% utilization (HIGH)
- **Current total utilization: 54%**

**Action:**
- Pay Card 1 down to $500 (10% utilization)
- Pay Card 2 down to $200 (10% utilization)
- **New total utilization: 14%**

**Timeline for impact:** 30-45 days (next billing cycle)

**Expected score improvement:** 20-40 points

<Tip>
Don't pay cards to $0 (zero utilization looks odd, like unused credit). Target 1-10% utilization instead—signals you use credit responsibly but don't max it out.
</Tip>

### Days 6-15: Authorized User Strategy (IF Available)

**If you have a trusted friend or family member with excellent credit:**

Ask them to add you as an authorized user on their credit card with:
- Long history (20+ years old ideal)
- Zero missed payments (perfect payment history)
- Low utilization (under 10%)

**What happens:**
- Their entire credit history gets added to YOUR report
- Immediate history boost (often +30-50 points in 30-45 days)
- Their payment history becomes your payment history
- Lender requires no action from you (you don't even need to use the card)

**Time to impact:** 30-45 days (when the bureau updates)

**Expected score improvement:** 30-50 points (instant for old, clean account)

<Warning>
Only do this with someone you trust completely. If they miss a payment later, YOUR score drops too. Authorized user relationship is a two-way street for credit purposes.
</Warning>

**How to verify authorized user worked:**
- Check credit reports 45 days after being added
- You should see their account on your report
- Their history (in years) now shows on YOUR profile

### Days 16-30: Address Collections & Errors

**Actions:**
1. Review credit reports for collections or negative items
2. If you see errors (wrong account, wrong amount, outdated info):
   - File dispute directly with bureau (free, online)
   - Include evidence contradicting the claim
3. If legitimate collection but unpaid:
   - Wait for Day 60-90 plan to address

**Timeline for impact:** 30-45 days (bureaus investigate disputes)

**Expected score improvement:** 5-30 points (if errors are found and removed)

### End of Day 30 Check

**Score expected:** +30-50 points from Day 1

- Best case (all tactics): +45-50 points
- Average case (utilization + authorized user): +40 points
- Minimum case (utilization only): +25 points

## Authorized User Fast Hack (Detailed Strategy)

### What is an Authorized User?

When someone makes you an "authorized user" on their credit card:
- Their account appears on YOUR credit report
- Their payment history becomes YOUR history
- You get instant credibility boost

### Timeline: 30-45 Days to Score Impact

| Day | What Happens |
|-----|-------------|
| Day 1 | Relative adds you as authorized user |
| Days 1-15 | Credit card company processes and reports to bureaus |
| Days 15-30 | Account appears on your credit report |
| Days 30-45 | FICO score recalculates and improves |
| **Day 45** | **New score visible (typically +30-50 points)** |

### How Much Can Authorized User Boost Your Score?

Depends on the account being added to you:

| Account Quality | Age | Utilization | Boost Expected |
|-----------------|-----|-------------|-----------------|
| Excellent (no lates) | 20+ years | Under 10% | **+45-50 points** |
| Good (rare late) | 10-20 years | 10-30% | **+35-40 points** |
| Fair (occasional late) | 5-10 years | 30-50% | **+20-30 points** |
| Poor (recent lates) | Under 5 years | 50%+ | **+5-15 points** |

### The Ask: How to Approach Someone

**Script:**
"I'm working on improving my credit score, and I know adding me as an authorized user on your credit account could help me. I won't actually use the card, but having access to your payment history would give my credit a boost. Would you be comfortable adding me?"

**Response if they say yes:**
- Confirm you won't use the card
- Explain it doesn't affect their credit (true)
- Thank them profusely
- Ask when it should appear on your report

**Response if they say no:**
- Thank them anyway
- Respect their decision
- Use other tactics (secured card, etc.)

### Important Cautions

**Don't:**
- Pressure someone to add you
- Forget to mention you won't use the card
- Expect them to say yes (many people are protective of credit accounts)
- Use the card if they DO add you (unless explicitly allowed)

**Do:**
- Only use this with people you deeply trust
- Inform them immediately if you can't make payments (so they're prepared)
- Return the favor by adding them if you ever have pristine credit

## Perfect Payment History: Your Secret Weapon

### Why Payment History Matters Most

Payment history = 35% of your score (the single biggest factor)

**One on-time payment = +0.5 to +2 points**
**One missed payment = -30 to -100 points**

The math is heavily weighted toward missing payments being bad. But the flip side: 12+ months of on-time payments is POWERFUL.

### Setting Up Perfect Payment History

**Step 1: Identify all bills**
- Credit cards
- Student loans
- Car loans
- Mortgage
- Medical payments
- Insurance
- Utilities

**Step 2: Automate everything**
- Bills with auto-pay available: enable it
- Set for 5 days before due date (safety margin)
- Verify it's active (check account)

**Step 3: For bills without autopay**
- Set phone calendar reminders (7 days before due)
- Create an email reminder
- Use bill management app (Prism, Truebill)

**Step 4: Monitor**
- Check email confirmations of payments
- Review statements monthly
- Verify deductions from checking account

### The Power of Compound Payment History

| Months On-Time | Score Impact | Credibility |
|---|---|---|
| 1 month | +2-5 points | Minimal |
| 3 months | +10-15 points | Building |
| 6 months | +25-35 points | Significant |
| 12 months | +50-75 points | Strong |
| 24 months | +100-150 points | Excellent |

**Key insight:** The longer your streak, the more powerful it becomes. One on-time payment is worth 2 points. But 12 in a row (compounding) is worth 50+ points.

### What Counts as a "Late" Payment?

| Days Late | Impact | Status |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| 1-29 days | Minor impact (5-10 points) | "Late" |
| 30 days | Reported to credit bureaus | "30-day late" |
| 60 days | Significant impact (20-30 points) | "60-day late" |
| 90+ days | Major impact (50+ points) | "90+ day late" / "Delinquent" |

**Important:** Even 1 day late can trigger a fee, but isn't reported to bureaus until 30 days. Lenders sometimes give grace periods.

### If You Miss a Payment

**Immediately:**
1. Make the missed payment ASAP
2. Call lender and explain the circumstance
3. Ask if they'll remove the late fee (sometimes they will)

**Following weeks:**
- Verify payment posted
- Confirm no collections action started
- Resume normal payment schedule

**Score recovery:**
- Late payment damage fades over time
- After 7 years, it falls off completely
- Adding positive history (on-time payments) helps counteract it

<Tip>
If you miss a payment by accident, contact the lender immediately. Many will remove the late fee and not report if you call within 24-48 hours. Lenders want on-time payments—sometimes they'll work with you.
</Tip>

## Credit Utilization Quick Fixes

### Understanding Utilization

**Formula:** Current balance ÷ Credit limit × 100 = Utilization percentage

**Example:**
- Balance: $2,500
- Limit: $5,000
- Utilization: $2,500 ÷ $5,000 × 100 = **50% utilization**

### Why Utilization Matters

Utilization is 30% of your score. It signals whether you're overleveraged or managing credit responsibly.

**Ideal range:** 1-10%
**Good range:** 10-30%
**Acceptable:** 30-50%
**Problematic:** 50%+
**Very bad:** Over 100% (balance exceeds limit)

### Quick Fixes (Immediate Impact in 30-45 Days)

**Fix #1: Pay Down Largest Cards First**

If you have 3 cards:
- Card A: $1,000 balance, $2,000 limit = 50% (HIGH)
- Card B: $300 balance, $1,000 limit = 30% (OK)
- Card C: $200 balance, $500 limit = 40% (HIGH)

**Action:** Pay $500 toward Card A first
- Card A: $500 balance, $2,000 limit = 25% (BETTER)
- New combined utilization: 35% (down from 40%)

**Fix #2: Request Credit Limit Increase**

Call your card issuer and ask for a limit increase:
- "Could you increase my credit limit from $2,000 to $3,000?"
- Many will approve instantly (soft pull, no credit impact)
- Same $500 balance on $3,000 limit = 17% (much better)

**Does NOT trigger hard inquiry** (no credit score impact from soft pull)

**Success rate:** 60-70% get approved, especially with good payment history

**Fix #3: Open New Card (Strategic)**

**Risk:** Hard inquiry (-5 to 10 points) + new account (reduces average age)
**Benefit:** New credit limit spreads utilization

**Example:**
- Current: $2,000 balance, $5,000 total limit = 40%
- After new card with $2,500 limit: $2,000 balance, $7,500 total limit = 27%

**Timeline:** Only do this if you can absorb the -5 to 10 point hit from inquiry. You'll gain it back in 2-3 months.

### The Reporting Cycle

Credit cards report utilization on your statement closing date (not the due date).

**Timeline:**
- Statement closes Day 30
- Card reports balance to bureaus Day 31-35
- Bureau updates score Day 35-45

**Strategy:** Pay BEFORE statement closes (not just on due date)
- If you normally pay on due date (Day 25), pay on Day 15 instead
- Lower balance reported to bureaus = lower utilization reported = faster score improvement

## Critical Mistakes That Destroy Your Progress

### Mistake #1: Paying Cards to $0

**What people do:** Assume paying off $5,000 card completely is best

**What actually happens:**
- Zero balance shows as "no activity"
- Slightly lower score boost than 10% balance
- Doesn't leverage credit activity

**Better approach:** Pay to $50-200 range (1-10%), THEN pay off fully after 3-6 months of perfect payment history.

### Mistake #2: Closing Old Cards After Paying Off

**What people do:** "I'm paying off my credit card, so I'll close it to avoid temptation"

**What actually happens:**
- Average account age drops (15% of score)
- Credit mix decreases
- Available credit drops (utilization spikes)
- Score drops 20-50 points

**Better approach:** Keep the card open, freeze it, but don't close it. Set one small recurring charge (Netflix) and autopay each month.

### Mistake #3: Applying for Multiple Cards Simultaneously

**What people do:** "I'll apply for 3 cards at once to get new accounts quickly"

**What actually happens:**
- 3 hard inquiries = -15 to 30 point drop
- Lenders see multiple recent inquiries = looks desperate
- Likely to get denied on #2 and #3

**Better approach:** Space applications 3-6 months apart. One new card at a time.

### Mistake #4: Checking Your Score Too Frequently

**What people do:** Check score daily on various websites, get discouraged

**What actually happens:**
- Score doesn't change daily (reports update monthly)
- Checking kills motivation (you'll see no movement for 30 days)
- Multiple checks look suspicious to some algorithms

**Better approach:** Check quarterly (every 90 days). That's when meaningful movement happens.

### Mistake #5: Paying Off Collections Without Verification

**What people do:** "I found a collection account, I'll pay it off to fix my score"

**What actually happens:**
- Paying collection updates the account (activity is reported)
- Score may DROP 10-20 points initially (on old paid collection)
- Then improve 30-40 points over 3-6 months

**Better approach:**
1. First, verify the collection is accurate (could be old or wrong)
2. Dispute inaccurate collections (don't pay them)
3. Only pay if: you confirm it's yours, or you can get "pay for delete" agreement

### Mistake #6: Applying for Lots of New Credit

**What people do:** "I'll get a new car loan, personal loan, and credit card to improve credit mix"

**What actually happens:**
- 3 hard inquiries = -15 to 30 points
- 3 new accounts = lower average age = -20 points
- Total damage: -35 to 50 points
- Recovery: 12 months

**Better approach:** Get 1 new account, wait 6 months of on-time history, then add second.

### Mistake #7: Ignoring Billing Statement Dates

**What people do:** Pay bills whenever, not considering when they report to bureaus

**What actually happens:**
- Higher balance gets reported (you paid after statement closed)
- Utilization reported as higher than necessary
- Score doesn't improve as fast

**Better approach:** Pay BEFORE statement closing date. Check your statement date and pay 10-15 days before.

<Warning>
The #1 mistake people make: They assume credit fixing is like dieting—if you do it right for 30 days, you're done. Wrong. Credit requires ongoing maintenance (on-time payments, low utilization) forever. It's a habit, not a sprint.
</Warning>

## Tracking Your Progress

### Month 1: Baseline Documentation

**Document these metrics:**
- Actual FICO score (from credit.com or creditkarma.com)
- All credit card balances and limits
- All accounts on credit report
- Recent inquiries count

**Save:**
- Screenshot of score
- Spreadsheet with all accounts
- Photo of credit reports

### Month 2-3: Measurement & Adjustment

**Check monthly:**
- Credit utilization on each card
- Any new accounts reporting
- Payment status on all bills
- New inquiries

**Adjust if needed:**
- If utilization not decreasing, pay more aggressively
- If authorized user not appearing, follow up with family member
- If secured card not approved, try different issuer

### Month 4 (90-day checkpoint): Full Assessment

**Measure final:**
- New FICO score (compare to baseline)
- Credit utilization percentage (target: under 10% across all cards)
- Recent inquiries (should be aging off)
- Payment history (look for perfect record)

**Assess:**
- Did you hit your 90-day goal?

- What's your next 90-day target?
