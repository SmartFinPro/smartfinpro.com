## Tax Implications of Debt Settlement

### How Forgiven Debt Becomes Taxable Income

When you settle $20,000 debt for $10,000, the creditor forgives $10,000. IRS treats this as income.

**Example:**
- Credit card balance: $15,000
- Settlement: $6,000 (60% reduction)
- Forgiven amount: $9,000
- Creditor sends IRS Form 1099-C for $9,000
- You report $9,000 as income on tax return
- Tax liability: $9,000 × 22% (your tax bracket) = **$1,980 owed to IRS**

### Insolvency Exception (How to Avoid Taxes)

If your total liabilities exceed your total assets, you're "insolvent" and may not owe taxes on forgiven debt.

**Example:**
- Assets: $25,000 (car, savings, possessions)
- Liabilities: $80,000 (credit cards, personal loans, medical debt)
- Insolvent by: $55,000
- Forgiven debt: $15,000
- Because you're insolvent by $55,000, the $15,000 forgiven debt is NOT taxable

**IRS Form 982:** File this form to claim insolvency exception.

### Tax Planning Strategy

1. **Before enrolling:** Calculate your insolvency status (liabilities - assets)
2. **During program:** Keep records of all settlements and forgiven amounts
3. **At tax time:** Work with tax professional to file Form 982 if insolvent
4. **Budget for taxes:** If not insolvent, budget $0.20-0.35 per $1 of forgiven debt for taxes

<Warning>
National Debt Relief provides basic tax guidance, but consult a CPA or tax attorney before enrolling. Tax liability on $20,000-50,000 forgiven debt can be $5,000-15,000—a surprise bill many customers don't anticipate.
</Warning>
