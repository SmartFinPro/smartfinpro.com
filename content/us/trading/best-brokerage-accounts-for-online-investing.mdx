Today, every major broker offers $0 commissions on US stocks and ETFs, which means the factors that actually differentiate brokerages have shifted entirely. Competition now centers on cash yield for uninvested funds, index fund expense ratios, margin rates, options commissions, research quality, and international market access. For investors selecting a brokerage in 2026, these secondary factors can represent thousands of dollars in annual cost differences that compound dramatically over a 10-to-30-year investing horizon.

The default uninvested cash sweep deposits into a bank account paying just 0.45% APY. The thinkorswim platform remains one of the best for active traders, featuring 400+ technical indicators, customizable charting, and paper trading capabilities that surpass even IBKR in raw usability for options strategy visualization.

## Vanguard and Robinhood

**Vanguard** pioneered the index fund philosophy and maintains a genuinely unique ownership structure: Vanguard funds are owned by their shareholders, which eliminates the profit-seeking incentives that drive other asset managers to maximize fees. This philosophical commitment has kept Vanguard fund expense ratios among the lowest in the industry at 0.03–0.06% for core index funds. Cash sweeps into federal money market funds earning approximately 4.5% APY. Margin rates range from 7.25% to 8.50%, significantly below Schwab and Fidelity. The primary criticisms are a dated digital interface, options commissions of $1.00/contract (versus $0.65 at most competitors), and customer service quality that has drawn inconsistent reviews in recent years. For pure long-term index investors who prioritize philosophy and low costs over platform sophistication, Vanguard remains an excellent choice — but Fidelity has matched or exceeded Vanguard in every measurable dimension while adding better technology and service infrastructure.

**[Robinhood](/trading/robinhood-review)** offers the simplest possible entry point for investors who have never opened a brokerage account. The mobile-first interface is genuinely intuitive, $0 commissions extend to options ($0/contract — unique among major brokers), and the $0 account minimum means any amount is sufficient to start investing. Robinhood Gold ($5/month or $50/year) provides 5.00% APY on uninvested cash and a 1% IRA contribution match that adds up meaningfully over years. The Robinhood Legend desktop platform, launched in late 2024, has added charting and technical analysis tools that address the platform's historical weakness in serving more active users. Limitations remain meaningful: no mutual fund access, minimal research tools compared to Fidelity or Schwab, chat-only customer service with no phone support, and lingering trust concerns from the [2021 FINRA $70M settlement](https://www.finra.org/media-center/newsreleases/2021/finra-orders-record-financial-penalties-against-robinhood-financial) related to GameStop/AMC trading restrictions that remain fresh in many investors' memories.

<Warning>

**Robinhood Trust and Regulatory History:** In June 2021, FINRA fined Robinhood Financial $70 million — the largest fine in FINRA history at the time — for widespread and significant harm to customers, including platform outages during critical trading periods and the controversial trading halts on GameStop and AMC shares in January 2021. While Robinhood has improved its infrastructure and added new features since the settlement, investors should be aware of this regulatory history and the platform's ongoing limitations in customer service and research capabilities before committing significant assets.

</Warning>

## Costs and Fees

While $0 stock and ETF commissions have become universal, the actual cost of using a brokerage extends well beyond trade execution. Three categories drive the most significant cost differences in 2026: uninvested cash yield, fund expense ratios, and margin interest rates. Understanding these secondary costs is essential because they compound silently over years and can dwarf any historical commission savings that first attracted investors to a particular platform.

The cash yield differential is the single most under-discussed factor in brokerage selection. Consider $100,000 in idle cash across three brokers: Fidelity's default 4.97% APY (SPAXX) earns $4,970/year. Schwab's default 0.45% bank sweep earns $450/year. E*TRADE's lowest tier at 0.01% earns just $10/year. The difference between the best and worst default rates on $100,000 is $4,960 annually — real money that requires zero trading skill to capture. Investors should verify whether their broker requires manual opt-in for competitive money market rates, as several platforms bury this option in account settings.

| Broker | $100K Cash (Default APY) | Annual Yield | Difference vs. Fidelity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fidelity (SPAXX) | 4.97% | $4,970 | — |
| Robinhood Gold | 5.00% | $5,000 | +$30 |
| Vanguard (Federal MM) | ~4.50% | $4,500 | -$470 |
| Schwab (Bank Sweep) | 0.45% | $450 | -$4,520 |
| E*TRADE (Lowest Tier) | 0.01% | $10 | -$4,960 |

<Tip>

If you already have a Schwab account, check whether your uninvested cash is in the default bank sweep (0.45%) or a money market fund (4.85%+). Log into Schwab.com, navigate to your cash position, and look for the "Change Sweep" option. Switching to the Schwab Value Advantage Money Fund takes five minutes and could earn you $4,000+ more per year on $100,000 in cash. </Tip>

Fund expense ratios represent the second hidden cost layer across all brokerage accounts. Fidelity's ZERO funds at 0% expense ratio save investors $150/year per $500,000 compared to Vanguard's 0.03% equivalent funds — and $300/year compared to the 0.06% tier. These differences appear small in isolation but compound over decades: $150/year at 8% annual growth becomes $18,000+ over 30 years of sustained returns. Investors should verify the exact expense ratio of every fund in their portfolio at least annually, as even minor differences accumulate meaningfully at institutional scale and the gap between the cheapest and most expensive broad-market funds has widened in recent years as index fund competition intensified.

For options traders, commission structures vary more than most investors realize before they commit to a platform. The industry standard is $0.65/contract at Fidelity, Schwab, IBKR, and E*TRADE, but [Robinhood](/trading/robinhood-review) charges $0/contract and [tastytrade](/trading/tastytrade-review) caps total commissions at $10/leg regardless of contract count — a significant advantage for large multi-leg strategies involving 20+ contracts per side. Vanguard charges $1.00/contract, making it the most expensive options platform among the seven major brokers evaluated here. High-frequency options traders running complex spreads should calculate their expected annual commission load across platforms before selecting a broker, since the difference between $0/contract and $0.65/contract scales dramatically with volume.

<Warning>

**SIPC Insurance Does Not Cover Market Losses:** SIPC (Securities Investor Protection Corporation) protects brokerage accounts up to $500,000 per customer ($250,000 cash sub-limit) in the event a broker becomes insolvent or fails. It does **not** protect against losses from declining security values, market volatility, or poor investment decisions. Additionally, money market funds — unlike bank savings accounts — are not FDIC insured; they carry a small but non-zero risk of "breaking the buck" (net asset value falling below $1.00), as occurred briefly during the 2008 financial crisis. Investors should understand this distinction when parking large cash balances in brokerage money market funds versus FDIC-insured bank accounts.

</Warning>

## How to Choose

Selecting a brokerage should be driven by your specific investor profile rather than brand recognition or marketing spend. The right broker for a 25-year-old opening their first Roth IRA is fundamentally different from the right broker for an active trader using margin across international markets. Four factors should guide your decision: your primary use case, your cash position, your margin needs, and your comfort with platform complexity. Getting this right from the start saves the administrative burden of account transfers, tax lot re-establishment, and dividend reinvestment reconfiguration later.

For most investors opening their first account in 2026, Fidelity is the default recommendation without serious competition. Zero-expense-ratio funds, a competitive default cash yield, excellent research, and no account minimums create a platform that scales from a $500 starter portfolio to a $5M retirement account without ever requiring a transfer. If you prioritize the absolute simplest user experience and trade primarily from a mobile device, [Robinhood](/trading/robinhood-review) is the fastest path from download to first trade — typically under 10 minutes with most document uploads processing in real time. For investors who need international market access, professional-grade execution quality, or the lowest available margin rates, [Interactive Brokers Pro](/trading/interactive-brokers-review) is the only serious option in US retail brokerage with no close competitors on these dimensions.
